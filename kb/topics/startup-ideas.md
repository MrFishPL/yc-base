# Startup Ideas — Compiled Truth

YC's collective advice is remarkably consistent: start from a real, personally-felt problem rather than a "cool" solution, pick a problem you have unfair insight into (founder-market fit beats market-sizing), and find out fast whether it's real by shipping a scrappy MVP to hand-picked users rather than reasoning about it in the abstract or pitching it to investors and friends. Good ideas frequently look small, boring, already-crowded, or even bad at first glance — that's often *why* smart people haven't taken them yet — while ideas that sound too clean or get suspicious unanimous praise ("tarpit ideas") are red flags. Most founders don't land on their eventual big idea first; expect to pivot, and pivot fast, using each round of real customer contact (not more brainstorming) to find the next one. New platform shifts, especially AI, keep reopening idea spaces that were previously "tarpit" or already-tried, so studying prior failed attempts and asking "why does this work now" is as important as the idea itself.
