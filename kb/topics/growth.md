# Growth — Compiled Truth

YC's collective advice is that "growth" is not a bag of hacks — it's what happens after you've earned product-market fit through real, flattening retention curves. Before that point, spend on paid acquisition or PR is close to wasted; the job is to do unscalable things by hand to find and delight your first users. Once retention holds, growth splits cleanly into conversion-rate optimization (funnel, onboarding, activation) and channel work, and nearly every company that reaches real scale ends up dominated by one or two channels chosen to match how customers naturally discover the product — rather than a scattershot of many. Paid acquisition is a late, disciplined tool (CAC must be paid back fast against real revenue), while the strongest consumer companies get the large majority of growth organically through word of mouth, referrals, network effects, and SEO. Throughout, data and experimentation beat founder intuition, and for first customers specifically, warm, personal, high-touch outreach reliably outperforms broad or cold tactics.

**State:** #talks: 95 · maturity: strong
**Open threads:** Should virality/referral loops be deliberately engineered into the product from day one (Airbnb's dozens of tuned referral-email experiments, "engineer network effects into the product"), or does durable growth emerge naturally from a product people love with zero engineered mechanics (Cursor, Monzo, ClassDojo, Bump all scaled to millions on pure word of mouth with no viral mechanics or paid spend)? Relatedly, how central should paid acquisition ever be: treated as "cheating growth" to be minimized even after PMF, versus a legitimate accelerant for seeding new markets or balancing a marketplace once revenue exists.
