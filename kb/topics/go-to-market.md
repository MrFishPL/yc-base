# Go-To-Market — Compiled Truth

YC's collective view is that go-to-market is not a separate discipline from product — it starts with a landing page or demo that instantly answers "what is this, is it for me, does it work, is it credible," and it is executed personally by founders (not hired reps) until a repeatable playbook exists. There is no single winning motion: top-down and bottoms-up split roughly evenly among successful YC B2B companies, and the right choice depends on whether the product resonates with individual users or executives; in enterprise specifically, the winning pattern is to start deliberately narrow with a wedge that slips between incumbents' gaps and to find asymmetries (platform-agnosticism, alternate monetization) that incumbents structurally can't copy. Whichever motion is chosen, price should be treated as a live, value-based experiment rather than a fixed or cost-plus number, pilots should carry a real financial commitment, and the founder's job continues through implementation and onboarding, not just the signature. Even outside software, the same discipline applies — biotech founders use a YC batch not to make scientific breakthroughs but to nail an MVP and a go-to-market/regulatory strategy. In the AI era this playbook is intensifying and mutating: forward-deployed-engineer models sell delivered outcomes instead of seats, AI-native service businesses are pricing per-unit or on outcomes while obsessing over COGS and output variance, and bottoms-up product-led growth is resurging as non-engineers become internal champions for AI-native tools — but founder-led selling, high signal-to-noise landing pages, and disciplined pricing experiments remain the constants underneath all of it.
