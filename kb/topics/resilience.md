# Resilience — Compiled Truth

YC's collective advice treats resilience not as a personality trait but as a practice: setbacks, rejection, and near-death moments are the normal texture of building a company, not exceptions, so the real skill is controlling your reaction, staying financially "default alive" so failure isn't fatal, being radically honest with your team and yourself when things break, and having a personal story or mission strong enough to keep going through years of grinding before it looks like "overnight success." Founders are repeatedly told to separate self-worth from company performance, to treat pivots and even shutdowns as legitimate outcomes rather than moral failures, and to build the boring infrastructure of resilience — sleep, honest co-founder relationships, cash discipline, therapy — well before a crisis forces the issue.

**State:** #talks: 162 · maturity: strong
**Open threads:** Persist-through-the-trough vs. cut-losses-and-pivot-fast — some speakers (Anne Wojcicki, Casetext's founders) credit years of stubborn persistence through a "trough of sorrow" for their eventual success, while others (Dalton Caldwell, Windsurf's Varun Mohan) warn that loss aversion and pride keep founders clinging to failing ideas long after the data says to pivot, and treat fast pivots as a "badge of honor." Relatedly, is stopping a failure? Avni Patel Thompson explicitly rejects the "pivot or die" narrative and argues a startup that did what it set out to do and shut down cleanly is not a failure, in tension with the more common framing (Geoff Ralston, Michael Seibel) that quitting is what kills companies and the goal is always to survive.
