# Do Things That Don't Scale — Compiled Truth

Across dozens of YC talks, "doing things that don't scale" means deliberately serving your earliest customers by hand — manually, personally, and in ways you know you can't sustain once the company is big — because the direct contact teaches founders more than any amount of theorizing, and because a handmade beginning is usually the only way to get an unscalable-looking idea off the ground at all. The pattern repeats across consumer (Airbnb, DoorDash, Instacart, Zepto), enterprise/dev-tools (Stripe, Algolia, PlanGrid), and hard tech (Solugen, Astranis) alike: found the first customers by hand, build the crudest possible version, watch what breaks, and only automate once the manual version's ROI starts to decline. The advice is not an excuse to skip building a real business — several speakers warn it can curdle into disguised, unscalable consulting if founders never graduate off the manual crutch.

**State:** #talks: 65 · maturity: strong
**Open threads:** Most speakers frame unscalable work as a *temporary* bridge to be abandoned once automation pays off (Gustaf Alstromer, Optimizely's AB-testing consulting cautionary tale) — but Bob McGrew's "forward deployed engineer" playbook argues some AI/enterprise businesses should keep doing unscalable, per-customer customization permanently and instead scale *contract size*, not customer count — "doing things that don't scale, at scale."
