# Default Alive — Compiled Truth

YC's collective advice treats "default alive" (will your current growth rate reach profitability before your bank account hits zero, with no more investor money assumed) as the single most important survival metric a founder can track, and recommends checking it weekly or monthly using nothing more than bank balance, money in, and money out. Being default alive is less about being profitable today than about having the margin for error to survive a failed fundraise, since rounds get structurally harder to close as a company matures and most seed companies never raise a Series A. The practical discipline that follows is near-universal: spend as if the money you have is the last you'll ever raise, don't hire ahead of product-market fit, don't let money buy anything but time before PMF, and treat a growing headcount or fundraise size as a vanity metric rather than progress. When a company drifts toward default-dead, the fix is blunt — cut headcount (the biggest lever), cut ad spend, and raise prices — and doing that math six months early beats doing it as an emergency tourniquet. The main tension in the corpus is between this "stay lean, assume no more capital" doctrine and a counter-current that says raising a lot of money is fine, even good, as long as founders don't lose their frugal instincts once it's in the bank.
