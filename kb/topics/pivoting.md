# Pivoting — Compiled Truth

YC's collective advice treats a pivot as simply changing your idea (customer or problem, not just the solution) — something early-stage founders should be doing lightly and often, because the opportunity cost of persisting on something that isn't working outweighs the sunk cost of abandoning it. Founders systematically pivot too slowly, not too quickly, held back by loss aversion, users' polite feedback masquerading as traction, and fear of admitting defeat; the clearest leading indicators to move on are a missing "north star" metric, obsession that has quietly evaporated, or simply no longer believing the current path will work. The best pivots lean into founders' pre-existing expertise or an insight surfaced by directly serving customers (Brex, Firecrawl, Segment, Scale AI, GoCardless), can happen in days or even a single weekend once conviction shifts (Windsurf, Rezi), and should be followed by real commitment — narrowing to one idea, one KPI, and "burning the other boats" — rather than repeating the cycle every week. A large fraction of the corpus's biggest outcomes (Brex, Segment, Figma, Scale AI, Gusto, PostHog, GoCardless, Nourish, GovDash, Zepto) trace back to one or more pivots, making it a normal and often decisive part of the YC path rather than a mark of failure.

**State:** #talks: 114 · maturity: strong
**Open threads:**
- How many pivots is healthy: Dalton Caldwell warns that pivoting repeatedly causes "founder whiplash" that is more deadly than persisting on a mediocre-but-workable idea, yet the corpus is full of counterexamples where serial pivoting (Figma through drones/WebGL/memes/photo-editing, GovDash across five pivots, Nourish five times in one batch, PostHog through several failed products) preceded a billion-dollar outcome.
- Whether to keep pivoting or give up outright: Parker Conrad argues the "never give up, keep pivoting" narrative is often wrong and founders should abandon a non-working idea entirely rather than assume they'll eventually pivot into success like Airbnb; Dalton Caldwell and the Windsurf CEO instead advise taking several fast, lightweight "shots on goal" and treating a pivot as a badge of honor, not a defeat.
- Whether not pivoting can still be a success: Avni Patel Thompson explicitly rejects the "pivot or die" narrative, arguing a startup that did what it set out to do and generated real learning is not a failure even if it shuts down instead of pivoting.
