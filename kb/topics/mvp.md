# MVP (Minimum Viable Product) — Compiled Truth

YC's collective advice is remarkably consistent: an MVP is not a product, it's a question — the fastest, cheapest thing you can put in front of a real user to learn whether you can deliver any value at all. Founders should hold the problem and the customer tightly but the solution loosely, ship in days or weeks rather than months, seek out the most desperate early users rather than the most impressive ones, and treat "launch" (getting one real customer) as separate from and far more important than "press launch" (getting buzz). The MVP is explicitly allowed to be ugly, manual, unscalable, and embarrassing — that's a feature, not a bug, because speed to real feedback beats polish every time. The main caveat speakers add is that "minimum" cannot mean "useless": the product still has to be genuinely viable enough that at least one person is glad it exists.

**State:** #talks: 26 · maturity: strong
**Open threads:** How minimal is too minimal — most speakers push extreme, embarrassing-speed minimalism, while a few (the "viable" term itself, and Reid Hoffman's-quote framing) caution the MVP must still functionally work for someone, not just ship fast for its own sake. Separately, does "MVP" even apply to hard tech/biotech? Some argue no true MVP exists pre-approval in biotech (progress is de-risking milestones, not iterative shipping), while others show hard-tech and biotech founders finding MVP-equivalents anyway (beaker-scale demos, LOIs, simple explanatory websites).
