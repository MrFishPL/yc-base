# Hiring — Compiled Truth

YC's collective advice treats hiring as one of a founder's highest-leverage and highest-risk activities, but insists the right approach flips entirely on whether you're pre- or post-product-market-fit: before PMF, hiring rarely fixes the real problem and headcount is a vanity metric that burns cash and founder energy, so the default is "don't hire, and if unsure whether you're pre- or post-PMF, figure that out first." After PMF, the advice inverts to "hire aggressively," but even then a founder's own hours — roughly half their time by several accounts — should go into proactive, outbound recruiting rather than waiting for inbound applicants, since startups win candidates on speed, personalization, and transparency (fast offers, full comp details up front, personal follow-ups) rather than pedigree or prestige. Across dozens of talks, a consistent playbook emerges for judging people: prioritize trust, flexibility, and productivity/ownership over deep domain expertise or brand-name résumés for early hires; use structured interviews with a single decision-maker rather than ad hoc gut calls; screen for values/culture fit (including how a candidate treats junior staff) as hard as for skill; and never tolerate a bad hire for months once it's clear they're wrong for the seat. The AI-native turn adds a new axis — several 2024-2026 talks argue founders should now spend on tokens/automation before adding headcount, and treat AI-tool fluency itself as a hiring bar.
