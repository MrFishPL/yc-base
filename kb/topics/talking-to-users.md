# Talking to Users — Compiled Truth

Across founders, partners, and Paul Graham himself, YC's most repeated advice is that direct, personal, ongoing contact with users is non-negotiable and cannot be outsourced — not to a salesperson, a product head, a survey tool, or a data-science team — at any stage of a company, from pre-idea to post-IPO. The mechanics are consistent: interview about specific past behavior rather than hypotheticals, don't pitch your idea until the very end (if at all), let the user do most of the talking, and treat feature requests as clues to an underlying problem rather than a spec to build. Founders are pushed to get physically or personally close — showing up in person, taking support calls themselves, handing out a personal cell number, splitting interviews by segment (non-users vs. active users) — because that proximity surfaces the real, often-hidden problem (and the "why" behind it) that an internally-generated vision never would. The single biggest failure mode named again and again is founders shrinking from this contact because it feels like sales, and retreating to build alone instead.

**State:** #talks: 128 · maturity: strong
**Open threads:** (1) Ask vs. observe — the Mom Test/interview school says structured questions about specific past behavior ("tell me about the last time X happened") reveal the truth, while Meesho's founders found the opposite on their second product: they got their real breakthrough only after they stopped asking shopkeepers questions and just silently watched them work, arguing people "often can't accurately articulate their own problems." (2) How much to trust what users say — several speakers warn that verbal praise is nearly worthless and only usage/retention/payment data can be trusted, while others (Superhuman's PMF survey, a single vivid Scentbird customer quote) treat a well-designed question or one emotional interview moment as decisive proof of product-market fit.
