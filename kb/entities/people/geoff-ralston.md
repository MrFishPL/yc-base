# Geoff Ralston — Compiled Truth

Former Y Combinator president who led Startup School and often moderated its marquee guest
conversations (Paul Graham, Aileen Lee). Co-founded Lala (sold to Apple) before joining YC. His
recurring throughline across talks is founder psychology under adversity: things will always go
wrong, so resilience, a self-sustaining narrative, default-to-action, and default-alive discipline
matter more than any single tactic — paired with concrete, no-nonsense fundraising mechanics
(post-money safes, dilution benchmarks, story-first pitching). As an angel-investing educator
(Startup Investor School), he also teaches that founder passion and grit under pressure-testing
matter more than market size or intelligence, and that treating founders well pays off in deal
flow over time.

**Recurring positions (cited):**
- The whole fundraising loop in one minute: craft your story, find the right investors, pitch
  repeatedly and iterate, agree on price, get the money in the bank, then get back to work
  [fundraising-fundamentals-by-geoff-ralston @ 02:43](https://youtu.be/gcevHkNGrWQ?t=163).
- The best time to raise money is when you don't need it — investors can smell desperation, and
  having money itself becomes a competitive advantage
  [fundraising-fundamentals-by-geoff-ralston @ 06:19](https://youtu.be/gcevHkNGrWQ?t=379).
- YC's standard instrument is a post-money safe (e.g., $150,000 for 7%) instead of a pre-money
  safe, because it gives founders and investors a clear, unambiguous read on ownership percentage
  at the moment of investment
  [fundraising-fundamentals-by-geoff-ralston @ 16:45](https://youtu.be/gcevHkNGrWQ?t=1005).
- Long-term (e.g., five-year) financial projections at seed are a red flag from either party —
  Ralston calls investors who demand them "noobs," since pre-product forecasts carry 50-100% error
  bars [fundraising-fundamentals-by-geoff-ralston @ 43:50](https://youtu.be/gcevHkNGrWQ?t=2630).
- Things will always go wrong at a startup — that's almost the beauty of it, and founders are the
  "problem solvers in chief" [geoff-ralston-parting-advice @ 04:35](https://youtu.be/s8ER6DIlAvY?t=275).
- When things go disastrously wrong, the default answer is action: code, talk to users, make the
  product better — sadness leads to inaction, apathy, and depression
  [geoff-ralston-parting-advice @ 10:29](https://youtu.be/s8ER6DIlAvY?t=629).
- Staying default alive lets you survive through anything, so keep profitability within reach
  whenever possible [geoff-ralston-parting-advice @ 11:17](https://youtu.be/s8ER6DIlAvY?t=677).
- Startups are almost certainly the hardest, most difficult thing a founder will do
  professionally, and without the right story to tell yourself you will quit and the startup will
  die [geoff-ralston-the-story-of-your-startup @ 00:34](https://youtu.be/yjOyMqYEX_8?t=34).
- Startup School's real value is community and mentorship — unpaid advisors and a peer cohort —
  more than the free lecture content itself
  [geoff-ralston-and-adora-cheung-discuss-startup-school @ 24:59](https://youtu.be/BTS-rFzROOg?t=1499).
- When evaluating founders, the first thing he reads for is genuine belief and near-obsessive
  passion, because without that resilience nothing else — market size, competition, funding,
  intelligence — matters
  [startup-investor-school-preview-with-geoff-ralston @ 08:50](https://youtu.be/AJDR-jMaFcg?t=530).
- Treating founders poorly to squeeze out extra return can cost far more long-term, since deal
  flow comes from founders recommending investors to each other as good people to have on the cap
  table [startup-investor-school-preview-with-geoff-ralston @ 28:57](https://youtu.be/AJDR-jMaFcg?t=1737).
- Your 20s are the time to take risk because you're at your most energetic, creative, and
  unencumbered, so choose your startup carefully and maximize your time
  [work-at-a-startup-expo-2019 @ 20:52](https://youtu.be/7bGZAj7G4WI?t=1252).
