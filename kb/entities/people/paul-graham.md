# Paul Graham — Compiled Truth

Co-founder of **Y Combinator**, essayist, and former Viaweb founder. Across his talks he repeatedly
returns to a small set of hard-won lessons: (1) founders should do unscalable, artisanal things for
their earliest customers because the learning is worth more than the efficiency lost
[paul-graham-what-does-it-mean-to-do-things-that-don-t-scale @ 00:05](https://youtu.be/5-TgqZ8nado?t=5);
(2) the single biggest founder mistake is retreating into a self-generated "vision" instead of doing
the humbling work of talking to real users and facing their harsh feedback
[paul-graham-what-are-some-common-mistakes-founders-make @ 00:05](https://youtu.be/845O4RE1XDM?t=5);
(3) startups are governed by a power law and irreducible luck, so even a perfect picker would still need
to fund ~20 companies to get ~5 giant outcomes
[a-conversation-with-paul-graham-moderated-by-geoff-ralston @ 08:01](https://youtu.be/4WO5kJChg3w?t=481);
(4) determination beats intelligence as a predictor of founder success
[a-conversation-with-paul-graham-moderated-by-geoff-ralston @ 35:22](https://youtu.be/4WO5kJChg3w?t=2122);
and (5) concentrating in a hub like Silicon Valley (or, temporarily, YC itself) compounds a founder's
access to better peers, faster-deciding investors, and a pay-it-forward culture
[paul-graham-founder-of-y-combinator-live-from-stockholm @ 01:30](https://youtu.be/QHJkUw31YX8?t=90).
He also repeatedly insists that startup mechanics themselves are secondary — what you actually need
is expertise in your own users, not expertise in "doing startups," which is why founders should generate
ideas as side effects of curiosity rather than by deliberate brainstorming
[before-the-startup-with-paul-graham-how-to-start-a-startup-2 @ 05:34](https://youtu.be/f4_14pZlJBs?t=334).
