# Elad Gil and Pejman Nozad - Startup Investor School Day 3 — Compiled Truth

Two prolific angels lay out how they pick and help startups: Elad Gil argues markets matter more than teams and gives concrete signals of a great market, while Pejman Nozad tells his own rags-to-riches story to argue that genuine founder-market fit, relentlessness, and long-term vision matter more than pedigree.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Elad Gil ranks markets first (twice) and team third, with a "no jerks" filter as a fourth criterion for who he'll work with long-term [elad-gil-and-pejman-nozad-startup-investor-school-day-3 @ 06:54](https://youtu.be/VThN7kpjRpM?t=414)
- Early compounding even off a small base is a key market signal: a $100K ARR company growing 20%/month compounds to $630M in four years versus just $35M at 13%/month [elad-gil-and-pejman-nozad-startup-investor-school-day-3 @ 08:29](https://youtu.be/VThN7kpjRpM?t=509)
- Customers paying a premium and large marquee logos paying early (e.g., PagerDuty having Google and Apple as customers at seed) is a strong product-market-fit signal that other investors often miss [elad-gil-and-pejman-nozad-startup-investor-school-day-3 @ 10:16](https://youtu.be/VThN7kpjRpM?t=616)
- Real moats are rare — network effects can be real but fragile (MySpace), data moats are usually a red herring, and durable defensibility comes from long-term margin, revenue recurrence, and customer mix [elad-gil-and-pejman-nozad-startup-investor-school-day-3 @ 12:35](https://youtu.be/VThN7kpjRpM?t=755)
- On teams, Gil looks for founders who already have a product (even a crappy alpha), who learn fast, who can sell in whatever form fits them, and who are visibly determined [elad-gil-and-pejman-nozad-startup-investor-school-day-3 @ 14:55](https://youtu.be/VThN7kpjRpM?t=895)
- Angels tend to underweight valuation: on ten $100K bets where one exits at $200M, investing at $5M vs. $20M valuation is the difference between breaking even and a 3x return [elad-gil-and-pejman-nozad-startup-investor-school-day-3 @ 18:40](https://youtu.be/VThN7kpjRpM?t=1120)
- A "bad" team signal can be misleading — Gil passed on a founder he'd seen underperform at a prior company who later built one of the most successful post-Twitter startups [elad-gil-and-pejman-nozad-startup-investor-school-day-3 @ 20:02](https://youtu.be/VThN7kpjRpM?t=1202)
- Pejman Nozad built his entire network by treating Persian-carpet sales to Silicon Valley tech customers as relationship-building, eventually seeding Dropbox, DoorDash, and Danger out of his rug gallery's back room [elad-gil-and-pejman-nozad-startup-investor-school-day-3 @ 40:20](https://youtu.be/VThN7kpjRpM?t=2420)
- Nozad passed on investing in Facebook's Series B over a disagreement about a 5-year office lease, teaching him to never let small details block a bet on an exceptional founder [elad-gil-and-pejman-nozad-startup-investor-school-day-3 @ 44:21](https://youtu.be/VThN7kpjRpM?t=2661)
- Nozad looks for founders with lived history with the problem they're solving, who take big personal risk (quitting before raising), and who hold a long-term mission rather than a short-term goal — citing Drew Houston's answer that Dropbox's real 20-year goal was cutting the work week from five days to four [elad-gil-and-pejman-nozad-startup-investor-school-day-3 @ 51:00](https://youtu.be/VThN7kpjRpM?t=3060)
- Both agree co-founder conflicts that surface very early are usually unresolvable, while later conflicts are usually fixable through direct communication [elad-gil-and-pejman-nozad-startup-investor-school-day-3 @ 1:03:34](https://youtu.be/VThN7kpjRpM?t=3814)
