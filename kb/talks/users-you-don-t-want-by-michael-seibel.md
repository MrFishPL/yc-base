# Users You Don't Want by Michael Seibel — Compiled Truth

Michael Seibel argues that early-stage startups must actively identify and kick out "hijack users" — people who use the product in ways that create no long-term value and actively harm it — rather than letting them steer the product roadmap.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Justin.tv was "by definition hijacked": built for people to livestream their lives, within a year it was being used to stream copyrighted content around the world [users-you-don-t-want-by-michael-seibel @ 00:08](https://youtu.be/BSYExUUrmD4?t=8).
- Not all off-label usage is bad — some of Justin.tv's hijack users turned out to be gamers, a group the team eventually built into what became Twitch [users-you-don-t-want-by-michael-seibel @ 00:56](https://youtu.be/BSYExUUrmD4?t=56).
- He frames product usage as a spectrum: users using the product as intended, users using it in interesting ways with long-term potential worth studying, users creating only short-term value, and hijack users who create no value and actively harm the network [users-you-don-t-want-by-michael-seibel @ 01:17](https://youtu.be/BSYExUUrmD4?t=77).
- Most YC founders don't know how to recognize hijack users and kick them out because no one talks about this group — advice usually only covers users with some value [users-you-don-t-want-by-michael-seibel @ 02:34](https://youtu.be/BSYExUUrmD4?t=154).
- Whether a support-heavy user is worth keeping depends on whether their revenue (or the learning they generate) justifies the support cost; on a free product, heavy support usage with no learning is a red flag [users-you-don-t-want-by-michael-seibel @ 03:18](https://youtu.be/BSYExUUrmD4?t=198).
- Using a hypothetical Airbnb example — guests renting apartments to host illegal "crack den" parties — he argues that even paying users should be cut if the negative impact (property damage, legal risk) outweighs the revenue [users-you-don-t-want-by-michael-seibel @ 03:58](https://youtu.be/BSYExUUrmD4?t=238).
- Founders tend to feel worse about kicking out paying hijack users than free ones, but paying for the product doesn't make a harmful user worth keeping [users-you-don-t-want-by-michael-seibel @ 05:02](https://youtu.be/BSYExUUrmD4?t=302).
- The core danger is letting hijack users steer the product roadmap — founders should focus on solving one problem really well rather than half-solving many problems for whoever walks in the door [users-you-don-t-want-by-michael-seibel @ 05:20](https://youtu.be/BSYExUUrmD4?t=320).
- It's fine to experiment with promising off-label use cases, but a founder must retain a clear opinion about the product rather than letting any user segment dictate features [users-you-don-t-want-by-michael-seibel @ 06:16](https://youtu.be/BSYExUUrmD4?t=376).
