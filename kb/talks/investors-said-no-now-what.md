# Investors Said No, Now What? — Compiled Truth

YC's core fundraising advice is "believe the no, don't believe the why" — investor rejection reasons are rarely reliable expert diagnoses, and founders should keep pitching and improve the actual business rather than chase or internalize any single investor's stated objection.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Founders wrongly assume investors are experts on their market, when in reality YC partners rarely feel qualified to judge an idea themselves, even after reading thousands of applications [investors-said-no-now-what @ 02:18](https://youtu.be/euZH0tVotPQ?t=138)
- The same investors who reject a founder's pitch will sometimes later want to invest once that founder gets into YC, even though nothing fundamental about the company changed, showing rejection reasons often aren't fixed expert judgments [investors-said-no-now-what @ 03:20](https://youtu.be/euZH0tVotPQ?t=200)
- Materially changing your product or market based on a single investor's feedback can actually make you less fundable, since it signals you're relying on the investor for product insight rather than having conviction [investors-said-no-now-what @ 04:45](https://youtu.be/euZH0tVotPQ?t=285)
- The stated rejection reason (e.g. "market's too small") is often not the real reason, which is more likely something like a missing technical co-founder, negative margins, or lack of growth [investors-said-no-now-what @ 05:25](https://youtu.be/euZH0tVotPQ?t=325)
- Only if a founder collects many rejections (15-25+) and the stated reasons are consistent across most of them should that feedback be treated as a real data point worth considering [investors-said-no-now-what @ 08:38](https://youtu.be/euZH0tVotPQ?t=518)
- Real reasons investors do reject founders include doubting the founder can build and lead a large organization, or not seeming to even aspire to that role [investors-said-no-now-what @ 09:42](https://youtu.be/euZH0tVotPQ?t=582)
- Investors are effectively pattern-matching founders against past successes and then stack-ranking them against other founders they're currently evaluating, and a "no" can simply mean you ranked lower, not that the idea is bad [investors-said-no-now-what @ 10:49](https://youtu.be/euZH0tVotPQ?t=649)
- About 60% of companies that get into YC were previously rejected by YC itself, illustrating that a rejection reflects a snapshot in time, not a permanent verdict [investors-said-no-now-what @ 12:43](https://youtu.be/euZH0tVotPQ?t=763)
- Founders should keep a list of investors who gave reasonable no's and send them monthly updates, since investors who see genuine progress will often reach out and reverse their decision [investors-said-no-now-what @ 13:06](https://youtu.be/euZH0tVotPQ?t=786)
- The best way to change an investor's mind is not better words in a follow-up email but changing the actual facts of the business, e.g. closing new customers, since progress is a far stronger signal than pitch tweaks [investors-said-no-now-what @ 13:44](https://youtu.be/euZH0tVotPQ?t=824)
