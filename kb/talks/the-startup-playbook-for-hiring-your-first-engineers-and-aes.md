# The Startup Playbook for Hiring Your First Engineers and AEs — Compiled Truth

Juicebox co-founder David Paffenholz lays out a sales-like playbook for sourcing, pitching, and closing a startup's first engineers and account executives, arguing hiring is fundamentally an outbound sales problem.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Your first 10 hires (and the next 40 after) define your company's culture, velocity, and future, so speed shouldn't be prioritized over who you bring on [the-startup-playbook-for-hiring-your-first-engineers-and-aes @ 00:47](https://youtu.be/i_PjjXKNpA4?t=47)
- Candidates evaluate joining a startup against big tech (stable, slower, less impact) and growth-stage companies (predictable comp upside, some structure) versus a startup's high-risk, high-ownership, culture-shaping opportunity [the-startup-playbook-for-hiring-your-first-engineers-and-aes @ 02:01](https://youtu.be/i_PjjXKNpA4?t=121)
- Sourcing (proactively finding candidates) matters most because the best candidates aren't applying; it should be treated like outbound sales with a search, outreach, interview, and close funnel [the-startup-playbook-for-hiring-your-first-engineers-and-aes @ 07:39](https://youtu.be/i_PjjXKNpA4?t=459)
- For engineers, look for signals like unique founder connections (e.g., shared nationality), specific technologies used, open-source contributions, or prior side-project/startup experience [the-startup-playbook-for-hiring-your-first-engineers-and-aes @ 11:42](https://youtu.be/i_PjjXKNpA4?t=702)
- For AEs, look for quota attainment signals (100%+ attainment, President's Club) and fast promotion cycles within the same company/team as evidence of strong performance [the-startup-playbook-for-hiring-your-first-engineers-and-aes @ 10:16](https://youtu.be/i_PjjXKNpA4?t=616)
- The number one mistake founders make is skipping straight to interviewing instead of selling the company first; the first call should focus on why the company is exciting before assessing the candidate [the-startup-playbook-for-hiring-your-first-engineers-and-aes @ 24:10](https://youtu.be/i_PjjXKNpA4?t=1450)
- Outreach personalization drives results: campaigns with 10-20% reply rates are decent, but the best (40%+) come from highly personalized, creative targeting rather than volume, and the "interested rate" matters more than raw reply rate [the-startup-playbook-for-hiring-your-first-engineers-and-aes @ 18:10](https://youtu.be/i_PjjXKNpA4?t=1090)
- Startups' main advantage in closing is speed: the whole process can run in 7-14 days versus much slower big tech and growth-stage hiring pipelines [the-startup-playbook-for-hiring-your-first-engineers-and-aes @ 25:06](https://youtu.be/i_PjjXKNpA4?t=1506)
- Every founder should personally be involved in outreach and sourcing since it shapes culture, and outreach from more senior people (ideally founders) gets better response rates [the-startup-playbook-for-hiring-your-first-engineers-and-aes @ 20:22](https://youtu.be/i_PjjXKNpA4?t=1222)
- Hiring is a repeated game: a candidate who turns down an offer due to stability concerns may still join later, so founders should keep relationships warm rather than write candidates off [the-startup-playbook-for-hiring-your-first-engineers-and-aes @ 39:41](https://youtu.be/i_PjjXKNpA4?t=2381)
- Once founders are working on more than two open hires at a time, it starts making sense to bring in recruiting help, choosing between in-house, contract/embedded, or contingency recruiters based on hiring volume and commitment level [the-startup-playbook-for-hiring-your-first-engineers-and-aes @ 41:11](https://youtu.be/i_PjjXKNpA4?t=2471)
