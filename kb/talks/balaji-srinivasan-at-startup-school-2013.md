# Balaji Srinivasan at Startup School 2013 — Compiled Truth

Balaji Srinivasan frames Silicon Valley's startups as a form of "exit" that complements political "voice," arguing that technology (mobile, Bitcoin, 3D printing) will increasingly let people opt out of legacy institutions rather than fight to reform them.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Riffing on a 1998 Bill Gates quote about fearing "some guys in a garage," Srinivasan frames the US as analogous to an aging incumbent like Microsoft, which could only be displaced by Google exiting to start a new company rather than reforming from within [balaji-srinivasan-at-startup-school-2013 @ 01:39](https://youtu.be/cOubCHLXT6A?t=99).
- He introduces "voice versus exit" as a core political-science concept: voice means changing a system from within (a patch, a complaint, a turnaround plan, voting), while exit means leaving to build something new (a fork, a startup, immigration) [balaji-srinivasan-at-startup-school-2013 @ 02:46](https://youtu.be/cOubCHLXT6A?t=166).
- Exit amplifies voice, and one of YC's earliest ads captured this ethos with the line "Larry and Sergey won't respect you in the morning" [balaji-srinivasan-at-startup-school-2013 @ 05:20](https://youtu.be/cOubCHLXT6A?t=320).
- He casts the conflict as Silicon Valley versus the "paper belt" — Boston (higher ed), New York (media/Wall Street), LA (entertainment), and DC (regulation) — with tech already reinventing each industry [balaji-srinivasan-at-startup-school-2013 @ 07:43](https://youtu.be/cOubCHLXT6A?t=463).
- Companies like Uber, Airbnb, Stripe, Square, and Bitcoin are cited as threats to DC's regulatory power, producing a "paper jam" backlash blaming Silicon Valley for unemployment and inequality [balaji-srinivasan-at-startup-school-2013 @ 09:21](https://youtu.be/cOubCHLXT6A?t=561).
- He argues technology's role is to reduce prices: an MVP that barely works and costs thousands eventually gets refined until it reaches the 10%, then the 50%, then the 99%, unlike the paper belt's tuition, mortgage, and medical-care bubbles that raise prices [balaji-srinivasan-at-startup-school-2013 @ 10:30](https://youtu.be/cOubCHLXT6A?t=630).
- "Silicon Valley's ultimate exit" means building an opt-in society, ultimately outside the US, run by technology, echoing Larry Page's idea of setting aside unregulated territory for experimentation [balaji-srinivasan-at-startup-school-2013 @ 12:02](https://youtu.be/cOubCHLXT6A?t=722).
- He calls for using technology, especially mobile, to reduce the barriers to exit, arguing people can opt out to whatever degree they prefer without needing to physically relocate [balaji-srinivasan-at-startup-school-2013 @ 14:32](https://youtu.be/cOubCHLXT6A?t=872).
- He closes by urging founders to build technologies — as minimal or maximal as they choose — that reduce lock-in and the barriers to exit from legacy institutions [balaji-srinivasan-at-startup-school-2013 @ 16:04](https://youtu.be/cOubCHLXT6A?t=964).
