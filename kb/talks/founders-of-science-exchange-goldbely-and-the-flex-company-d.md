# Founders of Science Exchange, Goldbely, and The Flex Company Discuss Fundraising — Compiled Truth

Three women YC founders at different stages (Science Exchange's Series C, Goldbely's Series A, and Flex's seed) compare notes on how fundraising difficulty, pitch messaging, and metrics evolve as a company grows.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- The founders rated their first fundraise as the hardest, with Flex's seed round rated a 9/10 in difficulty versus later rounds rated closer to a 4/10 [founders-of-science-exchange-goldbely-and-the-flex-company-d @ 03:34](https://youtu.be/i5DL6qsDd44?t=214).
- Science Exchange's early pitch focused on the exciting science itself, but investors only got engaged once the founder reframed the story around market size, transactional cost savings, and billion-dollar business potential [founders-of-science-exchange-goldbely-and-the-flex-company-d @ 07:06](https://youtu.be/i5DL6qsDd44?t=426).
- Goldbely used physical props (bringing a signature food item and researching each investor's hometown or alma mater for a nostalgic connection) to make its concept instantly tangible before and during pitch meetings [founders-of-science-exchange-goldbely-and-the-flex-company-d @ 10:17](https://youtu.be/i5DL6qsDd44?t=617).
- Founders recommend practicing pitches on lower-priority angel investors first, and directly asking them what metrics they expect to see at your stage before approaching top-choice firms [founders-of-science-exchange-goldbely-and-the-flex-company-d @ 11:38](https://youtu.be/i5DL6qsDd44?t=698).
- What investors want to see shifts by stage: early rounds emphasize vision, team, and market size, while later rounds (like Series C) demand proof of product-market fit, large enterprise customers, and clear unit economics [founders-of-science-exchange-goldbely-and-the-flex-company-d @ 14:16](https://youtu.be/i5DL6qsDd44?t=856).
- One founder noted that pushing through the initial, most bias-prone funding stage and getting early traction tends to outweigh gender-related friction in later rounds [founders-of-science-exchange-goldbely-and-the-flex-company-d @ 16:25](https://youtu.be/i5DL6qsDd44?t=985).
- Being profitable before a raise gave one founder more negotiating leverage and sped up the fundraising process to just a couple of months [founders-of-science-exchange-goldbely-and-the-flex-company-d @ 23:16](https://youtu.be/i5DL6qsDd44?t=1396).
- During fundraising, teams intentionally split roles so one co-founder focused entirely on investors while others kept the business's metrics and growth on track, since a stalled metric trajectory would hurt the raise itself [founders-of-science-exchange-goldbely-and-the-flex-company-d @ 21:31](https://youtu.be/i5DL6qsDd44?t=1291).
- A recurring piece of advice was to avoid founder burnout from working 24/7, and to seek out other founders early to talk honestly about struggles, since every company feels "messed up" behind the scenes [founders-of-science-exchange-goldbely-and-the-flex-company-d @ 24:23](https://youtu.be/i5DL6qsDd44?t=1463).
