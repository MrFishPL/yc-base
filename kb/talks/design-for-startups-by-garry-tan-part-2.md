# Design for Startups by Garry Tan (Part 2) — Compiled Truth

Garry Tan gives the practical playbook for when and how startups should find, hire, and interview designers, then answers audience questions on About pages, user interviews, and controversy as a growth lever.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Pre-seed/seed founders should design it themselves with a co-founder; by Series A almost every company needs its first dedicated designer hire [design-for-startups-by-garry-tan-part-2 @ 00:16](https://youtu.be/O6uREh3G3sQ?t=16)
- Sites like Dribbble, LinkedIn, Behance, and design schools (CMU, RISD, Parsons, Stanford, etc.) are good sourcing channels, though many great designers never attended design school [design-for-startups-by-garry-tan-part-2 @ 01:42](https://youtu.be/O6uREh3G3sQ?t=102)
- A clever low-cost trick is posting the same job on 99designs/Fiverr to 20 different freelancers, comparing output, and personally befriending the best performer [design-for-startups-by-garry-tan-part-2 @ 03:08](https://youtu.be/O6uREh3G3sQ?t=188)
- Hiring a lone designer onto a team of 15-20 engineers with no design-savvy culture is intimidating for candidates, so founders should build fluency in design language themselves first [design-for-startups-by-garry-tan-part-2 @ 05:33](https://youtu.be/O6uREh3G3sQ?t=333)
- Interviews should screen for communication skills on the phone, then have candidates walk through hard product trade-offs in person rather than just critique portfolios [design-for-startups-by-garry-tan-part-2 @ 07:54](https://youtu.be/O6uREh3G3sQ?t=474)
- A red flag in a design interview is a candidate who jumps straight to whiteboard solutions without first asking who the user is and what problem is being solved [design-for-startups-by-garry-tan-part-2 @ 09:15](https://youtu.be/O6uREh3G3sQ?t=555)
- An authentic About Us page, with founders' real names and stories, builds trust the way Coinbase's Brian Armstrong did early on [design-for-startups-by-garry-tan-part-2 @ 11:28](https://youtu.be/O6uREh3G3sQ?t=688)
- Good user interviews rely on open-ended questions and probing for emotion, since the best problems to solve are the ones that genuinely upset people [design-for-startups-by-garry-tan-part-2 @ 13:03](https://youtu.be/O6uREh3G3sQ?t=783)
- Founders are only really competing against obscurity, so openness and being human beats secrecy for most startups [design-for-startups-by-garry-tan-part-2 @ 16:34](https://youtu.be/O6uREh3G3sQ?t=994)
- Soylent's provocative name generated huge free earned attention because it created real controversy that people talked about, even though Tan advised against it at the time [design-for-startups-by-garry-tan-part-2 @ 17:41](https://youtu.be/O6uREh3G3sQ?t=1061)
