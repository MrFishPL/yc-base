# How to Get Started, Doing Things that Don't Scale, and Press (How to Start a Startup 2014: 8) — Compiled Truth

Three founders (DoorDash's Stanley Tang, Teespring's Walker Williams, and Justin.tv/Twitch's Justin Kan) share how they got their first users by doing unscalable, manual work, and how to think deliberately about press as a goal-driven, relationship-based tactic rather than a vanity metric.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- DoorDash started as a one-afternoon landing page experiment ("Palo Alto Delivery") with a personal phone number, built to test the delivery hypothesis before investing in any infrastructure, and it got its first real order within hours [how-to-get-started-doing-things-that-don-t-scale-and-press-h @ 03:22](https://youtu.be/sr0UabJd8qE?t=202)
- Early DoorDash founders were the delivery drivers, customer support, and marketing, hacking together Square for payments, Google Docs for orders, and Find My Friends for driver tracking instead of building real systems [how-to-get-started-doing-things-that-don-t-scale-and-press-h @ 06:41](https://youtu.be/sr0UabJd8qE?t=401)
- Doing unscalable work made the DoorDash founders experts in their own business — manual dispatching taught them what their driver-assignment algorithm should look like, and personalized post-delivery emails to every new customer built loyalty [how-to-get-started-doing-things-that-don-t-scale-and-press-h @ 08:03](https://youtu.be/sr0UabJd8qE?t=483)
- Stanley Tang's three takeaways: treat your startup idea like an experiment, launch fast (DoorDash launched in under an hour), and lean into doing things that don't scale as a competitive advantage until you have proven demand [how-to-get-started-doing-things-that-don-t-scale-and-press-h @ 10:32](https://youtu.be/sr0UabJd8qE?t=632)
- Walker Williams argues there's no silver bullet for first users — most companies' "dream growth curves" actually came from grueling, unsustainable manual effort like Teespring's founders personally designing, revising, and selling shirts one order at a time [how-to-get-started-doing-things-that-don-t-scale-and-press-h @ 17:14](https://youtu.be/sr0UabJd8qE?t=1034)
- Talking to users directly (running your own customer support, proactively reaching out to current and churned customers, and monitoring social media) is the fastest way to learn what to build next and to turn frustrated users into your biggest champions [how-to-get-started-doing-things-that-don-t-scale-and-press-h @ 21:20](https://youtu.be/sr0UabJd8qE?t=1280)
- Teespring optimized for speed over clean, scalable code — duplicating its codebase to serve enterprise customers in days instead of the month a "proper" build would take — and Williams recommends only worrying about the next order of magnitude of users, not the millionth [how-to-get-started-doing-things-that-don-t-scale-and-press-h @ 26:20](https://youtu.be/sr0UabJd8qE?t=1580)
- Justin Kan says press should be pursued only with a specific business goal in mind (e.g., targeting tech press to reach investors, or local press to reach a city's customers), not for its own sake, since it's a vanity-adjacent metric that doesn't equal real traction [how-to-get-started-doing-things-that-don-t-scale-and-press-h @ 33:49](https://youtu.be/sr0UabJd8qE?t=2029)
- The most reliable way to land a story is a warm introduction from an entrepreneur a reporter has already covered, followed by a face-to-face or phone pitch structured around memorized bullet points, rather than a cold email [how-to-get-started-doing-things-that-don-t-scale-and-press-h @ 40:05](https://youtu.be/sr0UabJd8qE?t=2405)
- Kan advises doing PR yourself before hiring an expensive PR firm ($5,000-$20,000/month), since firms can manage logistics and contacts but cannot tell you what's actually interesting about your company [how-to-get-started-doing-things-that-don-t-scale-and-press-h @ 44:57](https://youtu.be/sr0UabJd8qE?t=2697)
