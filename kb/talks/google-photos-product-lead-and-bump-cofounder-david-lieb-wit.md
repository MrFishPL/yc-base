# Google Photos Product Lead and Bump Cofounder David Lieb with Gustaf Alströmer — Compiled Truth

David Lieb recounts Bump's viral rise and failure to become a durable business due to weak usage frequency, the pivots through Flock and Photo Roll driven by direct user conversations, and the product philosophy that carried into building Google Photos.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Bump grew to 150 million downloads and 10 million monthly active users but the team tracked total usage instead of cohort retention, and only in hindsight realized retention was the single most important metric [google-photos-product-lead-and-bump-cofounder-david-lieb-wit @ 07:48](https://youtu.be/fDcW_qb-uew?t=468)
- Bump's long-term retention was fine but usage frequency was very low and each use delivered only marginal value, which made it hard to build a real business on top of a large but infrequently-engaged user base [google-photos-product-lead-and-bump-cofounder-david-lieb-wit @ 08:53](https://youtu.be/fDcW_qb-uew?t=533)
- Bump grew entirely through word-of-mouth driven by its novel physical "bump" gesture, spending almost nothing on marketing or customer acquisition [google-photos-product-lead-and-bump-cofounder-david-lieb-wit @ 10:40](https://youtu.be/fDcW_qb-uew?t=640)
- Product-market-fit is not a single binary state — it can exist for one customer segment while being absent for another as you expand, as seen at both Bump and Google Photos [google-photos-product-lead-and-bump-cofounder-david-lieb-wit @ 11:47](https://youtu.be/fDcW_qb-uew?t=707)
- Following PG's advice to "go talk to your users" when stuck, calling Bump's top 100 users revealed they mainly used the app to share photos, not contacts, which led directly to building Flock [google-photos-product-lead-and-bump-cofounder-david-lieb-wit @ 13:12](https://youtu.be/fDcW_qb-uew?t=792)
- Flock failed because it required both users to already have the app with no core solo utility — violating the "come for the tool, stay for the network" principle — teaching the team they had to become the camera roll itself [google-photos-product-lead-and-bump-cofounder-david-lieb-wit @ 16:36](https://youtu.be/fDcW_qb-uew?t=996)
- When designing Google Photos, Lieb used the technique of imagining a tireless, all-knowing human personal photo assistant and brainstorming everything that assistant would do, which produced the backup, face-recognition, and reminder features [google-photos-product-lead-and-bump-cofounder-david-lieb-wit @ 29:27](https://youtu.be/fDcW_qb-uew?t=1767)
- For evaluating AI startups, he frames it as two vectors: scaling a task humans can already do well but not at scale/price, versus solving problems humans fundamentally cannot solve at all, with the latter being riskier but higher payoff [google-photos-product-lead-and-bump-cofounder-david-lieb-wit @ 35:05](https://youtu.be/fDcW_qb-uew?t=2105)
- His biggest early product mistake at Bump was over-optimizing system performance and micro-latency instead of asking whether they were even solving the right problem — citing Sam Altman's line that the meta-problem killing most startups is working on the wrong thing [google-photos-product-lead-and-bump-cofounder-david-lieb-wit @ 40:27](https://youtu.be/fDcW_qb-uew?t=2427)
- Raising a large Series B gave Bump so much runway that it delayed existential strategic questions by roughly a year, and his advice is to only raise more money when you know specifically how you'll use it [google-photos-product-lead-and-bump-cofounder-david-lieb-wit @ 23:38](https://youtu.be/fDcW_qb-uew?t=1418)
- Consumer products succeed by scaling a durable, existing human need rather than chasing a trendy new format — Instagram won not because filters were novel but because it tapped the durable need to connect with other people [google-photos-product-lead-and-bump-cofounder-david-lieb-wit @ 55:18](https://youtu.be/fDcW_qb-uew?t=3318)
