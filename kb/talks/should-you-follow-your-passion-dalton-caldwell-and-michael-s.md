# Should You Follow Your Passion? – Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel — Compiled Truth

Passion for a "cool" idea is a weak foundation for a startup; founders who chase revenue, users, and momentum tend to develop real passion once the thing starts working, and that gravity is what sustains people through years of hard work.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Starting with a "solution in search of a problem" — building a cool piece of technology and then hunting for a market — is a common and dangerous founder trap [should-you-follow-your-passion-dalton-caldwell-and-michael-s @ 00:42](https://youtu.be/KWNNmPCF-Xs?t=42)
- Founders with no real opinions about the market often chase ideas because they carry social validation (an investor, a cool friend), mirroring how school rewards approval-seeking rather than genuine conviction [should-you-follow-your-passion-dalton-caldwell-and-michael-s @ 01:37](https://youtu.be/KWNNmPCF-Xs?t=97)
- Founders who directly experience a real, tangible problem (like failed bank-card swipes) and fix it are following genuine passion, unlike those chasing abstract "global" problems they don't personally encounter [should-you-follow-your-passion-dalton-caldwell-and-michael-s @ 05:12](https://youtu.be/KWNNmPCF-Xs?t=312)
- A useful definition of passion: would you still do this work even if there were no money and no approval from authority figures, as with the early Microsoft and Apple founders tinkering in a strip mall or a garage [should-you-follow-your-passion-dalton-caldwell-and-michael-s @ 06:34](https://youtu.be/KWNNmPCF-Xs?t=394)
- The most common founder pitfall is picking an idea (like "coordinating activities with friends") that feels deeply personal but is actually one of the most overused, already-failed college startup ideas [should-you-follow-your-passion-dalton-caldwell-and-michael-s @ 09:18](https://youtu.be/KWNNmPCF-Xs?t=558)
- Running a startup that isn't working is miserable, but once a business starts showing traction (rising revenue, growing users) founders often develop real passion for it, as happened with Twitch even before the company was profitable [should-you-follow-your-passion-dalton-caldwell-and-michael-s @ 11:24](https://youtu.be/KWNNmPCF-Xs?t=684)
- Teams should look for intrinsic motivators beyond the core problem itself — enjoying working with co-founders, not having a boss, and the thrill of consequences and winning can sustain people early on [should-you-follow-your-passion-dalton-caldwell-and-michael-s @ 13:35](https://youtu.be/KWNNmPCF-Xs?t=815)
- Deliberately making revenue a tracked, gamified metric can "hack" motivation — watching a number go up daily creates a psychological reward loop that builds passion for founders who started without any [should-you-follow-your-passion-dalton-caldwell-and-michael-s @ 15:51](https://youtu.be/KWNNmPCF-Xs?t=951)
- Founders driven by extrinsic motivators (hype, fundraising, hot tech) often look like they're winning early but usually die within two to three years, while intrinsically motivated, "weird" founders disproportionately survive [should-you-follow-your-passion-dalton-caldwell-and-michael-s @ 17:09](https://youtu.be/KWNNmPCF-Xs?t=1029)
