# Marc Andreessen at Startup School SV 2016 — Compiled Truth

Marc Andreessen explains why a16z was built around founder-CEOs and networked "superpowers," then demystifies the institutional fundraising process, reference-checking, and how to judge startup timing.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- a16z was founded on the belief that many of the best companies are run by their founders long-term, in contrast to the industry norm of installing "professional CEOs" [marc-andreessen-at-startup-school-sv-2016 @ 01:02](https://youtu.be/NEOR0AJsziE?t=62)
- a16z built an operating model to give founders "superpowers" — plugging them into networks of customers, investors, press, and recruits that a professional CEO would already have [marc-andreessen-at-startup-school-sv-2016 @ 03:40](https://youtu.be/NEOR0AJsziE?t=220)
- Institutional fundraising (Series A and beyond) is far more formal than seed/angel rounds, involving fiduciary-minded firms with established processes [marc-andreessen-at-startup-school-sv-2016 @ 06:14](https://youtu.be/NEOR0AJsziE?t=374)
- Warm referrals function as the "first test" of whether a founder can network their way to investors, hires, and customers — treat it as a test to pass, not an unfair barrier [marc-andreessen-at-startup-school-sv-2016 @ 07:45](https://youtu.be/NEOR0AJsziE?t=465)
- Engaging junior partners first is usually more effective than only chasing senior partners, since juniors can pre-flight the pitch and senior partners rarely decide from scratch [marc-andreessen-at-startup-school-sv-2016 @ 09:08](https://youtu.be/NEOR0AJsziE?t=548)
- The formal full-partnership pitch presentation is itself a second test of whether a founder can sell — a16z sees ~2,000 warm-referral startups a year and says yes to only ~20 at the institutional level [marc-andreessen-at-startup-school-sv-2016 @ 13:11](https://youtu.be/NEOR0AJsziE?t=791)
- Reference checking is the single most important way to judge an investor (or a hire) — seek "back door" references from people who worked with them, and listen for lukewarm tells like "they're very punctual" [marc-andreessen-at-startup-school-sv-2016 @ 14:28](https://youtu.be/NEOR0AJsziE?t=868)
- Andreessen draws lessons from historical founders (Thomas Watson/IBM, Bill Hewlett & Dave Packard, Edison, Henry Ford) because building something new has always been hard, and there are "no silver bullets, only lead bullets" [marc-andreessen-at-startup-school-sv-2016 @ 17:37](https://youtu.be/NEOR0AJsziE?t=1057)
- Great founders almost always feel too late rather than too early, but the historical pattern (Google as the 35th search engine, the Newton preceding the iPad by 20 years) shows most failures are from being too early, not too late [marc-andreessen-at-startup-school-sv-2016 @ 29:09](https://youtu.be/NEOR0AJsziE?t=1749)
- If starting over at 22, Andreessen would join the fastest-growing company with the best culture to learn, and would focus on cryptocurrency/distributed systems, AI/ML, or bio-CS intersection as the highest-potential fields [marc-andreessen-at-startup-school-sv-2016 @ 31:24](https://youtu.be/NEOR0AJsziE?t=1884)
