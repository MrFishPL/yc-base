# One Order of Operations for Starting a Startup by Michael Seibel — Compiled Truth

Michael Seibel gives a simple, active process for finding a startup idea: pick a problem you're passionate about, brainstorm solutions with people you respect, then build and launch an MVP together — rather than waiting for an idea to strike or chasing funding first.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- The number one blocker for talented technical people becoming founders is believing they need an amazing idea up front, when in fact most people are never taught a process for finding one [one-order-of-operations-for-starting-a-startup-by-michael-se @ 00:00](https://youtu.be/5fmDKGV0TnQ?t=0).
- His order of operations is: find a problem you're passionate about, brainstorm solutions with friends, then work together to build and launch an MVP [one-order-of-operations-for-starting-a-startup-by-michael-se @ 01:54](https://youtu.be/5fmDKGV0TnQ?t=114).
- To find a real problem, judge candidate problems by frequency and intensity — a rarely-felt annoyance (like stroller-friendly routes) is weaker than a problem like affordable child care that is both frequent and intense [one-order-of-operations-for-starting-a-startup-by-michael-se @ 03:52](https://youtu.be/5fmDKGV0TnQ?t=232).
- Your first proposed solution should be treated as a hypothesis to test and iterate on, not the final answer — trust that a big enough problem worked on for years will yield a real business [one-order-of-operations-for-starting-a-startup-by-michael-se @ 06:16](https://youtu.be/5fmDKGV0TnQ?t=376).
- Brainstorming a solution together with future co-founders builds shared ownership of the idea, which helps ignite and sustain motivation [one-order-of-operations-for-starting-a-startup-by-michael-se @ 07:56](https://youtu.be/5fmDKGV0TnQ?t=476).
- The biggest mistake at the MVP stage is listening to an imagined "Phantom MBA" voice urging incorporation and fundraising before even trying to build a simple V1, even one powered by just a spreadsheet [one-order-of-operations-for-starting-a-startup-by-michael-se @ 08:39](https://youtu.be/5fmDKGV0TnQ?t=519).
- Getting an MVP in front of even one or two customers tests whether the team can actually work together, boosts excitement before the grind of incorporation/fundraising, and gives leverage with investors [one-order-of-operations-for-starting-a-startup-by-michael-se @ 09:15](https://youtu.be/5fmDKGV0TnQ?t=555).
- Two failed orders of operations he warns against: pitching investors on an idea alone, and having an idea but no technical co-founder [one-order-of-operations-for-starting-a-startup-by-michael-se @ 10:48](https://youtu.be/5fmDKGV0TnQ?t=648).
- Outsourcing the MVP to contractors instead of finding a technical co-founder is a common failure mode — founders "iterate with the outsourcer" until money runs out, then approach investors or co-founders with a shoddy product and no traction [one-order-of-operations-for-starting-a-startup-by-michael-se @ 12:39](https://youtu.be/5fmDKGV0TnQ?t=759).
- Getting a technical co-founder tends to be cheaper in the end than paying an outsourcer, even though it feels harder upfront [one-order-of-operations-for-starting-a-startup-by-michael-se @ 13:58](https://youtu.be/5fmDKGV0TnQ?t=838).
