# Startup Investor School Day 4 Live Stream — Compiled Truth

The final day of YC's Startup Investor School traces the history of early-stage fundraising from the 1940s through the 2018 ICO boom, then pivots to a practical playbook for how angel investors build (or destroy) the reputation that drives their deal flow.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Early-stage investing has followed three secular trends across its history: decreasing cost to start a company, decreasing cost to invest, and the market's arc bending toward faster liquidity [startup-investor-school-day-4-live-stream @ 12:36](https://youtu.be/CKBeSyquRec?t=756)
- The first recognizable venture win was ARD's investment in Digital Equipment Corporation, which returned roughly 500x over 11 years and kicked off the modern venture fund model [startup-investor-school-day-4-live-stream @ 15:04](https://youtu.be/CKBeSyquRec?t=904)
- The 2%-management-fee/20%-carry fund structure, still standard today, first emerged among early venture firms in the late 1950s and 1960s [startup-investor-school-day-4-live-stream @ 16:20](https://youtu.be/CKBeSyquRec?t=980)
- By the 1980s, competition among roughly 650 venture firms forced investors to decide and invest faster and earlier to avoid losing deals [startup-investor-school-day-4-live-stream @ 21:18](https://youtu.be/CKBeSyquRec?t=1278)
- YC helped standardize convertible notes in 2005-2009 and later created the SAFE in 2014, both moves that lowered the cost and friction of raising early-stage capital [startup-investor-school-day-4-live-stream @ 25:41](https://youtu.be/CKBeSyquRec?t=1541)
- Being a good angel investor is directly correlated with deal flow: helping companies succeed makes founders talk about you, which brings more deals in a reinforcing loop [startup-investor-school-day-4-live-stream @ 1:24:49](https://youtu.be/CKBeSyquRec?t=5089)
- Angels should avoid pushing for heavy terms like board seats, 2x participating preferred, or drag-along rights in seed deals — YC's investor database flags investors who do this and their deal flow dries up [startup-investor-school-day-4-live-stream @ 1:33:11](https://youtu.be/CKBeSyquRec?t=5591)
- Waiting to invest only after seeing who else has committed leads to groupthink and worse outcomes — some of YC's best companies didn't attract broad investor interest early on [startup-investor-school-day-4-live-stream @ 1:35:08](https://youtu.be/CKBeSyquRec?t=5708)
- After closing, investors should get out of the way rather than acting like the CEO or bombarding founders with unsolicited feedback — described as becoming a "human DNS attack" [startup-investor-school-day-4-live-stream @ 1:39:17](https://youtu.be/CKBeSyquRec?t=5957)
- Being a "good" investor has nothing to do with being the smartest, writing the biggest checks, or being famous — both "dumb money" and "smart money" can be good if they are honest, move quickly, and are genuinely helpful [startup-investor-school-day-4-live-stream @ 1:50:59](https://youtu.be/CKBeSyquRec?t=6659)
