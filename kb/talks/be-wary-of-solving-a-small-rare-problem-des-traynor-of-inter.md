# Be Wary of Solving a Small, Rare Problem - Des Traynor of Intercom — Compiled Truth

Intercom co-founder Des Traynor traces the company's origin from a scrappy in-app messaging feature, and lays out hard-won lessons on content marketing, buyer psychology, keeping product and marketing in sync, and why founders should avoid building for small, rare problems.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Intercom started as a small "sorry we were down" chat widget bolted onto their earlier error-tracking product, Exceptional; user feedback on that widget outpaced interest in the core product itself [be-wary-of-solving-a-small-rare-problem-des-traynor-of-inter @ 02:15](https://youtu.be/P6pQyB6ACrk?t=135)
- Their content marketing philosophy was "content first, marketing second" — write things genuinely useful to startup readers rather than repeated pitches for the product [be-wary-of-solving-a-small-rare-problem-des-traynor-of-inter @ 06:48](https://youtu.be/P6pQyB6ACrk?t=408)
- His advice to new founders: think about who your target user actually is and all the problems in their life you can help with, and hope your product solves one of them [be-wary-of-solving-a-small-rare-problem-des-traynor-of-inter @ 07:22](https://youtu.be/P6pQyB6ACrk?t=442)
- Understanding how people shop for your product matters more than knowing how to sell it, since different customers arrive at the same product through completely different framings of their problem [be-wary-of-solving-a-small-rare-problem-des-traynor-of-inter @ 14:53](https://youtu.be/P6pQyB6ACrk?t=893)
- Effective content marketing builds a "cascading" series of ideas that walks a prospect step by step from a broad belief toward the specific product, rather than converting them in one post [be-wary-of-solving-a-small-rare-problem-des-traynor-of-inter @ 19:25](https://youtu.be/P6pQyB6ACrk?t=1165)
- Product, marketing, design, and code are all "renderings of the same core idea," and when product and marketing drift out of sync — building one thing while selling another — the company gets into real trouble [be-wary-of-solving-a-small-rare-problem-des-traynor-of-inter @ 25:22](https://youtu.be/P6pQyB6ACrk?t=1522)
- New-hire onboarding is one of the first things to break down as a company scales fast: employee 65 gets access to a much larger, more anonymous group than employee 5 did, with nobody redesigning onboarding to compensate [be-wary-of-solving-a-small-rare-problem-des-traynor-of-inter @ 31:14](https://youtu.be/P6pQyB6ACrk?t=1874)
- Hiring senior leaders in functions you don't understand (like marketing or sales) is especially risky, because "marketers are good at marketing to you" — the first candidate you meet will sell themselves on the role regardless of fit [be-wary-of-solving-a-small-rare-problem-des-traynor-of-inter @ 33:15](https://youtu.be/P6pQyB6ACrk?t=1995)
- Founders should "be really wary of solving a small, rare problem" — a product can be beautiful and still fail if the underlying problem is neither big nor frequent in people's lives [be-wary-of-solving-a-small-rare-problem-des-traynor-of-inter @ 41:30](https://youtu.be/P6pQyB6ACrk?t=2490)
- If you're only going to charge a small amount, the product needs to be entirely self-serve and frictionless; "fake traction" built on heavy founder hand-holding of early customers isn't a scalable business [be-wary-of-solving-a-small-rare-problem-des-traynor-of-inter @ 44:08](https://youtu.be/P6pQyB6ACrk?t=2648)
