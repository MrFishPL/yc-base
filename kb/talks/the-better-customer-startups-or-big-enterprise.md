# The Better Customer–Startups or Big Enterprise? — Compiled Truth

Selling to startups can be a powerful strategy (Stripe, PagerDuty, Gusto, AWS) but only when founders understand which "game" they're playing, since many founders wrongly assume startups are an easier, safer first customer than enterprise.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Selling to other startups has been a formula for success for top YC companies like Stripe and PagerDuty, whose early customers stuck around for years [the-better-customer-startups-or-big-enterprise @ 00:57](https://youtu.be/Hwfu1zNwSWI?t=57)
- Founders often cargo-cult the "sell to startups" strategy even when their product is naturally enterprise-grade, avoiding a long enterprise sales cycle rather than facing it [the-better-customer-startups-or-big-enterprise @ 01:39](https://youtu.be/Hwfu1zNwSWI?t=99)
- Selling to a customer who doesn't actually have your problem is worse than selling to one who has it but decides slowly, like trying to sell Snowflake-scale data warehousing to a startup [the-better-customer-startups-or-big-enterprise @ 03:16](https://youtu.be/Hwfu1zNwSWI?t=196)
- Startups aren't necessarily lower-maintenance customers than enterprises; end users can be equally demanding at either, but enterprise customers usually pay much more [the-better-customer-startups-or-big-enterprise @ 04:52](https://youtu.be/Hwfu1zNwSWI?t=292)
- Many product categories (HR tools, Heroku-style dev infra, CRM) reliably churn early customers as those companies grow and their needs change, unlike Stripe's payments model which scales with the customer [the-better-customer-startups-or-big-enterprise @ 05:46](https://youtu.be/Hwfu1zNwSWI?t=346)
- The buyer itself changes as a company scales (e.g., a marketing founder becomes a marketing team, a technical hiring manager becomes a non-technical recruiter), forcing a rebuilt product for bigger customers [the-better-customer-startups-or-big-enterprise @ 07:11](https://youtu.be/Hwfu1zNwSWI?t=431)
- Founders with product backgrounds sometimes chase self-serve, startup-only sales strategies to avoid doing real sales, which the hosts call the "civilization fallacy" [the-better-customer-startups-or-big-enterprise @ 09:27](https://youtu.be/Hwfu1zNwSWI?t=567)
- Gusto used startups as a training ground to build a great payroll product before expanding into the broader SMB market [the-better-customer-startups-or-big-enterprise @ 12:10](https://youtu.be/Hwfu1zNwSWI?t=730)
- AWS grew bottoms-up by winning over engineers at startups, who later carried cloud adoption into larger enterprises, but AWS and Stripe still eventually built real enterprise sales teams [the-better-customer-startups-or-big-enterprise @ 13:17](https://youtu.be/Hwfu1zNwSWI?t=797)
- Founders should identify which proven "game" (Stripe, Gusto, or AWS-style) fits their product and study companies that already nailed that strategy rather than reinventing it [the-better-customer-startups-or-big-enterprise @ 15:24](https://youtu.be/Hwfu1zNwSWI?t=924)
