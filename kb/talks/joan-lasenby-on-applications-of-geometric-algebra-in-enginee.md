# Joan Lasenby on Applications of Geometric Algebra in Engineering — Compiled Truth

Cambridge engineering professor Joan Lasenby explains geometric algebra — a unifying mathematical framework built on Clifford algebra — and how it simplifies computer vision, robotics, and physics problems that are otherwise scattered across matrices, tensors, and quaternions.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Lasenby's drone project with Cambridge's architecture department uses geometric algebra specifically because reconstructing lines (rather than points) from moving cameras is classically much harder in computer vision [joan-lasenby-on-applications-of-geometric-algebra-in-enginee @ 00:21](https://youtu.be/ikCIUzX9myY?t=21)
- Geometric algebra extends Grassmann's outer product with Clifford's combined inner-plus-outer product, letting scalars, vectors, planes (bivectors), and volumes (trivectors) all be added, multiplied, and differentiated within one algebra [joan-lasenby-on-applications-of-geometric-algebra-in-enginee @ 04:33](https://youtu.be/ikCIUzX9myY?t=273)
- David Hestenes revived Clifford's 1870s work in the 1960s and named it "geometric algebra," later showing it could simplify space-time physics enough to avoid needing separate machinery like differential geometry and tensor analysis [joan-lasenby-on-applications-of-geometric-algebra-in-enginee @ 02:57](https://youtu.be/ikCIUzX9myY?t=177)
- Rotation matrices have 9 components to represent only 3 true degrees of freedom and must be constantly re-constrained; quaternions fix some of this but geometric algebra generalizes rotation to any dimension via bivectors that square to -1, unifying quaternions as a special case [joan-lasenby-on-applications-of-geometric-algebra-in-enginee @ 13:08](https://youtu.be/ikCIUzX9myY?t=788)
- In 1999 David Hestenes and Hongbo Li introduced conformal geometric algebra, a 5-dimensional extension where points, lines, planes, circles, and spheres all become first-class algebraic objects that can be intersected and transformed with rotors [joan-lasenby-on-applications-of-geometric-algebra-in-enginee @ 23:20](https://youtu.be/ikCIUzX9myY?t=1400)
- Lasenby argues geometric algebra's real value isn't that it computes anything impossible in conventional math, but that it makes the right approach easier to think of and to program correctly the first time [joan-lasenby-on-applications-of-geometric-algebra-in-enginee @ 34:24](https://youtu.be/ikCIUzX9myY?t=2064)
- Adoption is limited less by correctness and more by switching cost: experts already fluent in matrices and tensors have to unlearn familiar tools, while students with no prior habits pick it up more easily [joan-lasenby-on-applications-of-geometric-algebra-in-enginee @ 43:20](https://youtu.be/ikCIUzX9myY?t=2600)
- Modern computer vision has moved from pure geometry toward machine learning, but Lasenby says geometric algebra remains close to the only tractable way she knows to handle moving-camera, multi-camera triangulation and motion-matching problems [joan-lasenby-on-applications-of-geometric-algebra-in-enginee @ 32:27](https://youtu.be/ikCIUzX9myY?t=1947)
- A 2000 invited paper on geometric algebra as a unifying language for computer science, engineering, and physics unexpectedly resurfaced on Hacker News years later and drew new researchers into the small geometric-algebra community [joan-lasenby-on-applications-of-geometric-algebra-in-enginee @ 40:09](https://youtu.be/ikCIUzX9myY?t=2409)
