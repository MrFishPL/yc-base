# How to Find the Right Co-founder — Compiled Truth

A YC partner argues that having a co-founder gives founders more productivity, moral support, and pattern-matches to historically successful startups, and lays out how to find, test, and formalize a co-founder relationship.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- A co-founder boosts productivity through complementary skills and better idea generation, provides moral support through the startup's emotional highs and lows, and matches the pattern of iconic successful companies like Apple, Facebook, and Microsoft, which all started with co-founders [how-to-find-the-right-co-founder @ 00:28](https://youtu.be/prKi3-rUPHc?t=28)
- The default should be to wait for a co-founder before starting, but solo founders can succeed if they have extremely high conviction in an idea (often from personal experience or domain expertise) and can independently make product progress, as Dropbox's Drew Houston did before finding co-founder Arash [how-to-find-the-right-co-founder @ 04:11](https://youtu.be/prKi3-rUPHc?t=251)
- The single most important thing to know about a potential co-founder before starting a company is how they handle stress, since startups are an intensely stressful, multi-year experience [how-to-find-the-right-co-founder @ 07:14](https://youtu.be/prKi3-rUPHc?t=434)
- It's critical to explicitly discuss goals and values for the company (e.g., high-growth/high-risk vs. slow-and-steady) because mismatched motivations between co-founders inevitably lead to conflict [how-to-find-the-right-co-founder @ 08:56](https://youtu.be/prKi3-rUPHc?t=536)
- Complementary skills matter less than trust and enjoying working together; a co-founder who lacks the exact skills needed for the current idea is fine as long as they're willing to learn and do unglamorous grunt work [how-to-find-the-right-co-founder @ 11:36](https://youtu.be/prKi3-rUPHc?t=696)
- The best way to find co-founders is to work on low-commitment side projects with people you know well before you're ready to start a company, since projects reveal who you work well with without the scarier ask of "let's start a company" [how-to-find-the-right-co-founder @ 13:51](https://youtu.be/prKi3-rUPHc?t=831)
- When actively searching, make a list of the people you're closest to, ask each one to be a co-founder, and if they say no ask them for referrals to grow a trusted network of candidates [how-to-find-the-right-co-founder @ 17:27](https://youtu.be/prKi3-rUPHc?t=1047)
- Before formalizing, derisk the relationship by allocating a set period (a month, a summer, evenings/weekends) to build an MVP or pitch customers together, then honestly decide if you want to continue [how-to-find-the-right-co-founder @ 20:30](https://youtu.be/prKi3-rUPHc?t=1230)
- The default equity split should be 50/50 since both founders make equal early contributions; taking more equity just because you made early solo progress risks under-motivating a co-founder you need fully committed for the long term [how-to-find-the-right-co-founder @ 22:22](https://youtu.be/prKi3-rUPHc?t=1342)
- If two co-founders both strongly want to be CEO and show visible contempt or interruption toward each other, that's a serious red flag meaning they shouldn't start a company together [how-to-find-the-right-co-founder @ 26:04](https://youtu.be/prKi3-rUPHc?t=1564)
