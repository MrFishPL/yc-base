# Jocko Willink and Mike Sarraille - Helping Veterans Transition into the Private Sector — Compiled Truth

Former Navy SEAL Jocko Willink and Mike Sarraille discuss their veteran-placement venture Echelon Front Overwatch and draw leadership lessons from the military that apply to hiring, managing, and scaling startup teams.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Echelon Front Overwatch vets special-operations veterans before placing them, because like any group the military has a bell curve of performers and clients need only the high end [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 13:09](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=789) and [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 23:27](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=1407).
- Since it's inherently hard to judge people from an interview, the recommended best practice for hiring (veterans or anyone) is to bring them on as a contractor for a trial period, such as a 90-day fellowship, before making a final offer [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 28:04](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=1684) and [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 29:23](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=1763).
- Good leadership requires balancing "dichotomies" — e.g., being too close to your people versus too distant, or micromanaging versus giving too little direction — and both extremes are wrong [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 30:51](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=1851).
- As a team scales from 10 to 100+ people, a leader must set up culture and "commander's intent" strongly enough that people can operate without direct oversight, because no leader has the cognitive capacity to personally control every decision at scale [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 48:27](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=2907).
- Decentralized command — letting frontline people who are closest to the problem make the decision — is undermined mainly by ego, and leaders should feel happy rather than threatened when a subordinate proposes a better plan [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 44:38](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=2678) and [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 45:51](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=2751).
- Achieving anything significant is unlikely solo; founders should find co-founders who complement their strengths and weaknesses (e.g., a big-idea person paired with a strong executor) [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 1:00:11](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=3611) and [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 1:01:13](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=3673).
- Solo founders risk becoming emotionally attached to their own plan and stop listening to their customers, similar to how the military says "the enemy has a vote" in every plan [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 1:02:10](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=3730) and [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 1:02:45](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=3765).
- Emotional detachment is a critical leadership skill: recognize signs of getting emotional (raised voice, aggressively typing an email) and step back, since anger is usually rooted in one's own ego being challenged [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 1:04:05](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=3845) and [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 1:05:05](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=3905).
- Building a company culture where even the newest hire is expected to lead produces high performers, illustrated by how brand-new SEALs on their first deployment in Task Unit Bruiser became standout performers because the unit's culture rewarded initiative from day one [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 46:56](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=2816) and [jocko-willink-and-mike-sarraille-helping-veterans-transition @ 47:18](https://youtu.be/NlhujkRroJY?t=2838).
