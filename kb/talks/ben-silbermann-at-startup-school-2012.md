# Ben Silbermann at Startup School 2012 — Compiled Truth

Ben Silbermann traces Pinterest's slow, uncertain path from a failed iPhone shopping app to a niche visual product that took years and a shift from broad-feature ambition to a single, polished idea before it found its people.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Four months after launch Pinterest had only about 3,000 registered users, illustrating that building something worthwhile can take much longer than the famous fast-growth stories suggest [ben-silbermann-at-startup-school-2012 @ 02:17](https://youtu.be/KYQHPHYs2Os?t=137)
- His first startup, Tote (an iPhone shopping catalog), stalled because the team crammed in too many ambitious features — offline caching, payments — instead of shipping one focused thing [ben-silbermann-at-startup-school-2012 @ 07:07](https://youtu.be/KYQHPHYs2Os?t=427)
- In 2008, as two non-technical co-founders, they heard every investor rejection in the book, including "call me back in a few months" and "who else is in" [ben-silbermann-at-startup-school-2012 @ 07:52](https://youtu.be/KYQHPHYs2Os?t=472)
- Lesson from fundraising: investors are just people subject to ordinary biases (even a tray of free cookies pulled a room's attention away from his pitch), and their opinions, however credentialed, can be wrong [ben-silbermann-at-startup-school-2012 @ 09:35](https://youtu.be/KYQHPHYs2Os?t=575)
- His advice on shipping: launch when you have one thing you're proud of, even if narrow — for Pinterest that meant making the visual collections look genuinely cool before anything else [ben-silbermann-at-startup-school-2012 @ 13:10](https://youtu.be/KYQHPHYs2Os?t=790)
- Rather than change the product when initial response was near-zero, he focused on finding more people like the few who already loved it, using in-person meetups and a "Pin It Forward" chain-letter campaign [ben-silbermann-at-startup-school-2012 @ 14:57](https://youtu.be/KYQHPHYs2Os?t=897)
- Pinterest's distribution problem turned out to be a community problem, not an engineering problem, while building the first compelling version was fundamentally a design problem [ben-silbermann-at-startup-school-2012 @ 18:32](https://youtu.be/KYQHPHYs2Os?t=1112)
- Silicon Valley investors and press read the same articles as everyone else and were skeptical of Pinterest because it wasn't real-time or feed-based like Twitter and Facebook at the time [ben-silbermann-at-startup-school-2012 @ 17:35](https://youtu.be/KYQHPHYs2Os?t=1055)
- Silbermann's closing advice: build something you believe in because a startup is a multi-year journey, and don't let anyone talk you out of your dream [ben-silbermann-at-startup-school-2012 @ 22:07](https://youtu.be/KYQHPHYs2Os?t=1327)
