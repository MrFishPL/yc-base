# Why Two IIT Engineers Turned Down $550K Jobs To Build A Startup — Compiled Truth

Varun, co-founder of Giga ML (AI agents for customer support), recounts pivoting from an EdTech YC application through LLM fine-tuning to winning enterprise contracts like Zepto and DoorDash, and shares lessons on conviction, pricing, and hiring.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Harj rejected their original EdTech idea outright in the YC interview and told them to pick something else because they were strong engineers, which the founders initially thought meant they'd been rejected [why-two-iit-engineers-turned-down-550k-jobs-to-build-a-start @ 03:38](https://youtu.be/2Ap1dnv-GXA?t=218)
- After talking to Coursera's COO and other EdTech operators who all said it was a bad idea, the founders pivoted about a month into the batch to LLM fine-tuning/caching, which led to open-sourcing models and topping Hugging Face benchmarks [why-two-iit-engineers-turned-down-550k-jobs-to-build-a-start @ 06:31](https://youtu.be/2Ap1dnv-GXA?t=391)
- Fine-tuning turned out to be a bad market since it only helps with cost/speed or requires a hard enterprise sales process, so the team pivoted again into customer support after noticing that was one of only two use cases (with coding) growing well among their fine-tuning customers [why-two-iit-engineers-turned-down-550k-jobs-to-build-a-start @ 07:27](https://youtu.be/2Ap1dnv-GXA?t=447)
- The team didn't know Sierra existed when they started on Zepto, and deliberately didn't worry about well-funded competitors, instead focusing entirely on whether the customer would pay and whether they could deliver value [why-two-iit-engineers-turned-down-550k-jobs-to-build-a-start @ 08:38](https://youtu.be/2Ap1dnv-GXA?t=518)
- An eight-person team won DoorDash's support contract against a 400-person well-funded competitor, largely on the strength of a clean three-month pilot and the inherent trust conferred by both companies being YC alumni [why-two-iit-engineers-turned-down-550k-jobs-to-build-a-start @ 09:31](https://youtu.be/2Ap1dnv-GXA?t=571)
- Their advice to early founders is to always validate that a customer will actually pay before building, since "it's never about the idea, it's about if somebody is willing to pay you money for it" [why-two-iit-engineers-turned-down-550k-jobs-to-build-a-start @ 14:57](https://youtu.be/2Ap1dnv-GXA?t=897)
- They now believe product, not sales, is the most important lever for AI companies, citing that Anthropic and OpenAI don't even pay sales commissions, correcting an earlier internal debate where Varun overweighted sales [why-two-iit-engineers-turned-down-550k-jobs-to-build-a-start @ 22:29](https://youtu.be/2Ap1dnv-GXA?t=1349)
- In interviews, they ask candidates to write code with AI assistance and then remove the tool and have them modify the code without it, specifically to confirm the candidate still understands how the code works [why-two-iit-engineers-turned-down-550k-jobs-to-build-a-start @ 20:32](https://youtu.be/2Ap1dnv-GXA?t=1232)
- Coding agents let the company operate with roughly six to seven times fewer engineers than it would otherwise need, because avoiding context-switching and having one person own a feature ships faster than splitting work across many people [why-two-iit-engineers-turned-down-550k-jobs-to-build-a-start @ 19:43](https://youtu.be/2Ap1dnv-GXA?t=1183)
- Varun frames "burning the boats" (turning down job offers and acquisition offers) as what made the company's success feel real and forced them to make things work [why-two-iit-engineers-turned-down-550k-jobs-to-build-a-start @ 23:22](https://youtu.be/2Ap1dnv-GXA?t=1402)
