# How To NOT Get Screwed As A Software Engineer — Compiled Truth

Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel lay out self-diagnostic questions for technical co-founders, engineers, and interns to tell whether they are being fairly treated on equity, decision-making power, and effort parity, or being quietly exploited.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Roughly equal equity between co-founders is the healthy pattern, and a non-technical founder taking 90% while the technical co-founder gets 10% or less with no real justification is a common anti-pattern seen in YC applications [how-to-not-get-screwed-as-a-software-engineer @ 02:45](https://youtu.be/fcfVjd_oV1I?t=165)
- Early technical employees often get hosed by receiving only 1% or less of the company despite doing the work of an equal technical co-founder [how-to-not-get-screwed-as-a-software-engineer @ 04:21](https://youtu.be/fcfVjd_oV1I?t=261)
- A good self-test for an essential engineer at a scaling company is whether a successful IPO or acquisition would actually change their life financially, not just earn them a pat on the back [how-to-not-get-screwed-as-a-software-engineer @ 04:59](https://youtu.be/fcfVjd_oV1I?t=299)
- A warning sign of exploitation is when all company decisions happen in meetings the technical person isn't invited to, reducing them to a "robot to write code" [how-to-not-get-screwed-as-a-software-engineer @ 06:59](https://youtu.be/fcfVjd_oV1I?t=419)
- If the technical co-founder is grinding constantly while business-side counterparts doing fundraising and sales are not putting in equivalent effort, that imbalance is a major vector for exploitation [how-to-not-get-screwed-as-a-software-engineer @ 07:38](https://youtu.be/fcfVjd_oV1I?t=458)
- If a launch is clearly bombing and the technical person who reads the analytics is told to "stop asking questions" by the business side, that's a red flag pattern akin to gaslighting [how-to-not-get-screwed-as-a-software-engineer @ 09:06](https://youtu.be/fcfVjd_oV1I?t=546)
- Good signs include feeling like you're getting the best risk-reward deal available to you, learning faster than peers at big tech, and colleagues being just as invested as you are [how-to-not-get-screwed-as-a-software-engineer @ 10:40](https://youtu.be/fcfVjd_oV1I?t=640)
- If a team was fully upfront that the job would be extremely hard and it turns out that way, that's honest expectation-setting rather than exploitation [how-to-not-get-screwed-as-a-software-engineer @ 12:28](https://youtu.be/fcfVjd_oV1I?t=748)
- If you have a seat at the table with equal equity and things aren't working, you can't just blame the business co-founder since you share responsibility for outcomes [how-to-not-get-screwed-as-a-software-engineer @ 11:51](https://youtu.be/fcfVjd_oV1I?t=711)
- To fix a bad situation, ask for a seat at the table directly, take ownership of problems to earn more decision-making power, and be willing to take a step backward in title or scope to move to a better long-term position [how-to-not-get-screwed-as-a-software-engineer @ 13:39](https://youtu.be/fcfVjd_oV1I?t=819)
