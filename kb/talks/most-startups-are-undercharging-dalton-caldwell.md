# Most Startups Are Undercharging - Dalton Caldwell — Compiled Truth

Dalton Caldwell argues that most startups drastically underprice their product out of misplaced fear, and that charging a premium — not a discount — is a much better signal of real product-market fit.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Most startups charge only a fraction — sometimes 1/10th or 1/100th — of what they could actually charge for their product [most-startups-are-undercharging-dalton-caldwell @ 00:00](https://youtu.be/RxjZbmr1d1w?t=0)
- There's a false idea floating around that investors want founders to not charge, or to charge very little; in reality YC's first advice is often to raise prices dramatically and quickly [most-startups-are-undercharging-dalton-caldwell @ 00:23](https://youtu.be/RxjZbmr1d1w?t=23)
- Startups that pitch "we win because we're cheaper" get dangerously misleading signal, since they may just be attracting price-sensitive customers rather than proving real demand [most-startups-are-undercharging-dalton-caldwell @ 00:46](https://youtu.be/RxjZbmr1d1w?t=46)
- Successful products typically charge a premium rather than a discount, because they solve such a large problem that customers happily pay more than alternatives [most-startups-are-undercharging-dalton-caldwell @ 01:27](https://youtu.be/RxjZbmr1d1w?t=87)
- Breakout companies like Instacart, DoorDash, Airbnb, and Dropbox launched as expensive options, not as cheaper substitutes for existing solutions [most-startups-are-undercharging-dalton-caldwell @ 01:45](https://youtu.be/RxjZbmr1d1w?t=105)
- Zapier proved a paid product can beat a free direct substitute: it charged money while IFTTT, a near-identical alternative, was free [most-startups-are-undercharging-dalton-caldwell @ 02:10](https://youtu.be/RxjZbmr1d1w?t=130)
