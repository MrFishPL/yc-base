# Kevin Hale - How to Evaluate Startup Ideas — Compiled Truth

Kevin Hale frames a startup idea as a hypothesis with three parts — problem, solution, and unfair-advantage insight — and gives concrete criteria investors use to judge whether that hypothesis points to fast growth.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- YC funds many companies at the pure idea stage (e.g. Zenefits, Reddit, Wufoo), so lack of traction is not disqualifying [kevin-hale-how-to-evaluate-startup-ideas @ 01:36](https://youtu.be/DOtCl5PU8F0?t=96)
- YC's definition of a startup is a company designed to grow very quickly; otherwise it's just a normal small business [kevin-hale-how-to-evaluate-startup-ideas @ 02:59](https://youtu.be/DOtCl5PU8F0?t=179)
- A startup idea is a hypothesis composed of three parts: the problem (initial conditions), the solution (the experiment), and the insight (why the experiment will succeed) [kevin-hale-how-to-evaluate-startup-ideas @ 06:09](https://youtu.be/DOtCl5PU8F0?t=369)
- Good problems are popular, growing, urgent, expensive, mandatory, and frequent — and BJ Fogg's model says behavior change needs motivation, ability, and a trigger all at once [kevin-hale-how-to-evaluate-startup-ideas @ 06:50](https://youtu.be/DOtCl5PU8F0?t=410)
- Avoid "SISP" (solution in search of a problem) — starting with an exciting technology and then shoehorning a problem onto it is much less efficient than starting with the problem [kevin-hale-how-to-evaluate-startup-ideas @ 10:55](https://youtu.be/DOtCl5PU8F0?t=655)
- There are five types of unfair advantage — founder expertise, a fast-growing market, a 10x-better product, free/organic acquisition, and monopoly-like network effects — and great companies usually need at least one [kevin-hale-how-to-evaluate-startup-ideas @ 13:32](https://youtu.be/DOtCl5PU8F0?t=812)
- If paid acquisition is a company's only growth channel, that advantage is discounted heavily because competitors can bid it away once a company gets big [kevin-hale-how-to-evaluate-startup-ideas @ 16:23](https://youtu.be/DOtCl5PU8F0?t=983)
- Investors distinguish "threshold beliefs" (default requirements like being able to build the product) from the "miracle belief" that actually determines whether a bet pays off [kevin-hale-how-to-evaluate-startup-ideas @ 18:31](https://youtu.be/DOtCl5PU8F0?t=1111)
- YC itself is used as a worked example: its unfair advantages were exceptional founders, a growing tech market, a differentiated open-application product, and Paul Graham's existing audience of hackers from his essays [kevin-hale-how-to-evaluate-startup-ideas @ 20:04](https://youtu.be/DOtCl5PU8F0?t=1204)
- The exercise ends with founders auditing their own problem/solution/insight to find gaps and decide what they still need to prove [kevin-hale-how-to-evaluate-startup-ideas @ 25:27](https://youtu.be/DOtCl5PU8F0?t=1527)
