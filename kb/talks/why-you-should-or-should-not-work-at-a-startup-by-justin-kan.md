# Why You Should or Should Not Work at a Startup by Justin Kan — Compiled Truth

Justin Kan lays out honest reasons not to join a startup (bad management, low odds of getting rich, no career stability) alongside three reasons you should — unqualified-for-your-level opportunities, a gateway to founding your own company, and maximized speed of learning.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Startup management is generally bad; YC companies were jokingly either "rocket ships with bad management" or "other companies with bad management," so employees often must actively demand mentorship [why-you-should-or-should-not-work-at-a-startup-by-justin-kan @ 01:33](https://youtu.be/YzyatiQrQlQ?t=93)
- Joining a startup is statistically unlikely to make you rich, and people now seeking career pathing, stability, and a five-year plan should instead go work at a big company like Facebook [why-you-should-or-should-not-work-at-a-startup-by-justin-kan @ 02:21](https://youtu.be/YzyatiQrQlQ?t=141)
- Startups give people access to jobs they are wildly unqualified for; an early Justin.tv hire ended up running the company's entire Rails backend for a top-20 site within a year of joining [why-you-should-or-should-not-work-at-a-startup-by-justin-kan @ 03:42](https://youtu.be/YzyatiQrQlQ?t=222)
- Working at a startup is a good gateway to starting your own; an employee at his company Exec later became a founder himself, went through YC, and built the startup Shogun [why-you-should-or-should-not-work-at-a-startup-by-justin-kan @ 05:44](https://youtu.be/YzyatiQrQlQ?t=344)
- He cites "you are the average of your five closest friends" (a line from Twitch co-founder Emmett) as a reason to put yourself around the kind of people you want to become [why-you-should-or-should-not-work-at-a-startup-by-justin-kan @ 06:06](https://youtu.be/YzyatiQrQlQ?t=366)
- The most important reason to join a startup is to maximize your own speed of learning, illustrated by Cruise co-founder Kyle Vogt, who joined Justin.tv as an MIT hardware hacker and ended up architecting a live-video system that became the fourth-largest bandwidth consumer in North America by the time Twitch sold to Amazon [why-you-should-or-should-not-work-at-a-startup-by-justin-kan @ 07:20](https://youtu.be/YzyatiQrQlQ?t=440)
- Learning happens even when a startup is failing: his brother Daniel learned deal negotiation by closing a distressed sale of home-cleaning startup Exec to Handy, and later applied those lessons as a Cruise co-founder before it sold to GM for a billion dollars [why-you-should-or-should-not-work-at-a-startup-by-justin-kan @ 12:26](https://youtu.be/YzyatiQrQlQ?t=746)
- He closes with a Paul Buchheit line he repeats to every batch: "it's not your y-intercept, it's your slope that's important," meaning founders and employees should optimize for their personal rate of growth [why-you-should-or-should-not-work-at-a-startup-by-justin-kan @ 14:41](https://youtu.be/YzyatiQrQlQ?t=881)
