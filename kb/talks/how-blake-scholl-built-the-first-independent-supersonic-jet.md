# How Blake Scholl Built The First Independent Supersonic Jet — Compiled Truth

Boom Supersonic founder Blake Scholl explains how he left a comfortable tech career to methodically prove out supersonic passenger flight from first principles, arguing ambitious founders should chase what inspires them rather than what they already know.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Chasing a mobile e-commerce startup because it matched his existing skills gave Scholl a sense of competence but no sense of purpose or drive [how-blake-scholl-built-the-first-independent-supersonic-jet @ 03:46](https://youtu.be/sOFmYwYa9Pk?t=226).
- Scholl believes skills and knowledge are variable and learnable, but passions are not, so founders should go after what inspires them and build the skills afterward [how-blake-scholl-built-the-first-independent-supersonic-jet @ 04:23](https://youtu.be/sOFmYwYa9Pk?t=263).
- Concord failed for economic reasons, not technical ones — a $20,000 ticket and half-empty flights — which reframed the problem as needing better fundamental airplane efficiency, not just speed [how-blake-scholl-built-the-first-independent-supersonic-jet @ 05:49](https://youtu.be/sOFmYwYa9Pk?t=349).
- Scholl built a spreadsheet model predicting airplane performance from just aerodynamic efficiency, propulsive efficiency, structural efficiency, and Mach number, and a Stanford professor told him his assumptions were actually conservative [how-blake-scholl-built-the-first-independent-supersonic-jet @ 06:24](https://youtu.be/sOFmYwYa9Pk?t=384).
- With zero industry contacts, Scholl networked into aerospace by asking every contact "who would your top five people be" regardless of availability, which recursively led him to the best people in the field [how-blake-scholl-built-the-first-independent-supersonic-jet @ 08:38](https://youtu.be/sOFmYwYa9Pk?t=518).
- He attributes widespread industry inaction on supersonic flight to a bystander effect, where people gave qualitative answers ("the market's too small," "sonic booms are too loud") to what were actually quantitative questions [how-blake-scholl-built-the-first-independent-supersonic-jet @ 09:25](https://youtu.be/sOFmYwYa9Pk?t=565).
- Boom intentionally recruited early-career engineers from places like SpaceX and Boeing before they were "corrupted" by big aerospace habits, while keeping a few veterans on speed dial to prevent foolish mistakes [how-blake-scholl-built-the-first-independent-supersonic-jet @ 11:02](https://youtu.be/sOFmYwYa9Pk?t=662).
- The XB-1 prototype was built by a core team of about 50 people, showing small high-caliber teams can achieve what big teams can't [how-blake-scholl-built-the-first-independent-supersonic-jet @ 10:41](https://youtu.be/sOFmYwYa9Pk?t=641).
- Scholl says the belief that keeps him from giving up is conviction in the cause itself — that supersonic flight is important — rather than confidence in any given day's problem [how-blake-scholl-built-the-first-independent-supersonic-jet @ 12:27](https://youtu.be/sOFmYwYa9Pk?t=747).
- His conclusion for founders is that ambitious people will run at their personal red line regardless of company size, so they may as well work on something big [how-blake-scholl-built-the-first-independent-supersonic-jet @ 13:03](https://youtu.be/sOFmYwYa9Pk?t=783).
