# The Number One Goal is Getting Started - Avni Patel Thompson of Poppy — Compiled Truth

Avni Patel Thompson recounts how her Procter & Gamble consumer-insight background, a failed subscription-box startup, and a scrappy four-week manual test with SMS and a spreadsheet led to Poppy, and argues founders must simply start rather than wait for a perfect plan.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- At Procter & Gamble she learned to watch how consumers actually behaved (like a woman cutting up a child-resistant pill pack) rather than trust what they said in focus groups, which shaped her belief in talking to real users [the-number-one-goal-is-getting-started-avni-patel-thompson-o @ 01:30](https://youtu.be/EOw6izSfzSA?t=90).
- Her first startup, a cultural-subscription box, failed after a year and $20,000 of personal money because she over-invested in a business plan and product before validating demand [the-number-one-goal-is-getting-started-avni-patel-thompson-o @ 18:01](https://youtu.be/EOw6izSfzSA?t=1081).
- She warns that friends and strangers saying "I love your idea" is not validation — the only real validation is people paying money, which is why she doesn't believe in friends-and-family discounts [the-number-one-goal-is-getting-started-avni-patel-thompson-o @ 17:13](https://youtu.be/EOw6izSfzSA?t=1033).
- With $200 left, she launched Poppy as a scrappy four-week test using Typeform, Squarespace, Excel as a database, Google Calendar as a scheduler, and her personal cell phone for SMS bookings [the-number-one-goal-is-getting-started-avni-patel-thompson-o @ 24:39](https://youtu.be/EOw6izSfzSA?t=1479).
- She got her first booking the day she emailed 15 parents, then tracked consistent week-over-week growth (4, then 6, then more) as the signal that something was working [the-number-one-goal-is-getting-started-avni-patel-thompson-o @ 25:33](https://youtu.be/EOw6izSfzSA?t=1533).
- The four-week test construct is especially useful for underrepresented founders facing extra hurdles, because it forces a fast, bounded way to know whether to commit or move on [the-number-one-goal-is-getting-started-avni-patel-thompson-o @ 26:34](https://youtu.be/EOw6izSfzSA?t=1594).
- She measures product-market fit by retention and by whether the product solves a problem so completely that users "have no choice but to come to you," citing a parent who said losing access to Poppy would make their life much worse [the-number-one-goal-is-getting-started-avni-patel-thompson-o @ 35:39](https://youtu.be/EOw6izSfzSA?t=2139).
- She credits growth itself with solving multiple problems at once: it helped her find her CTO/co-founder and helped her raise money, because visible traction attracts the right people [the-number-one-goal-is-getting-started-avni-patel-thompson-o @ 34:47](https://youtu.be/EOw6izSfzSA?t=2087).
- She applied to YC three times before getting in, framing persistence through investor and consumer rejection as something most founders quietly go through [the-number-one-goal-is-getting-started-avni-patel-thompson-o @ 31:12](https://youtu.be/EOw6izSfzSA?t=1872).
- Her closing advice to founders (especially women, who she says tend toward perfectionism) is to give yourself a small tangible target, like 200 paying users, and to take "big swings" sized to what a venture-backed outcome actually requires [the-number-one-goal-is-getting-started-avni-patel-thompson-o @ 44:06](https://youtu.be/EOw6izSfzSA?t=2646).
