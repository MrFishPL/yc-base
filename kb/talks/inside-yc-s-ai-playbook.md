# Inside YC's AI Playbook — Compiled Truth

YC partner Pete Koomen describes how YC rebuilt itself internally as an AI-native organization by putting all its data in one database, building a shared agent/tool registry, and defaulting to open, recorded, broadcast agent conversations.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- The original impetus for YC's internal agent infrastructure was giving the finance team direct control of their own workflows via prompts instead of routing every change through software engineers [inside-yc-s-ai-playbook @ 04:21](https://youtu.be/B246K_G7mHU?t=261)
- Giving agents unrestricted read-only SQL access to YC's single production Postgres database (rather than narrowly scoped tools) was the first real unlock, because it let agents answer arbitrary questions across every table [inside-yc-s-ai-playbook @ 06:13](https://youtu.be/B246K_G7mHU?t=373)
- Removing the friction of asking questions dramatically increased both the number and complexity of questions people dared to ask, a Jevons paradox effect versus the old world of waiting on a data science team's backlog [inside-yc-s-ai-playbook @ 08:45](https://youtu.be/B246K_G7mHU?t=525)
- YC's internal tool registry started with about 20 tools and has grown to more than 350 as every team adds its own, turning agents into something genuinely useful for day-to-day work [inside-yc-s-ai-playbook @ 14:16](https://youtu.be/B246K_G7mHU?t=856)
- A partner-written "two sentence description" skill got noticeably better after other partners fed it transcripts of a group office-hours coaching session, effectively baking their collective expertise into the skill [inside-yc-s-ai-playbook @ 22:35](https://youtu.be/B246K_G7mHU?t=1355)
- By default, every agent conversation at YC is globally viewable by employees and broadcast to a Slack channel, which let people learn by watching how others used the tools and kept private information private through social norms rather than restriction [inside-yc-s-ai-playbook @ 27:29](https://youtu.be/B246K_G7mHU?t=1649)
- Truly agentic, superintelligent organizations require being relatively egalitarian and trust-by-default, traits most organizations lack but that work best in small, aligned, high-trust startup environments [inside-yc-s-ai-playbook @ 29:02](https://youtu.be/B246K_G7mHU?t=1742)
- This infrastructure has raised the floor for new employees, letting them apprentice by AI on how the best people in the org operate instead of needing months to ramp up or bother busy senior staff [inside-yc-s-ai-playbook @ 31:02](https://youtu.be/B246K_G7mHU?t=1862)
- Koomen's "Horseless Carriages" essay argues AI's potential is to shift control of software from developer to user, and that AI-native software will look like an agent wrapping deterministic tools rather than deterministic software wrapping a hidden AI feature [inside-yc-s-ai-playbook @ 33:12](https://youtu.be/B246K_G7mHU?t=1992)
- The choice ahead is between a centralized future where a few companies control AI and lock down your own prompts (like Gmail's fixed AI email writer) versus a decentralized "Apple One moment" where individuals can run and modify their own agents and models [inside-yc-s-ai-playbook @ 40:54](https://youtu.be/B246K_G7mHU?t=2454)
