# Mike Knoop on Product and Design Processes for Remote Teams with Kevin Hale — Compiled Truth

Zapier co-founder Mike Knoop explains how Zapier became fully remote almost by necessity, and the explicit communication norms, OKR structure, and hiring filters (default-to-action, strong written communication) it built to make remote work scale to 200 people.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Zapier's decision to build an open integration platform in 2012, rather than build all integrations itself, was driven by launch-day chat logs full of requests for unsupported apps — it became a platform out of necessity, not strategy [mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams- @ 04:47](https://youtu.be/zF84IMiSP7I?t=287)
- Early platform partners were pitched on retention value, not distribution: integrating with Zapier let them say "yes" to customer integration requests instead of leaving them on a backlog [mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams- @ 06:57](https://youtu.be/zF84IMiSP7I?t=417)
- Zapier validated demand before building by launching landing pages for hundreds of prospective app integrations with email capture, building only ~50-60 integrations themselves before launch [mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams- @ 08:19](https://youtu.be/zF84IMiSP7I?t=499)
- Zapier was rejected by YC the first time with just a hackathon prototype and no traction, but got in the second time after validating willingness to pay (charged early users, dropped price to $5, had a few hundred paying users) [mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams- @ 11:30](https://youtu.be/zF84IMiSP7I?t=690)
- Going remote started as an accident of hiring: their first support hire wouldn't move to the Bay Area, so they let him work from Chicago, testing whether the founders' prior remote experience at their old jobs could extend to a real hire [mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams- @ 14:13](https://youtu.be/zF84IMiSP7I?t=853)
- Zapier stayed remote largely out of frugality and founder preference for autonomy, not ideology — profitability let them dismiss investor and VC pressure to "mature" into an office [mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams- @ 19:05](https://youtu.be/zF84IMiSP7I?t=1145)
- The core hiring filter for remote fit is "default to action": whether a candidate has a track record of making decisions without waiting for consensus or explicit instruction [mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams- @ 22:15](https://youtu.be/zF84IMiSP7I?t=1335)
- Zapier built explicit rules for "raising communication bandwidth" — start with async text, escalate to a video call when a Slack thread runs long (e.g., stop debating after 15 minutes and jump on a call) [mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams- @ 27:04](https://youtu.be/zF84IMiSP7I?t=1624)
- Product/design/engineering teams share OKRs (rather than individual goals) to prevent an "us vs. them" mentality between disciplines as the org scales [mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams- @ 32:19](https://youtu.be/zF84IMiSP7I?t=1939)
- Zapier built an internal "async" blog as a slower, curated alternative to Slack for weekly written updates, since Slack alone became overwhelming as headcount grew [mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams- @ 41:02](https://youtu.be/zF84IMiSP7I?t=2462)
- Remote design collaboration worked via structured exercises like the "nine-box" rapid-sketch exercise over Zoom, and a Tuesday/Thursday design review cadence that forced a 48-hour iteration cycle [mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams- @ 52:13](https://youtu.be/zF84IMiSP7I?t=3133)
