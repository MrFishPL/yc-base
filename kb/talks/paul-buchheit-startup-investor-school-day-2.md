# Paul Buchheit - Startup Investor School Day 2 — Compiled Truth

Paul Buchheit reviews years of angel investing to distill the patterns behind his best and worst bets, arguing that great founders matter more than ideas, markets, or price.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Good startup ideas often look bad at first, because if an opportunity were obviously good, a big company with more resources would already be pursuing it [paul-buchheit-startup-investor-school-day-2 @ 04:35](https://youtu.be/bSbPXrQ9Wf8?t=275)
- The single most important investing lesson is to "find your betters" — fund founders who are smarter and more insightful than you, and outsource the thinking on idea and market to them [paul-buchheit-startup-investor-school-day-2 @ 17:02](https://youtu.be/bSbPXrQ9Wf8?t=1022)
- Meraki impressed him by building and shipping hardware with essentially no outside funding, while Juicero is the cautionary opposite: $100M spent on a juice-bag squeezer without ever talking to a customer [paul-buchheit-startup-investor-school-day-2 @ 19:21](https://youtu.be/bSbPXrQ9Wf8?t=1161)
- Moving fast — accomplishing a lot with a little, like Kyle Vogt writing a full streaming media server over a weekend — is one of the strongest predictors of startup success [paul-buchheit-startup-investor-school-day-2 @ 35:19](https://youtu.be/bSbPXrQ9Wf8?t=2119)
- Skipping an investor meeting with Drew Houston (Dropbox) because a prior meeting ran long cost him roughly a 1,000x return, illustrating why founders deserve prompt, reliable investor behavior [paul-buchheit-startup-investor-school-day-2 @ 26:24](https://youtu.be/bSbPXrQ9Wf8?t=1584)
- Moving slowly on a decision creates adverse selection: the companies you can "string along" for months of meetings usually aren't the best ones, so it's better to decide quickly, as with Airbnb [paul-buchheit-startup-investor-school-day-2 @ 28:31](https://youtu.be/bSbPXrQ9Wf8?t=1711)
- There is no such thing as value investing in seed-stage startups — chasing a "good deal" on price has never generated him a return, and being deterred by a high price has always been a regret [paul-buchheit-startup-investor-school-day-2 @ 29:39](https://youtu.be/bSbPXrQ9Wf8?t=1779)
- Too much money is a liability for founders because it lets them insulate themselves from reality instead of being forced to talk to customers, as happened with Juicero [paul-buchheit-startup-investor-school-day-2 @ 51:14](https://youtu.be/bSbPXrQ9Wf8?t=3074)
- Key founder traits to look for are clear concise communication, moving fast, accomplishing a lot with little, pursuing implausibly ambitious or "toy-like" ideas, being a talent magnet, making something people want, and above all determination [paul-buchheit-startup-investor-school-day-2 @ 33:31](https://youtu.be/bSbPXrQ9Wf8?t=2011)
- Founders who have never failed (perfect grades, perfect resumes) are riskier because they're more likely to take an easy early exit rather than push through the inevitable hardship of building a company [paul-buchheit-startup-investor-school-day-2 @ 40:09](https://youtu.be/bSbPXrQ9Wf8?t=2409)
- He deliberately avoids over-learning from past mistakes, since drawing overly specific lessons (e.g. "never invest in streaming video again") can cause you to miss the next great outlier [paul-buchheit-startup-investor-school-day-2 @ 49:00](https://youtu.be/bSbPXrQ9Wf8?t=2940)
