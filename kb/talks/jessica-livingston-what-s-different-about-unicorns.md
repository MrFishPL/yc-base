# Jessica Livingston - What's Different about Unicorns — Compiled Truth

Jessica Livingston lays out nine traits that separate merely successful startups from unicorns, arguing that huge outcomes come from founders who stumble into an unpromising-seeming idea, are fanatically committed to it, and grow into whatever the company needs.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Unicorn founders are lucky, and the luck that matters most is timing — having the right idea at the right time, which is largely out of a founder's control [jessica-livingston-what-s-different-about-unicorns @ 03:14](https://youtu.be/Ygr3rx4hSsc?t=194)
- The best ideas aren't deliberate; they grow organically out of founders' own lives rather than being invented on purpose [jessica-livingston-what-s-different-about-unicorns @ 03:42](https://youtu.be/Ygr3rx4hSsc?t=222)
- Founders of the biggest startups are never mainly in it for money or to seem cool — they're fanatically interested in what the company does, which makes them work harder, think longer-term, and resist acquisition offers [jessica-livingston-what-s-different-about-unicorns @ 04:10](https://youtu.be/Ygr3rx4hSsc?t=250)
- YC's advice is not to try to hit a big market early on since market size is impossible to predict — instead work on something you yourself want and hope there are lots more people like you [jessica-livingston-what-s-different-about-unicorns @ 05:54](https://youtu.be/Ygr3rx4hSsc?t=354)
- Almost all the really big startup ideas seemed dubious or like bad ideas at first, including Airbnb, which Livingston herself didn't think much of when she first judged it [jessica-livingston-what-s-different-about-unicorns @ 07:07](https://youtu.be/Ygr3rx4hSsc?t=427)
- Working on an idea that looks bad to most people requires being independent-minded and not caring what other people think — every huge-startup founder she's seen is a maverick, not a conformist [jessica-livingston-what-s-different-about-unicorns @ 07:30](https://youtu.be/Ygr3rx4hSsc?t=450)
- As an initially small idea blossoms into a terrifyingly big one, most people react with fear, but the founders who build unicorns are more excited than afraid of that scale [jessica-livingston-what-s-different-about-unicorns @ 08:24](https://youtu.be/Ygr3rx4hSsc?t=504)
- All unicorn founders have exceptional drive, though it's hard to spot in advance since drive gets suppressed by authority in school or a job and only shows once someone starts a startup and faces an authority vacuum [jessica-livingston-what-s-different-about-unicorns @ 09:19](https://youtu.be/Ygr3rx4hSsc?t=559)
- In every huge startup, the company becomes at least one founder's life's work, which is why they'd never willingly sell or be acquired [jessica-livingston-what-s-different-about-unicorns @ 10:20](https://youtu.be/Ygr3rx4hSsc?t=620)
- As a startup gets really big, a founder who wants to keep running it has to become a manager — you don't need management ability initially, but you have to be able to learn it and probably even like it [jessica-livingston-what-s-different-about-unicorns @ 10:48](https://youtu.be/Ygr3rx4hSsc?t=648)
