# Michelle Zatlyn and Matthew Prince at Startup School SV 2014 — Compiled Truth

The Cloudflare co-founders reflect on five years of building the company, arguing that complementary co-founders, obsessing over unglamorous details, and choosing investors on trust matter more than any single silver-bullet tactic.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Matthew worries about the big dramatic "sharks" while Michelle worries about the small unglamorous "mosquitoes," and the mosquitoes end up mattering far more for survival [michelle-zatlyn-and-matthew-prince-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 03:58](https://youtu.be/l58rp7JoVYQ?t=238)
- Cloudflare shipped an embarrassingly early product to its first 10 friends, took all 10 of their sites offline, fixed the issues, and repeated until the product was solid [michelle-zatlyn-and-matthew-prince-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 05:13](https://youtu.be/l58rp7JoVYQ?t=313)
- With no money for servers, they emailed ~200 people in Project Honey Pot's community asking for donated hardware and got a 70% response rate, then drove around picking up machines while also collecting early product feedback [michelle-zatlyn-and-matthew-prince-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 06:29](https://youtu.be/l58rp7JoVYQ?t=389)
- Good co-founders cover a lot of different surface area with only a little overlap, share the same vision, and trust each other; if you're fighting over who does what, you probably have the wrong co-founder [michelle-zatlyn-and-matthew-prince-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 09:24](https://youtu.be/l58rp7JoVYQ?t=564)
- After an investor board member challenged their self-assigned VP titles, the founders dropped titles entirely, which selected for people who wanted to be there rather than a title [michelle-zatlyn-and-matthew-prince-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 13:49](https://youtu.be/l58rp7JoVYQ?t=829)
- Not being domain experts in web infrastructure was an advantage: they didn't know they were "supposed to" charge on bandwidth like every competitor, so they didn't, which helped their growth [michelle-zatlyn-and-matthew-prince-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 19:03](https://youtu.be/l58rp7JoVYQ?t=1143)
- A startup's greatest asset is momentum — making progress faster than incumbents, whether through product, community, or a scrappy team, is what compounds into a big company [michelle-zatlyn-and-matthew-prince-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 11:24](https://youtu.be/l58rp7JoVYQ?t=684)
- The litmus test for board members is whether you'd hesitate to call them in the absolute worst-case disaster scenario; if yes, they don't belong on the board [michelle-zatlyn-and-matthew-prince-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 24:13](https://youtu.be/l58rp7JoVYQ?t=1453)
- In their last fundraising round they took the lowest of the valuations offered (half the highest) in order to get one particular investor in the room, because aligned investors matter more than maximizing valuation [michelle-zatlyn-and-matthew-prince-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 25:13](https://youtu.be/l58rp7JoVYQ?t=1513)
- The average time to exit (acquisition or IPO) is about eight years, so founders should pick an idea they'd be willing to put blood, sweat, and tears into regardless of outcome [michelle-zatlyn-and-matthew-prince-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 22:41](https://youtu.be/l58rp7JoVYQ?t=1361)
