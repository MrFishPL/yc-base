# BillionToOne Is Solving One of Biotech's Hardest Problems — Compiled Truth

BillionToOne's founders built a next-generation DNA-detection platform by adding synthetic "counting template" DNA to samples before amplification, letting them cancel out PCR noise and find rare genetic signals; they used a deliberate three-step roadmap (prenatal testing, then late-stage cancer, then early-stage cancer screening) so each stage funded the next.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- The core technical insight is adding known synthetic DNA to a sample before PCR amplification so the resulting noise/bias can be measured and subtracted from the real signal, turning a hard biology problem into a math problem [billiontoone-is-solving-one-of-biotech-s-hardest-problems @ 03:15](https://youtu.be/kkv5rZhrLkc?t=195)
- They deliberately chose prenatal testing as their first, least capital-intensive product (their "minimal viable product") rather than starting with cancer, because it let them reach commercialization fast and fund harder problems later [billiontoone-is-solving-one-of-biotech-s-hardest-problems @ 13:29](https://youtu.be/kkv5rZhrLkc?t=809)
- Two months after launch they had only one physician sending one or two tests a week, which triggered an emergency response: hiring five sales reps within three weeks and training them over a weekend to get in front of patients and physicians [billiontoone-is-solving-one-of-biotech-s-hardest-problems @ 08:13](https://youtu.be/kkv5rZhrLkc?t=493)
- Their eventual sales breakthrough came from an inside-sales rep spending 30-45 minutes per patient on the phone teaching them about the test so patients could convince their own doctors to order it [billiontoone-is-solving-one-of-biotech-s-hardest-problems @ 09:11](https://youtu.be/kkv5rZhrLkc?t=551)
- Raising the first $300,000 after their fellowship took six months, arriving $10,000 at a time, and made them "very paranoid" about resource usage [billiontoone-is-solving-one-of-biotech-s-hardest-problems @ 07:33](https://youtu.be/kkv5rZhrLkc?t=453)
- They intentionally chose a resource-constrained, step-by-step path (rather than raising over a billion dollars up front) to eventually reach cancer screening, because as first-time founders they knew they could never raise that much without revenue [billiontoone-is-solving-one-of-biotech-s-hardest-problems @ 19:05](https://youtu.be/kkv5rZhrLkc?t=1145)
- They hire "interdisciplinary people" rather than building interdisciplinary teams, giving small research pods of a scientist plus two or three research associates end-to-end ownership of a product reporting directly to the co-founders to avoid bureaucracy [billiontoone-is-solving-one-of-biotech-s-hardest-problems @ 15:24](https://youtu.be/kkv5rZhrLkc?t=924)
- Their company culture line is "pressure is a privilege" — they tell recruits joining will likely be one of the hardest things they ever do, but something they'll be proud of [billiontoone-is-solving-one-of-biotech-s-hardest-problems @ 19:50](https://youtu.be/kkv5rZhrLkc?t=1190)
- Being resource-limited when they started actually helped them, since it forced the staged approach instead of trying to solve early cancer detection outright from day one [billiontoone-is-solving-one-of-biotech-s-hardest-problems @ 19:05](https://youtu.be/kkv5rZhrLkc?t=1145)
- A patient with metastatic colorectal cancer who had run out of options was found eligible for immunotherapy via their liquid biopsy test after standard tumor testing missed the relevant mutation, and went on to respond remarkably well [billiontoone-is-solving-one-of-biotech-s-hardest-problems @ 14:10](https://youtu.be/kkv5rZhrLkc?t=850)
