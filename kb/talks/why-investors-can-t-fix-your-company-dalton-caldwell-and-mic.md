# Why Investors Can't Fix Your Company – Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel — Compiled Truth

Different investor archetypes (finance people, big-company execs, non-tech entrepreneurs, junior investors, influencers, other founders, young scouts) each give advice shaped by their own background, and none of them can actually tell a founder exactly what to do to win — only the founder can take accountability for making it work.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Finance-background investors tend to push founders toward money-based solutions (raise more, spend more, ask for five-year financial projections) even pre-product-market-fit, when the real problem is usually that the product isn't good [why-investors-can-t-fix-your-company-dalton-caldwell-and-mic @ 01:49](https://youtu.be/UqKzpLqXuI0?t=109)
- Big-company-exec investors, who often only experienced companies at 100-1000+ employees, push founders to hire executives (VP of Engineering, marketing exec) before product-market-fit, when the real issue is usually the wrong person already in the seat, not a missing department [why-investors-can-t-fix-your-company-dalton-caldwell-and-mic @ 04:14](https://youtu.be/UqKzpLqXuI0?t=254)
- Failure for most YC companies isn't missing a Series A — it's getting zero real customers, a scale of failure that big-company-background investors often don't even conceive of [why-investors-can-t-fix-your-company-dalton-caldwell-and-mic @ 05:19](https://youtu.be/UqKzpLqXuI0?t=319)
- Successful entrepreneurs from non-tech industries (real estate, franchises) sometimes apply their industry's control-heavy investment norms to tech startups, expecting founders to be run like a franchise manager [why-investors-can-t-fix-your-company-dalton-caldwell-and-mic @ 08:12](https://youtu.be/UqKzpLqXuI0?t=492)
- Junior investors, whose careers depend on quick wins, tend to be overly optimistic and push founders to keep fundraising and scaling faster, because a markup on their investment is their personal KPI [why-investors-can-t-fix-your-company-dalton-caldwell-and-mic @ 09:49](https://youtu.be/UqKzpLqXuI0?t=589)
- Influencer investors understand distribution and often treat startup investments like ad deals, asking for advisor shares while overpromising distribution that rarely delivers as much as founders hope [why-investors-can-t-fix-your-company-dalton-caldwell-and-mic @ 12:10](https://youtu.be/UqKzpLqXuI0?t=730)
- Other founders on your cap table tend to give advice that is heavily autobiographical, projecting their own specific struggles (fundraising, distribution) onto your company [why-investors-can-t-fix-your-company-dalton-caldwell-and-mic @ 14:10](https://youtu.be/UqKzpLqXuI0?t=850)
- Even YC partners can over-recommend the lean-startup, talk-to-users, low-spend playbook as if it applies universally, when some of the best companies spent years building in a vacuum before finding product-market fit [why-investors-can-t-fix-your-company-dalton-caldwell-and-mic @ 17:33](https://youtu.be/UqKzpLqXuI0?t=1053)
- The best investor advice tends to simply and honestly name the problem (e.g., "your company sucks if you don't change stuff") rather than prescribe a solution, and it lands hardest when the advisor has no financial stake in the outcome [why-investors-can-t-fix-your-company-dalton-caldwell-and-mic @ 22:39](https://youtu.be/UqKzpLqXuI0?t=1359)
- Ultimately no investor can tell a founder exactly what to do to win — it's the founder's job to synthesize outside input while taking full personal accountability for making the company work [why-investors-can-t-fix-your-company-dalton-caldwell-and-mic @ 20:05](https://youtu.be/UqKzpLqXuI0?t=1205)
