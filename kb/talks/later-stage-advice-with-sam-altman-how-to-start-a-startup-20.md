# Later Stage Advice with Sam Altman — Compiled Truth

In the final lecture of the course, Sam Altman lists the things founders should deliberately ignore before product-market fit but must get right once a company scales past roughly 25 people, from management structure to HR, productivity, deal-making, and founder psychology.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Flat, no-management structure works fine up to ~20-25 employees but fails all at once past that; the fix is simple, not complex — every employee needs exactly one clear manager [later-stage-advice-with-sam-altman-how-to-start-a-startup-20 @ 01:14](https://youtu.be/d2GglPzOXfM?t=74)
- Around 25 employees, a founder's main job shifts permanently from building a great product to building a great company [later-stage-advice-with-sam-altman-how-to-start-a-startup-20 @ 03:25](https://youtu.be/d2GglPzOXfM?t=205)
- Four common founder-as-manager failure modes: being afraid to hire senior people, "hero mode" (working harder instead of delegating), bad delegation (making the decision yourself instead of trusting the employee to decide), and poor personal organization [later-stage-advice-with-sam-altman-how-to-start-a-startup-20 @ 03:44](https://youtu.be/d2GglPzOXfM?t=224)
- Founders should keep giving out significant equity to employees for years (3-5% of the company annually), even though investors will resist it as dilutive — the most successful companies do this [later-stage-advice-with-sam-altman-how-to-start-a-startup-20 @ 10:52](https://youtu.be/d2GglPzOXfM?t=652)
- A simple test for alignment: ask ten random employees what the company's top three goals are — founders always expect they'll match, but they almost never do, which is why goals must be repeated constantly [later-stage-advice-with-sam-altman-how-to-start-a-startup-20 @ 17:32](https://youtu.be/d2GglPzOXfM?t=1052)
- Company productivity roughly degrades with the square of headcount without deliberate systems, and the single most important lever is keeping the whole company aligned [later-stage-advice-with-sam-altman-how-to-start-a-startup-20 @ 16:30](https://youtu.be/d2GglPzOXfM?t=990)
- Founder psychology gets worse, not better, as the company grows — highs get better but lows get worse, and founders must consciously manage the widening emotional swings [later-stage-advice-with-sam-altman-how-to-start-a-startup-20 @ 26:24](https://youtu.be/d2GglPzOXfM?t=1584)
- Losing focus is often a symptom of burnout, and entertaining acquisition conversations you're not actually willing to accept a low offer for is one of the biggest startup killers [later-stage-advice-with-sam-altman-how-to-start-a-startup-20 @ 29:14](https://youtu.be/d2GglPzOXfM?t=1754)
- Ultimately, what kills most startups is founders giving up; managing your own psychology so you don't quit prematurely is the deciding factor [later-stage-advice-with-sam-altman-how-to-start-a-startup-20 @ 30:18](https://youtu.be/d2GglPzOXfM?t=1818)
- When a startup is failing, tell investors early, don't run out of cash into unpaid debts, and give departing employees notice plus severance to shut down gracefully [later-stage-advice-with-sam-altman-how-to-start-a-startup-20 @ 39:11](https://youtu.be/d2GglPzOXfM?t=2351)
- It's worth waiting to raise outside capital until the idea shows initial signs of promise, because raised money creates time pressure that forces a rushed pivot to "whatever the first vaguely plausible idea is" [later-stage-advice-with-sam-altman-how-to-start-a-startup-20 @ 47:07](https://youtu.be/d2GglPzOXfM?t=2827)
