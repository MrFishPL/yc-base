# How To Change The World? Get The Small Things Right – Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel — Compiled Truth

Founders who try to "force" the world to change without understanding the small motivations of every party in an ecosystem, and without researching who has already tried and failed, tend to fail; those who get the small things right (like early ride-sharing normalizing the idea via limo cars) earn the right to change the world.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Many founders build things because they believe the world should be a certain way, not because a customer actually wanted to buy it, unlike Elon Musk who could force his vision on the world [how-to-change-the-world-get-the-small-things-right-dalton-ca @ 00:47](https://youtu.be/qnav9vgHDHs?t=47)
- Founders often want to eliminate intermediaries (car dealers, realtors) without realizing those intermediaries exist because of real incentives that can't be waved away by software [how-to-change-the-world-get-the-small-things-right-dalton-ca @ 02:09](https://youtu.be/qnav9vgHDHs?t=129)
- Hiring startups repeatedly pitch replacing recruiters entirely, but recruiters have more institutional power than founders think and must be accommodated, not eliminated [how-to-change-the-world-get-the-small-things-right-dalton-ca @ 03:40](https://youtu.be/qnav9vgHDHs?t=220)
- You can create huge changes in human behavior only if you deeply understand the subtle pressure points and motivations of every key party and cater to those human institutions [how-to-change-the-world-get-the-small-things-right-dalton-ca @ 05:37](https://youtu.be/qnav9vgHDHs?t=337)
- Ride-sharing only became normal because early versions (hired limo cars) got the small trust-building steps right before consumers would accept getting into a stranger's car, and YC funded many failed "let people share rides" ideas before Uber worked [how-to-change-the-world-get-the-small-things-right-dalton-ca @ 06:27](https://youtu.be/qnav9vgHDHs?t=387)
- Founders frequently skip basic research (even a Google search) on people who already tried their idea, which is a self-own since that research is free and could save years of wasted effort [how-to-change-the-world-get-the-small-things-right-dalton-ca @ 09:07](https://youtu.be/qnav9vgHDHs?t=547)
- Instacart's founders succeeded partly because they knew Webvan had failed before them and intentionally designed around those lessons, unlike founders who repeat history blindly [how-to-change-the-world-get-the-small-things-right-dalton-ca @ 11:48](https://youtu.be/qnav9vgHDHs?t=708)
- Platforms change faster than human problems, so nearly every new startup is really an old human problem attacked with a new technology, which is why studying prior attempts matters [how-to-change-the-world-get-the-small-things-right-dalton-ca @ 12:48](https://youtu.be/qnav9vgHDHs?t=768)
- Investors and advisors can be biased for or against an idea based on their own narrow past experience, so founders should synthesize a wide variety of expert opinions rather than blindly accept any one view [how-to-change-the-world-get-the-small-things-right-dalton-ca @ 15:28](https://youtu.be/qnav9vgHDHs?t=928)
- Investors who know less about a space can be counterintuitively more optimistic than domain experts who have been burned before, so expertise does not always equal optimism [how-to-change-the-world-get-the-small-things-right-dalton-ca @ 16:46](https://youtu.be/qnav9vgHDHs?t=1006)
