# Why You Should Leave Your FAANG Job — Compiled Truth

Technical founders and founder-curious engineers grossly overvalue what a big tech (FAANG) job offers them, and the companies deliberately engineer retention systems that trap talent through vesting, lifestyle inflation, and prestige signaling.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Most people at FAANG companies work on unglamorous, narrow projects (like one pixel of an ad server) rather than the "hardest technical problems at the largest scale" recruiters advertise [why-you-should-leave-your-faang-job @ 04:07](https://youtu.be/sM2reZib2RY?t=247)
- Founders find that FAANG job skills often don't transfer to a startup because big companies provide massive proprietary tooling and infrastructure that doesn't exist when you start from scratch [why-you-should-leave-your-faang-job @ 06:42](https://youtu.be/sM2reZib2RY?t=402)
- Engineers from Google often have to unlearn overly high product standards, since what counts as barely an MVP at a startup would never "see the light of day" at Google [why-you-should-leave-your-faang-job @ 08:09](https://youtu.be/sM2reZib2RY?t=489)
- A FAANG resume can help with fundraising early on but loses its shine the longer someone stays, and founders overestimate how easily ex-FAANG engineers raise money because they only see the successful fundraises reported in the press [why-you-should-leave-your-faang-job @ 09:49](https://youtu.be/sM2reZib2RY?t=589)
- Big companies use gamified, four-year vesting equity schedules and loss aversion to keep employees trapped, since leaving early means forfeiting unvested equity [why-you-should-leave-your-faang-job @ 12:14](https://youtu.be/sM2reZib2RY?t=734)
- People surrounded by high-earning peers tend to inflate their lifestyle (nicer apartments, vacations) instead of saving, which further prevents them from ever having enough savings to leave and start a company [why-you-should-leave-your-faang-job @ 13:09](https://youtu.be/sM2reZib2RY?t=789)
- Speculative "moonshot" projects at big companies frequently get shut down with no guarantee the years of work will ever ship, as illustrated by Facebook's abandoned chip project and PayPal laying off a research lab [why-you-should-leave-your-faang-job @ 14:27](https://youtu.be/sM2reZib2RY?t=867)
- A clear sign it's time to leave is feeling like you can't stop doing something you hate, similar to an addiction, while the tactical fix is to keep personal burn low so you're not financially trapped [why-you-should-leave-your-faang-job @ 15:40](https://youtu.be/sM2reZib2RY?t=940)
- Good programmers who leave FAANG in a positive, on-good-terms way can generally return later, so there is less risk to leaving than people assume [why-you-should-leave-your-faang-job @ 16:16](https://youtu.be/sM2reZib2RY?t=976)
- The recommended approach is to have a plan going in: know exactly why you're taking the job (visa, savings, resume) and when you intend to leave, since fighting a surprise bonus offer with no plan usually means the bonus wins [why-you-should-leave-your-faang-job @ 18:54](https://youtu.be/sM2reZib2RY?t=1134)
