# A.I. Policy and Public Perception - Miles Brundage and Tim Hwang — Compiled Truth

Miles Brundage and Tim Hwang discuss how AI can be deliberately misused, why interpretability and fairness are contested near-term problems, and how public perception, national competition, and openness norms shape AI policy.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Brundage's paper "The Malicious Use of Artificial Intelligence" argues AI is a dual/omni-use technology needing norms like responsible disclosure, similar to biotech and computer security [a-i-policy-and-public-perception-miles-brundage-and-tim-hwan @ 00:37](https://youtu.be/be0NSfPRoWg?t=37)
- Their methodology extrapolates future misuse by tracking which research trends are becoming efficient and widely usable, not just reading code [a-i-policy-and-public-perception-miles-brundage-and-tim-hwan @ 02:45](https://youtu.be/be0NSfPRoWg?t=165)
- Physical-world AI misuse is harder and less scalable than virtual/digital misuse because of hardware costs and real-world perception difficulty [a-i-policy-and-public-perception-miles-brundage-and-tim-hwan @ 06:45](https://youtu.be/be0NSfPRoWg?t=405)
- The public's reaction to AI harms is unpredictable — people got desensitized to data breaches but a combined AI-plus-cyber incident could trigger a severe backlash [a-i-policy-and-public-perception-miles-brundage-and-tim-hwan @ 08:26](https://youtu.be/be0NSfPRoWg?t=506)
- The public conflates "AI" with popular culture and specific applications (e.g., not viewing Newsfeed as AI but viewing non-ML robots as AI), which distorts policy debate [a-i-policy-and-public-perception-miles-brundage-and-tim-hwan @ 09:24](https://youtu.be/be0NSfPRoWg?t=564)

- Robustness against adversarial examples and backdoored models (e.g., "BadNets") remains unsolved and is a major barrier to real-world deployment [a-i-policy-and-public-perception-miles-brundage-and-tim-hwan @ 12:05](https://youtu.be/be0NSfPRoWg?t=725)
