# Paul Buchheit — Compiled Truth

Paul Buchheit (Gmail creator, YC partner) lays out six mental "patterns" he returns to when stuck or doubtful: distrust certainty and experience, silence internal doubt loops, accept setbacks as gifts, choose the interesting path, love what you do rather than chase what you love, and maintain a healthy disregard for the impossible.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- The best startup opportunities live in collective blind spots — if an opportunity were obvious to everyone, someone would already have taken it, as when Larry and Sergey couldn't find a buyer for Google at $1M in 1997 [paul-buchheit @ 03:27](https://youtu.be/v5w-0H-hq6E?t=207).
- Experience and dogma are both dangerous because they convince you an idea "won't work" or put fixed boundaries around your thinking, closing off exploration [paul-buchheit @ 03:27](https://youtu.be/v5w-0H-hq6E?t=207).
- "Kill all demon processes" — actively clear out the internalized voices of doubt, anger, and self-loathing that discourage risk-taking, the way many inside Google doubted Gmail before launch [paul-buchheit @ 05:21](https://youtu.be/v5w-0H-hq6E?t=321).
- "Yes, and thank you" — treat setbacks as gifts and keep moving forward instead of getting stuck in anger, since the best startups turn setbacks into advantages (e.g., Uber turning a London taxi strike into growth) [paul-buchheit @ 07:31](https://youtu.be/v5w-0H-hq6E?t=451).
- "Interestingness" is a useful signal for what to invest in or build — pursue productive uncertainty rather than outcomes you already know or that are pure gambling, as with his early bet on Justin.tv/Twitch [paul-buchheit @ 09:58](https://youtu.be/v5w-0H-hq6E?t=598).
- You can guarantee a form of success by redefining it to include "learning something interesting," so a startup shouldn't have only one narrow definition of success [paul-buchheit @ 11:25](https://youtu.be/v5w-0H-hq6E?t=685).
- "Love what you do" beats "do what you love" — intrinsic enjoyment of the daily grind (not a fixed passion) sustains founders through the mostly unpleasant, uncertain work of building a company [paul-buchheit @ 12:11](https://youtu.be/v5w-0H-hq6E?t=731).
- Maintain a "healthy disregard for the impossible," as Google did aiming to index the whole web in memory or write Gmail's interface in JavaScript despite near-universal warnings against it [paul-buchheit @ 15:04](https://youtu.be/v5w-0H-hq6E?t=904).
- Buchheit frames startups as machines for harnessing self-interest toward a larger purpose, not merely a pursuit of money — the truly significant founders he's known always have a bigger mission [paul-buchheit @ 17:13](https://youtu.be/v5w-0H-hq6E?t=1033).
