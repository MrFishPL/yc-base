# Startup Investor School Day 2 Live Stream — Compiled Truth

Dalton Caldwell, Paul Buchheit, and Michael Seibel share hard-won angel investing lessons on building a decision process, reading founders, avoiding groupthink and bad deal terms, and sizing checks for meaningful returns.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Treat investing like a sales funnel (leads, intros, qualification, decision, close) and know your budget and target number of deals before you start meeting founders [startup-investor-school-day-2-live-stream @ 07:31](https://youtu.be/IvttzQnsxls?t=451).
- Once you decide you're a yes, you're fully committed — backing out after committing causes lasting reputational damage, and a lingering non-answer is worse for founders than a clear no [startup-investor-school-day-2-live-stream @ 20:59](https://youtu.be/IvttzQnsxls?t=1259).
- The best investments are often contrarian or unappealing to everyone else, because groupthink herds investors into the same deals and produces non-optimal returns [startup-investor-school-day-2-live-stream @ 16:18](https://youtu.be/IvttzQnsxls?t=978).
- Milestone-based investment terms are really just an option for the investor to invest later; founders should take upfront money without committing to accept future milestone-based cash [startup-investor-school-day-2-live-stream @ 47:23](https://youtu.be/IvttzQnsxls?t=2843).
- Good startup ideas often look bad at first, because if an opportunity were obviously good, a large, well-resourced company would already be executing on it [startup-investor-school-day-2-live-stream @ 1:18:36](https://youtu.be/IvttzQnsxls?t=4716).
- Since founders and market insight matter more than the investor's own expertise, the key criterion is finding founders who are "better than you" so you can effectively outsource the thinking to them [startup-investor-school-day-2-live-stream @ 1:31:29](https://youtu.be/IvttzQnsxls?t=5489).
- There is no such thing as value investing in startups — chasing a "good price" or bargain valuation has never produced a return, and being deterred by a high price should instead prompt writing a bigger check [startup-investor-school-day-2-live-stream @ 1:43:50](https://youtu.be/IvttzQnsxls?t=6230).
- A personal checklist for evaluating founders includes clear concise communication, moving fast, accomplishing a lot with little, pursuing implausibly ambitious ideas, being a talent magnet, and determination [startup-investor-school-day-2-live-stream @ 1:47:52](https://youtu.be/IvttzQnsxls?t=6472).
- Angel checks need to be sized so that even a realistic (not best-case) billion-dollar exit, after dilution and taxes, produces a return that actually matters to the investor [startup-investor-school-day-2-live-stream @ 2:17:41](https://youtu.be/IvttzQnsxls?t=8261).
- Apply the "FOMO/friend rule": if a friend is starting a company and you'd feel FOMO watching it become a billion-dollar company, just write the check [startup-investor-school-day-2-live-stream @ 2:19:44](https://youtu.be/IvttzQnsxls?t=8384).
- The most hyped company on demo day is often not the best investment, because the room's enthusiasm is frequently driven by investors talking up deals they've already made [startup-investor-school-day-2-live-stream @ 2:22:05](https://youtu.be/IvttzQnsxls?t=8525).
