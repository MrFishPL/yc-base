# Competition is for Losers with Peter Thiel (How to Start a Startup 2014: 5) — Compiled Truth

Peter Thiel argues that founders should always aim for monopoly rather than competition, because a company's value depends on both the size of the market it creates (X) and the percentage of that value it captures (Y), and these are independent variables that people systematically lie about.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- A company's value equals X dollars of value created times Y percent of that value captured, and X and Y are independent — a huge market with tiny capture (airlines) can be worth far less than a smaller market with high capture (Google search) [competition-is-for-losers-with-peter-thiel-how-to-start-a-st @ 01:07](https://youtu.be/3Fx5Q8xGU8k?t=67)
- There are really only two kinds of businesses, perfectly competitive and monopoly, and people constantly lie about which one they're in: monopolists claim huge competitive markets to avoid regulation, while non-monopolists in tiny markets claim uniqueness to attract capital [competition-is-for-losers-with-peter-thiel-how-to-start-a-st @ 04:35](https://youtu.be/3Fx5Q8xGU8k?t=275)
- Startups should deliberately go after small markets first (Amazon started with just books, PayPal with ~20,000 eBay power sellers, Facebook with 10,000 Harvard students), then expand concentrically, since going after a giant market on day one usually signals miscategorized competition [competition-is-for-losers-with-peter-thiel-how-to-start-a-st @ 13:22](https://youtu.be/3Fx5Q8xGU8k?t=802)
- A monopoly-worthy technology should be roughly an order of magnitude better than the next best alternative on some key dimension, as PayPal was 10x faster than checks for clearing eBay payments [competition-is-for-losers-with-peter-thiel-how-to-start-a-st @ 19:46](https://youtu.be/3Fx5Q8xGU8k?t=1186)
- The goal is to be the "last mover" in a category rather than the first mover, because most of a tech company's discounted value comes from cash flows far in the future, so durability matters more than current growth rate [competition-is-for-losers-with-peter-thiel-how-to-start-a-st @ 23:05](https://youtu.be/3Fx5Q8xGU8k?t=1385)
- Software businesses are especially prone to building lasting monopolies because zero marginal costs create strong economies of scale and enable fast adoption before competitors can catch up [competition-is-for-losers-with-peter-thiel-how-to-start-a-st @ 22:06](https://youtu.be/3Fx5Q8xGU8k?t=1326)
- Across 250 years of science and technology history, most innovators captured close to 0% of the value they created (scientists, railroad builders, the Wright Brothers), with real financial capture largely limited to vertically integrated monopolies (Standard Oil, Ford, Tesla, SpaceX) and software businesses [competition-is-for-losers-with-peter-thiel-how-to-start-a-st @ 29:15](https://youtu.be/3Fx5Q8xGU8k?t=1755)
- Thiel says he is personally skeptical of lean startup methodology and iterative customer-feedback-driven building, arguing the truly great companies made quantum improvements rather than relying on constant surveys [competition-is-for-losers-with-peter-thiel-how-to-start-a-st @ 44:51](https://youtu.be/3Fx5Q8xGU8k?t=2691)
- Human competitiveness is partly a psychological trap — a mimetic, ape-like tendency to treat "many people are doing this" as validation, when in fact crowded pursuits (like 20,000 people a year moving to LA to become movie stars) are often evidence of poor odds rather than opportunity [competition-is-for-losers-with-peter-thiel-how-to-start-a-st @ 38:09](https://youtu.be/3Fx5Q8xGU8k?t=2289)
