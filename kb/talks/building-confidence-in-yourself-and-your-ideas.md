# Building Confidence In Yourself and Your Ideas — Compiled Truth

Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel argue that most premature pivots come not from real evidence but from low founder conviction and fear-driven, low-effort "validation" (cold LinkedIn blasts, one bad fundraising meeting), and that founders need to complete high-quality reps and build real conviction before changing direction.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- A founder who emailed 25 people, got no replies, and pivoted did not complete a "high quality rep" and learned essentially nothing from the exercise [building-confidence-in-yourself-and-your-ideas @ 04:20](https://youtu.be/al-15mMAS18?t=260)
- The most important customer to convince is yourself: build genuine conviction that the idea is worth your time rather than trying to please investors or your YC partner [building-confidence-in-yourself-and-your-ideas @ 05:00](https://youtu.be/al-15mMAS18?t=300)
- Founders trained as PMs or user researchers often struggle to get first customers because interviewing skills don't transfer to selling [building-confidence-in-yourself-and-your-ideas @ 02:57](https://youtu.be/al-15mMAS18?t=177)
- High conviction means not getting blown off course by a single hard fundraising meeting or one skeptical customer conversation; many great companies were dismissed by most investors early on [building-confidence-in-yourself-and-your-ideas @ 07:14](https://youtu.be/al-15mMAS18?t=434)
- Fear drives founders to fabricate narratives, like assuming (without checking) that 75% of their batch already has launched, growing products [building-confidence-in-yourself-and-your-ideas @ 08:50](https://youtu.be/al-15mMAS18?t=530)
- Founders can unlock their own sales instincts by remembering they were once a b2b software buyer themselves and asking what would have actually gotten them to respond or buy [building-confidence-in-yourself-and-your-ideas @ 10:54](https://youtu.be/al-15mMAS18?t=654)
- Constantly pivoting after each shallow signal is a "random walk" that burns founder energy without making real forward progress, typically leaving founders exhausted within 1.5-2 years [building-confidence-in-yourself-and-your-ideas @ 13:48](https://youtu.be/al-15mMAS18?t=828)
- An MVP isn't truly "minimum viable" if it doesn't work for even a single user; founders should be able and willing to use their own product before selling it to strangers [building-confidence-in-yourself-and-your-ideas @ 16:20](https://youtu.be/al-15mMAS18?t=980)
- The bar to aim for is winning over just one genuinely happy customer before worrying about scaling to millions [building-confidence-in-yourself-and-your-ideas @ 19:01](https://youtu.be/al-15mMAS18?t=1141)
