# Jared Friedman - Advice for Hard-tech and Biotech Founders — Compiled Truth

A YC partner argues that hard tech and biotech companies face mostly technical risk rather than market risk, and lays out how founders can make cheap early progress and prove demand before they have millions of dollars to build a real product.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- A hard tech company is defined as one where building the first product takes a lot of time and money and it's unclear whether it's even possible, versus normal software companies which mostly face market risk not technical risk [jared-friedman-advice-for-hard-tech-and-biotech-founders @ 01:00](https://youtu.be/C1DlZWfI6rk?t=60)
- Sam Altman's paradox is that it's easier to start a hard company than an easy one, because launching a simple product is easy but making people care about it is hard, whereas ambitious hard-tech ideas attract talent and investors from day one [jared-friedman-advice-for-hard-tech-and-biotech-founders @ 02:19](https://youtu.be/C1DlZWfI6rk?t=139)
- Hard tech applicants to YC have about a 10x higher acceptance rate than other applications, and YC is the largest hard tech and biotech seed investor in the world [jared-friedman-advice-for-hard-tech-and-biotech-founders @ 05:43](https://youtu.be/C1DlZWfI6rk?t=343)
- Boom Supersonic made cheap early progress with no money by assembling advisors, building computer simulations, and showing airlines a plastic model plane to generate customer demand before building the real jet [jared-friedman-advice-for-hard-tech-and-biotech-founders @ 07:45](https://youtu.be/C1DlZWfI6rk?t=465)
- Solugen proved its novel industrial process for making hydrogen peroxide with a simple beaker MVP that produced about one cup, before scaling to a full plant [jared-friedman-advice-for-hard-tech-and-biotech-founders @ 08:39](https://youtu.be/C1DlZWfI6rk?t=519)
- Ginkgo Bioworks and similar hard tech companies pre-sold contracts to customers before building the product, then used those signed contracts as proof of demand to raise the money needed to actually build it [jared-friedman-advice-for-hard-tech-and-biotech-founders @ 11:35](https://youtu.be/C1DlZWfI6rk?t=695)
- When pre-sales are illegal (e.g., medical products needing FDA approval), founders can use a non-binding Letter of Intent (LOI) to prove real customer demand, and the more specific the LOI the more valuable it is [jared-friedman-advice-for-hard-tech-and-biotech-founders @ 13:09](https://youtu.be/C1DlZWfI6rk?t=789)
- Hard tech fundraising should be split into a series of small, discrete raises tied to specific milestones rather than trying to raise the full $50 million needed all at once [jared-friedman-advice-for-hard-tech-and-biotech-founders @ 15:19](https://youtu.be/C1DlZWfI6rk?t=919)
- Good hard tech founders are maniacal about keeping each fundraising step as small as possible so they can hit milestones and unlock the next round without hitting a fundraising wall [jared-friedman-advice-for-hard-tech-and-biotech-founders @ 16:35](https://youtu.be/C1DlZWfI6rk?t=995)
- Hard tech companies often find recruiting easier than typical startups because talented people are drawn to crazy, ambitious ideas [jared-friedman-advice-for-hard-tech-and-biotech-founders @ 23:24](https://youtu.be/C1DlZWfI6rk?t=1404)
