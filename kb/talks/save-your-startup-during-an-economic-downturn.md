# Save Your Startup During an Economic Downturn — Compiled Truth

Michael Seibel and Dalton Caldwell unpack Paul Graham's "default alive vs. default dead" framework and argue founders should run the math early, take proactive hits to growth or burn, and avoid betting company survival on the next fundraise.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Default alive means your current growth rate gets you to profitability before your bank account hits zero even if no more investor money comes in, which is different from being profitable today [save-your-startup-during-an-economic-downturn @ 01:28](https://youtu.be/0OVSTWozvfY?t=88)
- Founders avoid doing the default-dead math because raising money is addictive and admitting you might not raise the next round feels like a loss of confidence in front of co-founders and investors [save-your-startup-during-an-economic-downturn @ 03:15](https://youtu.be/0OVSTWozvfY?t=195)
- Fundraising rounds get structurally harder as you go (fewer Series A's than seed rounds, fewer Series B's than A's), so assuming the next round will be as easy as the last is a mistake [save-your-startup-during-an-economic-downturn @ 04:30](https://youtu.be/0OVSTWozvfY?t=270)
- Being default alive gives founders huge margin for error because a failed fundraise doesn't kill the company, while default-dead founders die if the raise doesn't close [save-your-startup-during-an-economic-downturn @ 06:14](https://youtu.be/0OVSTWozvfY?t=374)
- Investors and founders have misaligned incentives at the portfolio level: pushing many companies to burn fast and grow explosively makes sense for a VC's portfolio even though it kills most of the individual companies [save-your-startup-during-an-economic-downturn @ 11:23](https://youtu.be/0OVSTWozvfY?t=683)
- The three levers to fix a default-dead company are cutting headcount (the biggest lever, since people are the main cost), cutting ad spend even if it hurts growth optics, and raising prices instead of subsidizing every sale [save-your-startup-during-an-economic-downturn @ 19:39](https://youtu.be/0OVSTWozvfY?t=1179)
- Founders often only ask for help once they're down to low runway, when it's too late to fix things, rather than doing the survival math six months earlier when it could still be tied off like a tourniquet [save-your-startup-during-an-economic-downturn @ 16:01](https://youtu.be/0OVSTWozvfY?t=961)
- Companies with no cash are not attractive acquisition targets because a smart buyer knows a hemorrhaging company will soon be bankrupt and may carry legal liability [save-your-startup-during-an-economic-downturn @ 18:27](https://youtu.be/0OVSTWozvfY?t=1107)
- At Justin.tv/Twitch, Seibel's team faced dying in two months, called an all-hands admitting the mistake, cut costs and added ads, and went from that meeting in August to break-even by October and $1.2M profit by December [save-your-startup-during-an-economic-downturn @ 27:23](https://youtu.be/0OVSTWozvfY?t=1643)
- Taking a proactive "startup bankruptcy" hit to growth is better than traditional bankruptcy because startups are judged mainly on the last 6-18 months, so a company can rebuild its story fast [save-your-startup-during-an-economic-downturn @ 25:46](https://youtu.be/0OVSTWozvfY?t=1546)
