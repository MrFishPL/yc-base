# Carolynn Levy and Kirsty Nathoo - Startup Investor School Day 1 — Compiled Truth

YC's safe co-author and CFO walk investors through how the SAFE works, how it converts into equity, and the norms and pitfalls of the signing and conversion process.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- The SAFE is not debt: it accrues no interest and has no maturity date or right of repayment, unlike a convertible note [carolynn-levy-and-kirsty-nathoo-startup-investor-school-day- @ 04:52](https://youtu.be/Sk5B2Ud1DVg?t=292).
- The only two terms an investor negotiates in a capped safe are the investment amount and the valuation cap; everything else is boilerplate [carolynn-levy-and-kirsty-nathoo-startup-investor-school-day- @ 05:38](https://youtu.be/Sk5B2Ud1DVg?t=338).
- The valuation cap is not a current valuation of the company — it's a mechanism to reward early investors for taking on more risk when the priced round eventually happens at a higher valuation [carolynn-levy-and-kirsty-nathoo-startup-investor-school-day- @ 10:07](https://youtu.be/Sk5B2Ud1DVg?t=607).
- When a priced round closes, three things happen in order: the option pool is created/increased, safes convert into shares, then new money buys shares — and the sequencing materially affects how many shares each safe converts into [carolynn-levy-and-kirsty-nathoo-startup-investor-school-day- @ 12:17](https://youtu.be/Sk5B2Ud1DVg?t=737).
- A worked example shows an $800k safe raise at an $8M cap converting to just over 7% ownership after a $4M priced round at a $16M pre-money valuation, illustrating that safe holders can't know their exact ownership until the priced round happens [carolynn-levy-and-kirsty-nathoo-startup-investor-school-day- @ 19:31](https://youtu.be/Sk5B2Ud1DVg?t=1171).
- In an acquisition, safes convert to common stock and participate in merger proceeds in a "home run" scenario, but in an "aqua-hire" with little money, holders typically elect to just get their safe paid off [carolynn-levy-and-kirsty-nathoo-startup-investor-school-day- @ 22:36](https://youtu.be/Sk5B2Ud1DVg?t=1356).
- In a dissolution, safe holders are repaid after trade creditors and employee salaries but before common stockholders — though often there's little or nothing left [carolynn-levy-and-kirsty-nathoo-startup-investor-school-day- @ 24:27](https://youtu.be/Sk5B2Ud1DVg?t=1467).
- The "handshake protocol" — an email exchange confirming dollar amount and cap before signing — exists to prevent misunderstandings between optimistic founders and investors, and backing out after a handshake damages an investor's reputation [carolynn-levy-and-kirsty-nathoo-startup-investor-school-day- @ 27:23](https://youtu.be/Sk5B2Ud1DVg?t=1643).
- Investors should always review the pro forma cap table at conversion since lawyers frequently get the conversion math wrong, and should push back if a lead investor tries to ignore contractual pro rata rights [carolynn-levy-and-kirsty-nathoo-startup-investor-school-day- @ 32:23](https://youtu.be/Sk5B2Ud1DVg?t=1943).
- Their closing advice: don't waste time negotiating downside protection if you believe in the power law, be helpful without demanding a board seat, and tolerate founder pivots since the safe gives no right to get your money back [carolynn-levy-and-kirsty-nathoo-startup-investor-school-day- @ 33:28](https://youtu.be/Sk5B2Ud1DVg?t=2008).
