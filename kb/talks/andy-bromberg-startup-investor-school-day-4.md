# Andy Bromberg - Startup Investor School Day 4 — Compiled Truth

CoinList CEO Andy Bromberg traces the 70-80 year history of early-stage investing — from 1940s friends-and-family deals through the JOBS Act to 2017's ICO boom — to argue three durable trends: falling cost to start a company, falling cost/barrier to invest, and a market that keeps pushing toward faster liquidity.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Three trends recur across the whole history of venture: decreasing cost to start a company, decreasing cost/barrier for investors to invest, and the market's arc bending toward liquidity [andy-bromberg-startup-investor-school-day-4 @ 06:43](https://youtu.be/T6g2D92H1FQ?t=403)
- In the 1940s-1950s, founders had to put in hundreds of thousands of dollars of their own or friends-and-family money before any venture fund would even look at them — a barrier that held for roughly 40 years [andy-bromberg-startup-investor-school-day-4 @ 09:49](https://youtu.be/T6g2D92H1FQ?t=589)
- The 1970s saw institutional capital (endowments, corporations) enter venture as an "alternative asset" for the first time, which funded the first wave of angels-turned-fund-managers [andy-bromberg-startup-investor-school-day-4 @ 11:57](https://youtu.be/T6g2D92H1FQ?t=717)
- Venture AUM went from about $12B in 1996 to LPs putting in $120B in a single year (2000), illustrating the dot-com-era capital boom before the crash [andy-bromberg-startup-investor-school-day-4 @ 16:20](https://youtu.be/T6g2D92H1FQ?t=980)
- YC's standardization of convertible notes (2005-2009) and later the SAFE (2014) removed interest terms and legal friction, driving down the cost and speed of raising a seed round [andy-bromberg-startup-investor-school-day-4 @ 24:11](https://youtu.be/T6g2D92H1FQ?t=1451)
- The 2012 JOBS Act exempted venture funds from registered-investment-adviser status and created general solicitation (506(c)) and equity crowdfunding, dramatically lowering the bar to become an angel or raise from one [andy-bromberg-startup-investor-school-day-4 @ 22:05](https://youtu.be/T6g2D92H1FQ?t=1325)
- ICOs let companies raise money from thousands of investors without ever meeting them, representing a step change in the trend toward cheaper, faster fundraising, but token holders own a stake in a network, not equity or governance in a company [andy-bromberg-startup-investor-school-day-4 @ 30:17](https://youtu.be/T6g2D92H1FQ?t=1817)
- Evaluating an ICO without in-person meetings means relying on team background, published technology, Slack/Telegram interaction, and following the diligence of top crypto funds that do get direct access [andy-bromberg-startup-investor-school-day-4 @ 42:34](https://youtu.be/T6g2D92H1FQ?t=2554)
- Faster liquidity has a real downside: if a company hits a rough patch and its biggest investors can exit immediately, that can kill the company, which is why even token deals are increasingly using lockups [andy-bromberg-startup-investor-school-day-4 @ 53:19](https://youtu.be/T6g2D92H1FQ?t=3199)
- Bromberg argues the "ideal" ICO never raises a second round — enough money is raised to launch the network, after which the network belongs to the world rather than the company [andy-bromberg-startup-investor-school-day-4 @ 54:42](https://youtu.be/T6g2D92H1FQ?t=3282)
