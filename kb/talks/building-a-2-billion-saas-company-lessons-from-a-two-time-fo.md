# Building A $2 Billion SaaS Company: Lessons From A Two Time Founder — Compiled Truth

Zip co-founder Rujul Zaparde compares his scrappy, asset-heavy first startup FlightCar to his disciplined second startup Zip, a procurement software company that raised a Series D at a $2.2B valuation, drawing lessons on margins, hiring, cold sales, and founder psychology.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- At FlightCar, founders launched within days of Paul Graham's prodding by improvising: parking customers' cars in a BART transit lot until the transit police shut it down, then switching to an "undercover" operation [building-a-2-billion-saas-company-lessons-from-a-two-time-fo @ 04:12](https://youtu.be/rjyJsbUunQ4?t=252)
- To fake initial supply, underage founders (too young to rent cars themselves) found an obscure rental agency willing to rent to 18-year-olds and personally drove dozens of rental cars one at a time to seed the marketplace [building-a-2-billion-saas-company-lessons-from-a-two-time-fo @ 06:04](https://youtu.be/rjyJsbUunQ4?t=364)
- FlightCar's asset-heavy, low-margin business model meant it needed money more desperately while also being harder to raise for, creating a negative feedback loop, versus a higher-margin business creating a positive one [building-a-2-billion-saas-company-lessons-from-a-two-time-fo @ 08:44](https://youtu.be/rjyJsbUunQ4?t=524)
- After selling FlightCar, the founder deliberately joined Airbnb as a product manager to learn what a well-run company and best-in-class product/engineering teams actually look like before starting again [building-a-2-billion-saas-company-lessons-from-a-two-time-fo @ 10:04](https://youtu.be/rjyJsbUunQ4?t=604)
- As a second-time founder working with Dalton at YC, the advice that shaped Zip was to pick an idea with execution risk but low market risk: find an old, unchanged software category and figure out what's changed in the world that creates a new problem to solve there [building-a-2-billion-saas-company-lessons-from-a-two-time-fo @ 17:09](https://youtu.be/rjyJsbUunQ4?t=1029)
- Zip's founders deliberately closed their first 10 customers through totally cold outreach (no friends or referrals) to prove real market pull, building a 107-page notes document from outbound conversations that shaped the product [building-a-2-billion-saas-company-lessons-from-a-two-time-fo @ 18:36](https://youtu.be/rjyJsbUunQ4?t=1116)
- Founders should charge early customers a rational amount (e.g., $10-20K/year) because charging generates honest signal, and if a mid-size company won't pay that, it signals a need to change the product rather than keep it free [building-a-2-billion-saas-company-lessons-from-a-two-time-fo @ 20:21](https://youtu.be/rjyJsbUunQ4?t=1221)
- As a first-time founder he cared heavily about optics with the team, investors, and press, but the second time around he focused on building something real and disproving assumptions rather than managing perception [building-a-2-billion-saas-company-lessons-from-a-two-time-fo @ 22:56](https://youtu.be/rjyJsbUunQ4?t=1376)
