# How The Best Companies Defend Against Mediocrity And Rot — Compiled Truth

Eric Ries argues that shareholder primacy is a historically recent, never-legislated norm that destroys long-term value, and that founders can build "incorruptible" mission-controlled companies through deliberate governance choices like PBCs and independent trust structures.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- The more successful an organization becomes, the more valuable a target it is for being taken over or stolen from, which is a blind spot founders aren't warned about [how-the-best-companies-defend-against-mediocrity-and-rot @ 01:53](https://youtu.be/7VKliOQXQ9M?t=113)
- Twilio's Jeff Lawson was fired just 199 days after his dual-class voting protections expired at year 7, despite revenue being up since IPO, illustrating how temporary founder protections fail [how-the-best-companies-defend-against-mediocrity-and-rot @ 08:26](https://youtu.be/7VKliOQXQ9M?t=506)
- Sol Price built FedMart on a fiduciary-to-the-customer ethos, was ousted by his own board in 1975 after taking the company public, and FedMart went bankrupt within 7 years while his next venture became Costco [how-the-best-companies-defend-against-mediocrity-and-rot @ 17:12](https://youtu.be/7VKliOQXQ9M?t=1032)
- Shareholder primacy is not actually law — it emerged from academic and judicial opinion in the 1960s-70s and became a "normative consensus" that was never put to a vote [how-the-best-companies-defend-against-mediocrity-and-rot @ 27:13](https://youtu.be/7VKliOQXQ9M?t=1633)
- Forming a Public Benefit Corporation (PBC) is an easy, low-cost, no-brainer first step for founders, especially before taking on equity investors, and restores "purposeful incorporation" [how-the-best-companies-defend-against-mediocrity-and-rot @ 22:57](https://youtu.be/7VKliOQXQ9M?t=1377)
- Independent board directors are marketed as good governance but have no financial stake in a company's mission enduring, only an incentive to be seen as pro-investor, since investors recommend them for future board seats [how-the-best-companies-defend-against-mediocrity-and-rot @ 33:11](https://youtu.be/7VKliOQXQ9M?t=1991)
- Novo Nordisk's non-profit foundation trustees blocked a lucrative merger in the early 2000s because it wasn't necessary for survival, which preserved the GLP-1 research program that later drove the company's valuation past $600 billion [how-the-best-companies-defend-against-mediocrity-and-rot @ 38:19](https://youtu.be/7VKliOQXQ9M?t=2299)
- Industrial foundation structures (a for-profit paired with an outside non-profit or perpetual purpose trust) make companies six times more likely to survive to year 50 (60% vs 10% probability) [how-the-best-companies-defend-against-mediocrity-and-rot @ 41:29](https://youtu.be/7VKliOQXQ9M?t=2489)
- Anthropic's long-term benefit trust, a perpetual purpose trust with outside trustees who appoint board directors, took about two years to establish and is credited with giving the company structural strength to make principled decisions [how-the-best-companies-defend-against-mediocrity-and-rot @ 48:11](https://youtu.be/7VKliOQXQ9M?t=2891)
- Believing dual-class control makes a founder invincible is a dangerous illusion — such protections are frequently defeated (including by the founder's death) and can foster hubris syndrome, making founders less generous and more afraid of losing power [how-the-best-companies-defend-against-mediocrity-and-rot @ 43:47](https://youtu.be/7VKliOQXQ9M?t=2627)
