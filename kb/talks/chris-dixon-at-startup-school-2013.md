# Chris Dixon at Startup School 2013 — Compiled Truth

Chris Dixon argues that the best startup ideas are "good ideas that look like bad ideas," built on a secret the founder knows from direct experience that most people, including smart investors, don't yet believe.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Riffing on a Peter Thiel/Paul Graham framing, most durable startups sit in the sweet spot of good ideas that look like bad ideas, since obviously good ideas are already being chased by big companies and governments [chris-dixon-at-startup-school-2013 @ 00:30](https://youtu.be/akOazwgDiSI?t=30).
- Google looked like a bad business at launch because its search results were so good that users immediately left the site, the opposite of the "stickiness" portals like Yahoo were optimizing for, and one VC reportedly avoided even meeting the founders because the idea seemed obviously bad [chris-dixon-at-startup-school-2013 @ 01:22](https://youtu.be/akOazwgDiSI?t=82).
- Airbnb initially read to most observers as a weird, niche hipster behavior (strangers sleeping on your couch) rather than a future replacement for the hotel industry [chris-dixon-at-startup-school-2013 @ 03:24](https://youtu.be/akOazwgDiSI?t=204).
- One way to find a secret is knowing the tools better than anyone: Drew Houston built Dropbox to feel like a local hard drive by solving hard technical problems (NAT hole-punching, syncing) that the "hundred" existing cloud-storage competitors hadn't bothered to solve [chris-dixon-at-startup-school-2013 @ 05:42](https://youtu.be/akOazwgDiSI?t=342).
- Another way is knowing the problem better than anyone: Kickstarter's founder worked on the idea for about ten years, drawing on his own experience trying to coordinate artists and backers for a music festival [chris-dixon-at-startup-school-2013 @ 07:18](https://youtu.be/akOazwgDiSI?t=438).
- Dixon's own company, SiteAdvisor, succeeded because coming from the consumer-internet world rather than the security industry let the team treat phishing as a user-experience problem instead of a purely technical one [chris-dixon-at-startup-school-2013 @ 08:47](https://youtu.be/akOazwgDiSI?t=527).
- Good ideas that look bad are often dismissed as toys by powerful incumbents, as Western Union did with the telephone and as VCs did with Skype over concerns about microphone adoption and broadband [chris-dixon-at-startup-school-2013 @ 11:14](https://youtu.be/akOazwgDiSI?t=674).
- Such ideas often unbundle functions previously bundled together, as happened piece by piece to newspapers (distribution, classifieds, curation, then individual reporter brands) and is now happening to universities [chris-dixon-at-startup-school-2013 @ 13:57](https://youtu.be/akOazwgDiSI?t=837).
- These ideas often start as hobbyist projects that technical people work on for love rather than money, as with the homebrew computer club, open source, and GitHub's roots in the Git hobbyist community [chris-dixon-at-startup-school-2013 @ 16:47](https://youtu.be/akOazwgDiSI?t=1007).
- They frequently challenge social norms, as Flickr did by defaulting photos to public when prior photo services like Shutterfly defaulted to private [chris-dixon-at-startup-school-2013 @ 19:41](https://youtu.be/akOazwgDiSI?t=1181).
- Looking back across thousands of pitches and dozens of investments, the best predictor of success was founders with direct technical or problem-domain experience rather than ideas derived from abstract analyst reports or "X for Y" analogies [chris-dixon-at-startup-school-2013 @ 22:16](https://youtu.be/akOazwgDiSI?t=1336).
