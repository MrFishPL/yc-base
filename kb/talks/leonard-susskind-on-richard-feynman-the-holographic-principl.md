# Leonard Susskind on Richard Feynman, the Holographic Principle, and Unanswered Questions in Physics — Compiled Truth

Physicist Leonard Susskind reflects on his career-long habit of chasing "conflicts of principle" wherever theory doesn't fit together, describes the origins of string theory and the holographic principle, and discusses what motivated Feynman and himself to keep working despite no guarantee of success.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Susskind says his reputation as a "radical thinker" is a misconception: his ideas emerged from noticing conflicts of principle — situations where things weren't fitting together — and concluding something had to change [leonard-susskind-on-richard-feynman-the-holographic-principl @ 01:04](https://youtu.be/CQAcLW6qdQY?t=64)
- His new ideas almost always came from a sense that things weren't fitting together properly, what he calls a "conflict of principle" or paradox, such as the early puzzle of quark confinement [leonard-susskind-on-richard-feynman-the-holographic-principl @ 05:39](https://youtu.be/CQAcLW6qdQY?t=339)
- String theory originated when Susskind noticed a mathematical formula describing hadron properties resembled vibrating elastic strings, despite having no deep prior background in particle physics [leonard-susskind-on-richard-feynman-the-holographic-principl @ 09:31](https://youtu.be/CQAcLW6qdQY?t=571)
- The holographic principle arose from the paradox of whether information is lost when it falls into a black hole; the resolution was that infalling information is also stored, hologram-like, on the black hole's horizon [leonard-susskind-on-richard-feynman-the-holographic-principl @ 28:34](https://youtu.be/CQAcLW6qdQY?t=1714)
- Susskind describes staying motivated across a long career simply through unrelenting curiosity — he never planned to do physics until a certain age, he just kept getting curious about things [leonard-susskind-on-richard-feynman-the-holographic-principl @ 48:13](https://youtu.be/CQAcLW6qdQY?t=2893)
- On Feynman: despite disliking pretentious "philosophizing," Feynman was in Susskind's view deeply philosophical, and he felt real anguish over having both helped build nuclear weapons and having enjoyed the work [leonard-susskind-on-richard-feynman-the-holographic-principl @ 42:13](https://youtu.be/CQAcLW6qdQY?t=2533)
- Feynman's stance was that a scientist's job is to discover what the world is, not to police how discoveries get used — that responsibility falls to politicians and society [leonard-susskind-on-richard-feynman-the-holographic-principl @ 44:23](https://youtu.be/CQAcLW6qdQY?t=2663)
- Susskind explains he was drawn to public teaching partly because explaining ideas honestly to laypeople, without fake analogies, sharpened his own understanding of the physics [leonard-susskind-on-richard-feynman-the-holographic-principl @ 1:02:28](https://youtu.be/CQAcLW6qdQY?t=3748)
- A personal motivation for teaching traced back to his father's plumber friends, who were curious but had no way to distinguish real science from "crackpot" science, and Susskind wanted to be that resource for people like them [leonard-susskind-on-richard-feynman-the-holographic-principl @ 1:04:42](https://youtu.be/CQAcLW6qdQY?t=3882)
