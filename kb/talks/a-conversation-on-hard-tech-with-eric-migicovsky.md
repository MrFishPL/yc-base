# A Conversation on Hard Tech with Eric Migicovsky — Compiled Truth

Pebble founder and YC partner Eric Migicovsky draws on his five-year hardware journey and his work with hard-tech YC founders to give advice on MVPs, crowdfunding, financing inventory, hiring, and moats for hardware and deep-tech startups.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Pebble's predecessor watch was hand-assembled one unit at a time up to 700-800 units, which let the team catch catastrophic defects (like exploding backs) on a small batch instead of a mass production run [a-conversation-on-hard-tech-with-eric-migicovsky @ 04:10](https://youtu.be/7atClUTkR3A?t=250)
- It took five years from Pebble's initial 2008 prototype to real product-market fit in 2012, and Eric says frugality was vital — right after YC they raised $250K and spent it all on a 3,000-unit manufacturing run that took 8 months instead of 3, by which time their customer email list had gone cold [a-conversation-on-hard-tech-with-eric-migicovsky @ 06:55](https://youtu.be/7atClUTkR3A?t=415)
- Crowdfunding is useful in exactly two situations: raising ~$25-50K on a napkin idea to reach a working prototype, or as a marketing/sales channel later once you already have a validated product — Pebble's Kickstarter launched only after they'd already made and sold 1,500 of their first watch [a-conversation-on-hard-tech-with-eric-migicovsky @ 12:04](https://youtu.be/7atClUTkR3A?t=724)
- For early hires, Eric prioritized trust and reliability over deep domain expertise, since contractors and consultants lack the same motivation as full-time co-founders racing to build a startup [a-conversation-on-hard-tech-with-eric-migicovsky @ 17:28](https://youtu.be/7atClUTkR3A?t=1048)
- Startups can have great product-market fit but still fail from picking the wrong "business model-company fit" — Pebble misjudged that it was in a hits-driven, game-like market and needed to stay frugal to keep engineering more hits [a-conversation-on-hard-tech-with-eric-migicovsky @ 20:06](https://youtu.be/7atClUTkR3A?t=1206)
- For regulated or fundamental hard-tech, an MVP can be peer-reviewed science plus a clear path to clearance or a scaled-down demonstration, paired with early customer contracts or pilots as validation [a-conversation-on-hard-tech-with-eric-migicovsky @ 24:42](https://youtu.be/7atClUTkR3A?t=1482)
- Eric pushes YC hard-tech founders to start selling immediately rather than perfecting the prototype first — one company went from zero leads to $4-5M in lined-up sales within three months once they focused entirely on customer conversations [a-conversation-on-hard-tech-with-eric-migicovsky @ 27:19](https://youtu.be/7atClUTkR3A?t=1639)
- The best defensible moats are network effects and data lock-in that founders control directly, rather than patents, which are expensive to enforce and rarely work for startups outside biotech [a-conversation-on-hard-tech-with-eric-migicovsky @ 44:03](https://youtu.be/7atClUTkR3A?t=2643)
- Hardware founders should buy off-the-shelf, halfway-there products (e.g., scooter companies gluing cell modems onto Xiaomi scooters) rather than custom-building from scratch, to get a physical test of the idea within days for a few hundred dollars [a-conversation-on-hard-tech-with-eric-migicovsky @ 39:33](https://youtu.be/7atClUTkR3A?t=2373)
- Inventory financing is one of the hardest problems for hardware companies because of the cash conversion cycle; the two fixes are getting customers to pre-order (as Xiaomi did with weekly phone drops) or reducing lead times, even at higher per-unit cost, to free up cash flow [a-conversation-on-hard-tech-with-eric-migicovsky @ 58:11](https://youtu.be/7atClUTkR3A?t=3491)
