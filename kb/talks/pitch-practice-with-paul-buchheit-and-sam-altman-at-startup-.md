# Pitch Practice with Paul Buchheit and Sam Altman at Startup School SV 2016 — Compiled Truth

Live pitch practice where founders pitch cold to Paul Buchheit and Sam Altman, then Sam re-pitches the same business, illustrating what makes a pitch land with a skeptical seed investor.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- As a seed investor the median startup is worth about $0 and roughly 95% are a bad investment, so investors default to "no" and are looking for why a given company is the exception [pitch-practice-with-paul-buchheit-and-sam-altman-at-startup- @ 01:12](https://youtu.be/fcyf9z4gSeI?t=72)
- A strong pitch opens with a plain-language explanation of who the customer is, what they pay for, and what the product actually does before layering on the ambitious vision [pitch-practice-with-paul-buchheit-and-sam-altman-at-startup- @ 31:48](https://youtu.be/fcyf9z4gSeI?t=1908)
- Founders should be able to explain their business in the first 30 seconds in a way the listener can understand, or the rest of the pitch is unlikely to recover [pitch-practice-with-paul-buchheit-and-sam-altman-at-startup- @ 31:28](https://youtu.be/fcyf9z4gSeI?t=1888)
- On the shipping marketplace pitch, the "why now" was framed around a natural monopoly forming because cargo owners go where rates are cheapest and ship owners go where demand is, mirroring how Airbnb and Uber centralized fragmented markets [pitch-practice-with-paul-buchheit-and-sam-altman-at-startup- @ 14:44](https://youtu.be/fcyf9z4gSeI?t=884)
- A believable answer to "why will incumbents not just copy you" combined founder domain expertise (having personally traded commodities and shipped goods) with software know-how that incumbents typically lack [pitch-practice-with-paul-buchheit-and-sam-altman-at-startup- @ 18:00](https://youtu.be/fcyf9z4gSeI?t=1080)
- Sam's repitch showed that combining humans (brokers) with software, rather than pure automation, was framed as the reason other software-only attempts at the industry had failed [pitch-practice-with-paul-buchheit-and-sam-altman-at-startup- @ 15:32](https://youtu.be/fcyf9z4gSeI?t=932)
- Never leave an investor meeting without a concrete follow-up plan (an email, a next call), and always have an ask, though asking for a check after a first meeting is a red flag [pitch-practice-with-paul-buchheit-and-sam-altman-at-startup- @ 20:04](https://youtu.be/fcyf9z4gSeI?t=1204)
- YC's own onboarding process spends the first month getting each company to produce a clear one-line description of what they do, because most founders start out unable to state it clearly [pitch-practice-with-paul-buchheit-and-sam-altman-at-startup- @ 32:05](https://youtu.be/fcyf9z4gSeI?t=1925)
- On the healthcare-records pitch, founders repeatedly tried to describe the product as "that, plus a thousand other things," which Sam flagged as a common early-stage mistake — investors need one concrete, focused framework before hearing the expansive vision [pitch-practice-with-paul-buchheit-and-sam-altman-at-startup- @ 30:56](https://youtu.be/fcyf9z4gSeI?t=1856)
- People generally keep doing things the same way unless they're forced to change, so a good pitch identifies the specific pain point (e.g., a commodity trader losing money without a fast quote) that pushes customers to adopt new technology [pitch-practice-with-paul-buchheit-and-sam-altman-at-startup- @ 19:42](https://youtu.be/fcyf9z4gSeI?t=1182)
