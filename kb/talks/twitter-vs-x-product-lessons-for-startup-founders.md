# Twitter vs. X: Product Lessons For Startup Founders — Compiled Truth

Two former consumer-product founders dissect Twitter/X's shift to an algorithmic, engagement-maximizing feed, using it as a case study in why optimizing a single metric without a clear product vision erodes long-term user trust.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- The biggest change in Twitter/X has been the shift from a chronological feed of chosen topics to a TikTok-style algorithmic feed of engaging-but-unrequested content [twitter-vs-x-product-lessons-for-startup-founders @ 01:15](https://youtu.be/EW9TUqOgjmQ?t=75)
- When a product team is given a single number like dwell time or engagement to optimize, they tend to chase it while ignoring harder-to-measure things like taste, quality, or educational value [twitter-vs-x-product-lessons-for-startup-founders @ 02:47](https://youtu.be/EW9TUqOgjmQ?t=167)
- Watching more content isn't the same as getting value from a product; users can be highly engaged with content they don't actually want, which erodes retention over time as their best users churn [twitter-vs-x-product-lessons-for-startup-founders @ 03:33](https://youtu.be/EW9TUqOgjmQ?t=213)
- The history of Facebook, Instagram, and TikTok shows a recurring pattern: tight-knit, chosen-network feeds get diluted by growth and then deliberately broadened for engagement, ending in algorithmic feeds optimized for the "human brain" rather than for connection [twitter-vs-x-product-lessons-for-startup-founders @ 08:57](https://youtu.be/EW9TUqOgjmQ?t=537)
- What founders should ask is what real-world problem they're solving: if it's boredom, a TikTok-style feed may be right, but if it's genuine human connection, they must explicitly resist the pull toward pure engagement optimization [twitter-vs-x-product-lessons-for-startup-founders @ 09:14](https://youtu.be/EW9TUqOgjmQ?t=554)
- Twitter still shines during breaking news because it's uniquely easy for first-party information owners to post directly to the platform, faster than traditional media can produce coverage [twitter-vs-x-product-lessons-for-startup-founders @ 12:16](https://youtu.be/EW9TUqOgjmQ?t=736)
- Overloading the blue checkmark to mean both "verified identity" and "paid subscriber" destroyed its original signal of credibility [twitter-vs-x-product-lessons-for-startup-founders @ 12:54](https://youtu.be/EW9TUqOgjmQ?t=774)
- Grok's placement inside Twitter/X is an example of a product expanding beyond what users think it's for, illustrating a founder's job to clearly articulate what a product is for and how it should be used [twitter-vs-x-product-lessons-for-startup-founders @ 14:01](https://youtu.be/EW9TUqOgjmQ?t=841)
- When renaming a product/company, do it early, pick a name that's easy to say, spell, and Google, and let meaning attach to the name over time rather than requiring the name to explain the product [twitter-vs-x-product-lessons-for-startup-founders @ 18:05](https://youtu.be/EW9TUqOgjmQ?t=1085)
- Founder-CEOs have a unique moral authority to enforce a consistent product vision even when it isn't the objectively optimal growth path, which matters more than chasing a single metric [twitter-vs-x-product-lessons-for-startup-founders @ 21:22](https://youtu.be/EW9TUqOgjmQ?t=1282)
