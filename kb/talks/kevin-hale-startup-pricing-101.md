# Kevin Hale - Startup Pricing 101 — Compiled Truth

Pricing is the most neglected but highest-leverage growth lever, and startups should reason from first principles about cost, price, and value rather than guessing a number.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- A 1% improvement in pricing yields roughly a 6.7% boost to revenue, more than acquisition (3.32%) or retention (6.7% comparably), yet pricing is the lever founders touch least out of fear [kevin-hale-startup-pricing-101 @ 02:15](https://youtu.be/jwXlo9gy_k4?t=135)
- The "pricing thermometer" frames price between cost (the gap is your margin/incentive to sell) and value (the gap is your incentive to buy) [kevin-hale-startup-pricing-101 @ 03:03](https://youtu.be/jwXlo9gy_k4?t=183)
- Value-based pricing (setting price from perceived value rather than cost-plus) lets you charge much more, but most founders default to underpricing because they don't understand their own costs or value [kevin-hale-startup-pricing-101 @ 04:16](https://youtu.be/jwXlo9gy_k4?t=256)
- Startups in the product-development and introduction stages are selling to early adopters, not mainstream customers, and early adopters care about benefits over price [kevin-hale-startup-pricing-101 @ 06:44](https://youtu.be/jwXlo9gy_k4?t=404)
- Charging less than a better competitor can backfire with early adopters because a very low price signals reputation risk ("too good to be true") [kevin-hale-startup-pricing-101 @ 08:53](https://youtu.be/jwXlo9gy_k4?t=533)
- Price optimization can be done with a simple table of price points versus conversion rate, sales volume, and resulting revenue rather than a complex demand curve [kevin-hale-startup-pricing-101 @ 09:37](https://youtu.be/jwXlo9gy_k4?t=577)
- SMB customers sit in a "danger zone" because they spend like consumers but expect enterprise-level service, making that segment especially hard to price and sell into [kevin-hale-startup-pricing-101 @ 11:44](https://youtu.be/jwXlo9gy_k4?t=704)
- Price point determines go-to-market motion: sub-$2,000 must be self-serve/inbound with no sales team, $2,000-$10,000 supports inside sales and SDRs with a 1-3 month cycle, and $25,000+ enterprise deals justify sales engineers and a 6-12 month cycle [kevin-hale-startup-pricing-101 @ 12:32](https://youtu.be/jwXlo9gy_k4?t=752)
- Rule of thumb: price so that perceived value is 10x the price, and if customers don't immediately grasp that 10x value, the price is too low relative to perceived value [kevin-hale-startup-pricing-101 @ 15:22](https://youtu.be/jwXlo9gy_k4?t=922)
- Practice raising prices in 5% increments and keep raising until you start losing about 20% of deals, which signals a well-calibrated price [kevin-hale-startup-pricing-101 @ 16:09](https://youtu.be/jwXlo9gy_k4?t=969)
