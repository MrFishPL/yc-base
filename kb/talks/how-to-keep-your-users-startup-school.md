# How To Keep Your Users | Startup School — Compiled Truth

David Lieb (ex-Bump, Google Photos) argues that cohort retention is the single best quantitative way to know if you've made something people want, and explains how to build, read, and avoid fooling yourself with retention curves.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Cohort retention tracks individual groups of new users acquired in the same period, rather than mixing the whole user base together, to see how usage actually holds up over time [how-to-keep-your-users-startup-school @ 02:31](https://youtu.be/VNxBZ7ka5J0?t=151)
- To measure it you must define three things: how you isolate cohorts (usually by signup week/month), which action counts as "active," and which time granularity matches your product's intended usage [how-to-keep-your-users-startup-school @ 02:51](https://youtu.be/VNxBZ7ka5J0?t=171)
- Pick an action correlated with real value delivered, not just opening the app — e.g. Google Photos used "viewed a photo full screen" rather than app opens [how-to-keep-your-users-startup-school @ 04:52](https://youtu.be/VNxBZ7ka5J0?t=292)
- The only thing that matters in a retention curve is whether it flattens out, not the absolute height at which it flattens — a curve that goes to zero means you haven't made something people want [how-to-keep-your-users-startup-school @ 11:53](https://youtu.be/VNxBZ7ka5J0?t=713)
- Google Photos' early cohorts flattened at 20-40% weekly retention, which gave Lieb confidence the product would eventually reach a huge fraction of the world's population — and it did [how-to-keep-your-users-startup-school @ 12:50](https://youtu.be/VNxBZ7ka5J0?t=770)
- A common way founders fool themselves is picking too large a time period (e.g. widening from weekly to quarterly cohorts before investor meetings), which artificially flattens and raises curves [how-to-keep-your-users-startup-school @ 14:05](https://youtu.be/VNxBZ7ka5J0?t=845)
- Another mistake is picking too easy an action (like a notification-driven app open), which can be gamed and doesn't reflect real usage, as happened with Google+'s notification bell metric [how-to-keep-your-users-startup-school @ 15:38](https://youtu.be/VNxBZ7ka5J0?t=938)
- Paying is often a worse active-user signal than usage because users typically stop using a product before they cancel payment, so pairing "is paying" with "is actively using" is better [how-to-keep-your-users-startup-school @ 17:14](https://youtu.be/VNxBZ7ka5J0?t=1034)
- To improve flat-but-low cohorts, founders can improve the product itself, acquire better-fit users, improve onboarding/activation, or build network effects so the product gets better as more people join [how-to-keep-your-users-startup-school @ 21:19](https://youtu.be/VNxBZ7ka5J0?t=1279)
- The best possible outcome is a "layer cake" chart where accumulated cohorts from every prior month stack up and total active users keep growing — a sign the company can become very large [how-to-keep-your-users-startup-school @ 27:29](https://youtu.be/VNxBZ7ka5J0?t=1649)
