# Sam Altman - Startup Investor School Day 1 — Compiled Truth

Sam Altman lays out how to think like an angel investor: internalize the power law, ignore what other investors think, judge founders more than ideas, and look for markets that will be huge in ten years rather than huge today.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- His first big mistake as an investor was caring too much about what other investors thought, since roughly 80% of investors outsource their decision-making to social proof, creating herd effects [sam-altman-startup-investor-school-day-1 @ 03:30](https://youtu.be/s-4QqHHE16s?t=210).
- The power law means your best investment will return more than all your other investments combined, so angel investing is a game of home runs, not singles, and failure rate is the wrong thing to optimize [sam-altman-startup-investor-school-day-1 @ 04:30](https://youtu.be/s-4QqHHE16s?t=270).
- At YC, the top five companies represent about two-thirds of all value created and the single top company represents about a third, illustrating just how extreme the power law is in practice [sam-altman-startup-investor-school-day-1 @ 07:07](https://youtu.be/s-4QqHHE16s?t=427).
- Reputation with founders now matters more than deal terms, because founders have become the ones with leverage and choice, and they do reference checks on investors just as investors do on them [sam-altman-startup-investor-school-day-1 @ 09:40](https://youtu.be/s-4QqHHE16s?t=580).
- His best investments have usually been the deals that felt most expensive at the time, and value-investing behavior has generally not worked well in angel investing [sam-altman-startup-investor-school-day-1 @ 12:36](https://youtu.be/s-4QqHHE16s?t=756).
- Paul Buchheit's four traits of founders who build giant companies are obsession, focus, a sense of destiny/grandiosity, and love for what they're building [sam-altman-startup-investor-school-day-1 @ 16:05](https://youtu.be/s-4QqHHE16s?t=965).
- Execution speed and rate of improvement in a founder are highly predictive of success; YC watches how much progress founders make between office hours as a signal [sam-altman-startup-investor-school-day-1 @ 18:15](https://youtu.be/s-4QqHHE16s?t=1095).
- It's dogma among investors to say growth rate matters, not current size, yet in the same breath many only value large existing markets — the better filter is preferring a small, rapidly growing market to a large static one [sam-altman-startup-investor-school-day-1 @ 23:18](https://youtu.be/s-4QqHHE16s?t=1398).
- A real trend is one where a small number of users use the product obsessively (like early iPhone owners) and spontaneously evangelize it, versus a fake trend like early VR headsets that sit unused on shelves [sam-altman-startup-investor-school-day-1 @ 26:00](https://youtu.be/s-4QqHHE16s?t=1560).
- The best investment opportunities look like bad ideas that are actually good (e.g., early Reddit, which his own friends dismissed), while the worst money chases bad ideas that look good, often just repeating what already worked two years ago [sam-altman-startup-investor-school-day-1 @ 28:47](https://youtu.be/s-4QqHHE16s?t=1727).
- He asks himself "what do I understand that other people don't" before investing, since conviction without an independent edge means no real advantage in that decision [sam-altman-startup-investor-school-day-1 @ 34:22](https://youtu.be/s-4QqHHE16s?t=2062).
