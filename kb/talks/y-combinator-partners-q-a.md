# Y Combinator Partners Q&A — Compiled Truth

Five YC partners field crowd-sourced questions on Silicon Valley's advantages, solo founders, co-founder disputes, the YC application process, and how to persevere as a startup founder.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Even if your customers are elsewhere, it's worth visiting or starting in California for its offline network effects, capital availability, and cultural bias toward believing ideas "might just work" [y-combinator-partners-q-a @ 04:44](https://youtu.be/ZTv4iO7e8bQ?t=284)
- Silicon Valley is hard to replicate because of three factors: concentrated technical talent, investor sophistication from seeing huge deal volume, and an outsized density of liquidity events [y-combinator-partners-q-a @ 07:31](https://youtu.be/ZTv4iO7e8bQ?t=451)
- YC funds solo founders but they must be "about four times as good," since building and selling are very different skills that are rare to find combined in one person, and co-founders provide emotional balance during a startup's highs and lows [y-combinator-partners-q-a @ 15:07](https://youtu.be/ZTv4iO7e8bQ?t=907)
- Most co-founder disputes stem from broken communication, uneven commitment, or power imbalances, and the key fix is to talk about problems early before they fester into an irreparable relationship [y-combinator-partners-q-a @ 17:36](https://youtu.be/ZTv4iO7e8bQ?t=1056)
- Splitting equity unequally because one founder "had the idea first" is nonsense since most of the multi-year work of building the company still lies ahead and should be shared accordingly [y-combinator-partners-q-a @ 20:19](https://youtu.be/ZTv4iO7e8bQ?t=1219)
- YC ran and then abandoned a "no idea founder" experiment because people who lacked a first idea tended to keep not having ideas, since having one signals the ability to generate more [y-combinator-partners-q-a @ 21:12](https://youtu.be/ZTv4iO7e8bQ?t=1272)
- Rejected applicants who take interview feedback to heart and reapply often end up at or near the top of a later batch — Drew Houston of Dropbox was rejected once before being accepted [y-combinator-partners-q-a @ 22:36](https://youtu.be/ZTv4iO7e8bQ?t=1356)
- YC funds nonprofits that are scalable and face the same growth challenges as for-profits (acquiring users, building a product), rather than small local organizations like a soup kitchen [y-combinator-partners-q-a @ 26:40](https://youtu.be/ZTv4iO7e8bQ?t=1600)
- Get customers or users before building the product rather than building in isolation for a long time — Paul Buchheit describes building the first version of Gmail in a day and iterating daily based on real user feedback [y-combinator-partners-q-a @ 32:19](https://youtu.be/ZTv4iO7e8bQ?t=1939)
- Founders should focus on getting a small number of real users happy one at a time in the early stage, even though it feels unscalable, rather than "floating in outer space" building something nobody wants [y-combinator-partners-q-a @ 33:54](https://youtu.be/ZTv4iO7e8bQ?t=2034)
