# Hiroki Takeuchi — Compiled Truth

GoCardless co-founder Hiroki Takeuchi shares four lessons from the company's early days: your journey starts earlier than you think, beware whiteboard ideas, focus on momentum over perfection, and be an "emotional cockroach" who can withstand extreme highs and lows.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Your entrepreneurial journey starts far earlier than the moment you write code or found a company — Takeuchi traces his to an internship he pursued after meeting founders in a university entrepreneurship society [hiroki-takeuchi @ 01:42](https://youtu.be/s4RBfIwUBgY?t=102).
- Be wary of "whiteboard ideas" — concepts that seem theoretically great in a big market but aren't something you'd actually want to use yourself [hiroki-takeuchi @ 11:12](https://youtu.be/s4RBfIwUBgY?t=672).
- GoCardless's first product, Group A (a group-payments tool), failed because the founders built a solution for a theoretical market rather than for themselves or people they knew [hiroki-takeuchi @ 10:53](https://youtu.be/s4RBfIwUBgY?t=653).
- When Group A wasn't working, the team pivoted to GoCardless, and much of the work already done (payments knowledge, a banking deal, regulatory applications) carried over directly [hiroki-takeuchi @ 10:09](https://youtu.be/s4RBfIwUBgY?t=609).
- Progress isn't linear — it's a slow zigzag of getting things wrong and course-correcting, so it's better to start doing the wrong thing than not start at all [hiroki-takeuchi @ 13:51](https://youtu.be/s4RBfIwUBgY?t=831).
- In the early days, momentum itself — getting from a standstill to something, anything — is harder than deciding whether an idea is right or wrong [hiroki-takeuchi @ 13:51](https://youtu.be/s4RBfIwUBgY?t=831).
- Fundraising can swing from near-total rejection (60+ nos, less than a quarter of the target raised) to closing $1.5M in days once the right investors say yes [hiroki-takeuchi @ 17:21](https://youtu.be/s4RBfIwUBgY?t=1041).
- Founders need to be "emotional cockroaches," able to withstand the biggest ups and downs, since peaks and troughs never really end even after milestones are hit [hiroki-takeuchi @ 19:02](https://youtu.be/s4RBfIwUBgY?t=1142).
- Sam Altman told Takeuchi that starting a business is like riding a wave between life and death — if you can hang on long enough, you're bound to succeed [hiroki-takeuchi @ 19:47](https://youtu.be/s4RBfIwUBgY?t=1187).
