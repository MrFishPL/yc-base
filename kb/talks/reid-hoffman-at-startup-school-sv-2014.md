# Reid Hoffman at Startup School SV 2014 — Compiled Truth

Reid Hoffman recounts LinkedIn's founding and his investing philosophy, arguing that most Silicon Valley "myths" about capital, valuation, and network effects are subtly wrong and that great ideas often look bad to smart people at first.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- The real question for a new product is not whether it works at hundreds of millions of users but how you get the first million, since without that the rest never happens [reid-hoffman-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 02:26](https://youtu.be/nef6uTa2a5w?t=146)
- LinkedIn's growth curve changed only after they let users upload their address book to see who else they knew was already on LinkedIn [reid-hoffman-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 03:37](https://youtu.be/nef6uTa2a5w?t=217)
- Open capital markets are not ideal for founders because more available capital means 5x more companies competing for the same talent and attention; historically, most game-changing companies were founded during downturns [reid-hoffman-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 05:11](https://youtu.be/nef6uTa2a5w?t=311)
- Raise more money than you think you need to reach a milestone that meaningfully changes the company, but don't overraise — companies that raise huge first rounds (e.g. $30M+) tend to become "train wrecks" because excess capital dulls sharpness [reid-hoffman-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 10:02](https://youtu.be/nef6uTa2a5w?t=602)
- Calling something a "network" doesn't mean it has a network effect — a true network effect requires that more nodes superlinearly increase the value of the system for everyone else [reid-hoffman-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 12:44](https://youtu.be/nef6uTa2a5w?t=764)
- A useful signal for a good startup idea is when smart friends think it's a bad idea — if everyone likes it, there are probably unseen problems, and if some smart people hate it, competition will be lighter [reid-hoffman-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 14:15](https://youtu.be/nef6uTa2a5w?t=855)
- LinkedIn's Series A strategy was to punt on revenue and use cases and instead focus entirely on establishing a distinct "professional network" category separate from Friendster, because that was the one problem that had to be solved to make the next round work [reid-hoffman-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 11:22](https://youtu.be/nef6uTa2a5w?t=682)
- Entrepreneurship requires holding two things in tension: enough self-delusion to believe you can make it work, while regularly asking whether your confidence is going up or down and what to learn or pivot if it isn't [reid-hoffman-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 08:23](https://youtu.be/nef6uTa2a5w?t=503)
- Doing something very contrarian for its own sake (e.g. "Airbnb for dogs") rarely produces big companies; better to look at areas where other people genuinely aren't looking [reid-hoffman-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 07:11](https://youtu.be/nef6uTa2a5w?t=431)
- Facebook succeeded partly because it built high trust and real identity by starting in colleges, and Zuckerberg's lack of interest in games created room for Zynga to build a serious company on the platform [reid-hoffman-at-startup-school-sv-2014 @ 20:38](https://youtu.be/nef6uTa2a5w?t=1238)
