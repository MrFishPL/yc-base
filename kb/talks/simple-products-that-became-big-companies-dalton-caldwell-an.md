# Simple Products That Became Big Companies – Dalton Caldwell and Michael Seibel — Compiled Truth

Founders should nail a single simple use case before adding complexity, because a broken product with many features is worse than a working product with one — a pattern shown by OpenSea, Gusto, and the failures of crowdfunded hardware and copy-pasted international business models.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Founders commonly try to "boil the ocean" — building an elaborate, fully-formed product before getting a single user — which just lets their unvalidated assumptions about customers run wild [simple-products-that-became-big-companies-dalton-caldwell-an @ 00:42](https://youtu.be/G7TMqY7gkGY?t=42)
- "Google-itis" — modeling your MVP on what a mature company looks like today (e.g. wanting to build "everything Amazon has") — ignores that these companies started as extremely simple products (Amazon just sold books) [simple-products-that-became-big-companies-dalton-caldwell-an @ 01:48](https://youtu.be/G7TMqY7gkGY?t=108)
- OpenSea spent one to two years grinding on a barely-functional, single-purpose website for buying and selling NFTs (without even its own wallet) before network effects kicked in and it became a multi-billion dollar company [simple-products-that-became-big-companies-dalton-caldwell-an @ 02:46](https://youtu.be/G7TMqY7gkGY?t=166)
- Gusto (originally ZenPayroll) launched doing exactly one thing — running payroll online — replacing a manual, phone-call-based process, proving a working single feature beats a broken multi-feature product [simple-products-that-became-big-companies-dalton-caldwell-an @ 04:30](https://youtu.be/G7TMqY7gkGY?t=270)
- Copying a business model that works overseas and assuming product-market fit is a given in a new country is a common trap — companies raise money and hire fast before realizing local users don't want the product [simple-products-that-became-big-companies-dalton-caldwell-an @ 06:20](https://youtu.be/G7TMqY7gkGY?t=380)
- Getting validation from investors, press, or a startup competition doesn't mean the product works — like a driver's license, it's not proof you're actually good at driving [simple-products-that-became-big-companies-dalton-caldwell-an @ 07:07](https://youtu.be/G7TMqY7gkGY?t=427)
- Founders must get "ground truth" answers by talking to customers directly rather than looking to authority figures (investors, teachers) for validation, since no one can substitute real observation of the problem [simple-products-that-became-big-companies-dalton-caldwell-an @ 09:13](https://youtu.be/G7TMqY7gkGY?t=553)
- Magical thinking about growth-over-revenue tends to fade once runway drops to about 12 months, showing that discipline (not just intelligence) drives founders to do focused work before their back is against the wall [simple-products-that-became-big-companies-dalton-caldwell-an @ 11:47](https://youtu.be/G7TMqY7gkGY?t=707)
- Hardware crowdfunding often fails because founders set prices and timelines with no idea what the product will actually cost or take to build, and backers who feel scammed can permanently damage a founder's reputation [simple-products-that-became-big-companies-dalton-caldwell-an @ 16:47](https://youtu.be/G7TMqY7gkGY?t=1007)
- Best practice for hardware crowdfunding is to do it only after you've already shipped and manufactured a couple of product versions, not at the earliest stage [simple-products-that-became-big-companies-dalton-caldwell-an @ 19:01](https://youtu.be/G7TMqY7gkGY?t=1141)
