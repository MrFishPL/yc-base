# Turning Your Users Into Paying Customers — Compiled Truth

Michael Seibel, Harj Taggar, and Brad Flora argue that founders who avoid charging are usually acting out of fear rather than strategy, and that asking for money early is the fastest way to learn whether a product is actually wanted.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- The best feedback about a product comes in the three seconds after telling a customer the price, from their facial expression or tone of voice [turning-your-users-into-paying-customers @ 00:00](https://youtu.be/9pQJXR0Tcos?t=0)
- Founders often avoid charging because they think getting feedback and getting revenue are in tension, so they optimize only for free user feedback [turning-your-users-into-paying-customers @ 01:31](https://youtu.be/9pQJXR0Tcos?t=91)
- Founders wrongly believe pricing must be perfect from day one because there are "no redos," when in fact successful startups change pricing constantly [turning-your-users-into-paying-customers @ 02:16](https://youtu.be/9pQJXR0Tcos?t=136)
- Dropbox reportedly picked $9.99/month as a "reasonable" number rather than through rigorous analysis, and simply moved forward rather than getting stuck [turning-your-users-into-paying-customers @ 03:17](https://youtu.be/9pQJXR0Tcos?t=197)
- Founders can always grandfather existing users into their original price and raise prices for new users later, so early pricing mistakes rarely matter once a company scales [turning-your-users-into-paying-customers @ 04:25](https://youtu.be/9pQJXR0Tcos?t=265)
- Startups selling to big companies without charging miss the anti-signal that not charging sends — enterprise buyers spend money on software routinely, and a free offer can make them doubt a vendor's viability [turning-your-users-into-paying-customers @ 05:18](https://youtu.be/9pQJXR0Tcos?t=318)
- Founders can waste years believing they have big-name "customers" (e.g., a large company using the product for free) when in fact zero revenue means the real sale was never made [turning-your-users-into-paying-customers @ 06:12](https://youtu.be/9pQJXR0Tcos?t=372)
- Free is legitimate only with a structured plan, as in open-core models (e.g., GitLab) where free developer adoption is deliberately tracked toward a later enterprise upsell [turning-your-users-into-paying-customers @ 07:41](https://youtu.be/9pQJXR0Tcos?t=461)
- A freemium model only works if there is a real constraint users will pay to remove and the founder disciplined about tracking free-to-paid conversion over time [turning-your-users-into-paying-customers @ 09:05](https://youtu.be/9pQJXR0Tcos?t=545)
- Large ad-supported consumer businesses (YouTube, Facebook, Twitter) are an exception where accumulating a huge free user base before monetizing makes sense, but founders must be honest that this is actually their model [turning-your-users-into-paying-customers @ 10:11](https://youtu.be/9pQJXR0Tcos?t=611)
