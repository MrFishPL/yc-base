# Rick and Morty Writer: Ryan Ridley — Compiled Truth

Rick and Morty writer Ryan Ridley describes how relentless rejection, peer pressure inside a competitive creator community, and years of unpaid work eventually turned into a hit show, arguing that making the thing you personally want to see is the only reliable path forward.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Ridley and Dan Harmon were both driven by a mix of wanting to prove themselves and fear of being exposed as frauds, which fueled the ambition behind the show [rick-and-morty-writer-ryan-ridley @ 05:55](https://youtu.be/RAOadSUNfIk?t=355)
- Channel 101, a purely democratic "competitive TV network" where audience votes could cancel or promote shows, forced everyone in it to keep getting better or get cancelled [rick-and-morty-writer-ryan-ridley @ 08:04](https://youtu.be/RAOadSUNfIk?t=484)
- His first submission to Channel 101 was rejected outright, and his second got in but wasn't voted back, which he says only made him work harder until the audience "can't deny you" [rick-and-morty-writer-ryan-ridley @ 10:21](https://youtu.be/RAOadSUNfIk?t=621)
- He warns it's "a curse and a blessing" when your employers are also your friends, since Rick and Morty's team was built from a pre-existing peer group rather than a hired staff [rick-and-morty-writer-ryan-ridley @ 10:57](https://youtu.be/RAOadSUNfIk?t=657)
- Writers avoid direct spoofs and instead try to hide references, aiming to capture the "essence" of a genre (e.g. the survivalist subgenre for the pickle episode) rather than parody it directly [rick-and-morty-writer-ryan-ridley @ 22:32](https://youtu.be/RAOadSUNfIk?t=1352)
- Creative disagreements (like whether the Meeks should be tiny or life-sized) get argued out extensively, then the team moves on once the episode ships regardless of which choice was "better" [rick-and-morty-writer-ryan-ridley @ 25:40](https://youtu.be/RAOadSUNfIk?t=1540)
- Because the show is animation, they will rewrite scripts and redraw storyboards very late, which causes real production pain compared to shows with bigger budgets [rick-and-morty-writer-ryan-ridley @ 28:19](https://youtu.be/RAOadSUNfIk?t=1699)
- Ridley's core advice to aspiring writers is to make the stuff you personally want to watch, for an audience of one, and hope it resonates with millions [rick-and-morty-writer-ryan-ridley @ 45:39](https://youtu.be/RAOadSUNfIk?t=2739)
- Season one was made with no expectation of success — the team was "just making ourselves laugh" as a modest Adult Swim show, and were shocked by its outsized cultural impact [rick-and-morty-writer-ryan-ridley @ 47:06](https://youtu.be/RAOadSUNfIk?t=2826)
- Before Rick and Morty, Ridley and collaborators made a podcast and web series for free, for years, with no pay, purely because they wanted to create [rick-and-morty-writer-ryan-ridley @ 47:24](https://youtu.be/RAOadSUNfIk?t=2844)
