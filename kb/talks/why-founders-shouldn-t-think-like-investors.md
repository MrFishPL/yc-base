# Why Founders Shouldn't Think Like Investors — Compiled Truth

Dalton and Michael argue that founders who adopt VC/banker-style thinking (market sizing, comps, fundraising trends) to pick and validate ideas instead of talking to customers consistently sabotage themselves.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- VC-style thinking is a framework built for analyzing big companies using market sizing, comps, and fundraising trends, and it doesn't transfer to picking a first startup idea [why-founders-shouldn-t-think-like-investors @ 00:48](https://youtu.be/SMi3JO-hsyY?t=48)
- YC interviews often surface founders with polished market-opportunity slides but zero customers, when what actually matters at the earliest stage is just whether the idea makes sense, whether the founders understand it, and whether they can get customers [why-founders-shouldn-t-think-like-investors @ 03:21](https://youtu.be/SMi3JO-hsyY?t=201)
- Market analysis and comps are only useful when applied to companies close to an outcome (like an IPO); applying them to a startup that's a decade from an exit is flawed analysis [why-founders-shouldn-t-think-like-investors @ 04:07](https://youtu.be/SMi3JO-hsyY?t=247)
- The rise of investor content marketing and startup classes taught by non-founder investors has spread VC-style pitching and market-analysis habits to founders who should instead be learning how to build [why-founders-shouldn-t-think-like-investors @ 05:31](https://youtu.be/SMi3JO-hsyY?t=331)
- Great later-stage investors succeed by finding companies that already have product-market fit and traction and attaching to them, not by predicting winners in advance, so there is little founders can actually learn from investors about picking an idea [why-founders-shouldn-t-think-like-investors @ 07:16](https://youtu.be/SMi3JO-hsyY?t=436)
- Founders should focus on problems in areas of personal strength and direct observation rather than reasoning from a VC toolbox to judge which idea is "good" [why-founders-shouldn-t-think-like-investors @ 09:40](https://youtu.be/SMi3JO-hsyY?t=580)
- The "zero to one" trap is founders glossing over how hard getting the first customer is because they're already mentally planning for scale, which is why launching quickly to get real feedback is advised [why-founders-shouldn-t-think-like-investors @ 11:59](https://youtu.be/SMi3JO-hsyY?t=719)
- Deep immersion with actual users (e.g., an AI-for-trucking founder who owned a used car lot) reveals nuanced, non-obvious opportunities that market analysis from an apartment never surfaces [why-founders-shouldn-t-think-like-investors @ 19:03](https://youtu.be/SMi3JO-hsyY?t=1143)
- Founders whose idea-filter differs from the consensus VC filter gain a "superpower" because they see opportunities everyone else screens out as bad ideas [why-founders-shouldn-t-think-like-investors @ 20:21](https://youtu.be/SMi3JO-hsyY?t=1221)
- Worrying about an eventual exit strategy or revenue ceiling before you even have a first customer is premature; it's rare for a founder who reaches $20-40M in revenue to run out of ideas for growth [why-founders-shouldn-t-think-like-investors @ 22:06](https://youtu.be/SMi3JO-hsyY?t=1326)
