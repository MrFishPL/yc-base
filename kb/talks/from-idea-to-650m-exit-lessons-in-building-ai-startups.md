# From Idea to $650M Exit: Lessons in Building AI Startups — Compiled Truth

Casetext founder Jake Heller explains how his team picked an AI idea, built reliable AI products through evaluations, and marketed/sold an AI product on the way to a $650M acquisition by Thomson Reuters.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Choosing an AI idea is easier now because you can look at what people are already paying other people to do — that spend is your addressable market, whether the AI assists, replaces, or does previously unthinkable work [from-idea-to-650m-exit-lessons-in-building-ai-startups @ 03:48](https://youtu.be/l0h3nAW13ao?t=228)
- The addressable market for AI products is the combined salaries of the people currently doing the job, not just seat-based SaaS pricing — often 10x-1000x larger than the old TAM [from-idea-to-650m-exit-lessons-in-building-ai-startups @ 06:03](https://youtu.be/l0h3nAW13ao?t=363)
- To build reliable AI, break down how the best human professional would do the task step by step, then turn each deterministic step into code and each judgment step into a prompt [from-idea-to-650m-exit-lessons-in-building-ai-startups @ 09:30](https://youtu.be/l0h3nAW13ao?t=570)
- Evaluations are the part most founders skip: define what "good" looks like from domain expertise, make evals objectively gradable, and grind on a prompt for two weeks to go from 60% to ~97% pass rate [from-idea-to-650m-exit-lessons-in-building-ai-startups @ 15:30](https://youtu.be/l0h3nAW13ao?t=930)
- Real customer failures in beta become the best new eval cases — customers use products in unpredictable ways that lab-created tests never anticipate [from-idea-to-650m-exit-lessons-in-building-ai-startups @ 21:18](https://youtu.be/l0h3nAW13ao?t=1278)
- Product quality matters far more than sales and marketing sophistication — once Casetext had a genuinely great product, word of mouth and inbound press became free marketing and existing salespeople became mere "order takers" [from-idea-to-650m-exit-lessons-in-building-ai-startups @ 24:25](https://youtu.be/l0h3nAW13ao?t=1465)
- Price AI products according to the value delivered rather than legacy SaaS norms — Casetext charged $500 per contract review versus the $1,000 a law firm would charge, but still a huge step up from $20/month SaaS pricing [from-idea-to-650m-exit-lessons-in-building-ai-startups @ 25:37](https://youtu.be/l0h3nAW13ao?t=1537)
- Enterprises are wary of new AI products, so building trust through head-to-head comparisons, pilots, and studies matters, and the sale isn't done at signing — a lot of "ARR" is really unconverted pilot revenue that risks a "mass extinction event" [from-idea-to-650m-exit-lessons-in-building-ai-startups @ 27:45](https://youtu.be/l0h3nAW13ao?t=1665)
- Competitors shouldn't determine market choice — most knowledge-work markets are so large that no single company will win them, and building the product itself reveals surprising defensibility others can't easily replicate [from-idea-to-650m-exit-lessons-in-building-ai-startups @ 30:45](https://youtu.be/l0h3nAW13ao?t=1845)
- Across seed to exit, the highest-leverage focus at every stage should have been building a product that achieves product-market fit — other functions like HR, marketing, and fundraising should serve that end rather than become ends in themselves [from-idea-to-650m-exit-lessons-in-building-ai-startups @ 32:59](https://youtu.be/l0h3nAW13ao?t=1979)
