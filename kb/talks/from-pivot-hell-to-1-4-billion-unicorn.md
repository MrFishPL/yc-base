# From Pivot Hell To $1.4 Billion Unicorn — Compiled Truth

PostHog founder James Hawkins recounts how the company burned through many failed ideas before finding self-hosted, open-source product analytics, and how relentless full-effort execution, radical transparency, and remarkable (often absurd) marketing turned that idea into a $1.4B company.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- PostHog tried several pivots — including a sales territory management tool and a developer technical-debt survey tool — before landing on self-hosted, open-source product analytics [from-pivot-hell-to-1-4-billion-unicorn @ 02:17](https://youtu.be/5WN8bfG06Hk?t=137)
- Building for sales leaders gave poor signal because they're too agreeable and positive on calls compared to engineers, who are closer to "what they say is close to what they'll do" [from-pivot-hell-to-1-4-billion-unicorn @ 06:07](https://youtu.be/5WN8bfG06Hk?t=367)
- Charging for each pivot idea early was the real test — if people you have a personal relationship with still won't pay, that's a strong signal the product isn't solving a real problem [from-pivot-hell-to-1-4-billion-unicorn @ 08:11](https://youtu.be/5WN8bfG06Hk?t=491)
- The Hacker News launch of open-source product analytics instantly flipped the company from pushing for attention to being pulled by inbound demand [from-pivot-hell-to-1-4-billion-unicorn @ 11:14](https://youtu.be/5WN8bfG06Hk?t=674)
- PostHog's seed round was brutally hard (roughly 160 investors contacted, and COVID hit mid-raise), but committing to one focused open-source community strategy rather than a scattered plan eventually won investors over [from-pivot-hell-to-1-4-billion-unicorn @ 14:53](https://youtu.be/5WN8bfG06Hk?t=893)
- The recent $75M raise was taken preemptively, driven by growing conviction that AI would be hugely important and would let PostHog make much bigger product bets across its whole suite [from-pivot-hell-to-1-4-billion-unicorn @ 17:37](https://youtu.be/5WN8bfG06Hk?t=1057)
- PostHog's marketing philosophy is to reject the "80/20 polish" most companies settle for and instead push far past normal effort (billboards, an unusually deep website) specifically to be remarkable and get talked about [from-pivot-hell-to-1-4-billion-unicorn @ 33:33](https://youtu.be/5WN8bfG06Hk?t=2013)
- Radical transparency — showing team member photos and bios, publishing a public handbook, and writing "building in public" posts — builds trust with a skeptical developer audience [from-pivot-hell-to-1-4-billion-unicorn @ 23:10](https://youtu.be/5WN8bfG06Hk?t=1390)
