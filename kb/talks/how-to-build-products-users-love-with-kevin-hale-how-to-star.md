# How to Build Products Users Love with Kevin Hale (How to Start a Startup 2014: Lecture 7) — Compiled Truth

Kevin Hale (Wufoo) argues that building products users love means treating early user relationships like dating and marriage — obsessing over first impressions, closing the feedback loop between builders and support, and constantly reinvesting effort to fight the natural decay of a relationship.

**Key ideas (with timestamps):**
- Growth is the interaction between conversion rate and churn, and a 1% gain in conversion has the same effect as a 1% reduction in churn — but reducing churn is usually cheaper and easier, and gets neglected [how-to-build-products-users-love-with-kevin-hale-how-to-star @ 00:40](https://youtu.be/12D8zEdOPYo?t=40)
- First impressions matter disproportionately because early-relationship mistakes end things, while long-tenured users forgive much more — so founders should hunt for every "first moment" (first email, first login, first error page, first support reply) and make it memorable [how-to-build-products-users-love-with-kevin-hale-how-to-star @ 05:10](https://youtu.be/12D8zEdOPYo?t=310)
- Products should aim for both atarimae hinshitsu (taken-for-granted functional quality) and miryokuteki hinshitsu (enchanting quality), but the witty/delightful touches only work once the basic functionality is solid [how-to-build-products-users-love-with-kevin-hale-how-to-star @ 06:43](https://youtu.be/12D8zEdOPYo?t=403)
- Wufoo's practice of "support-driven development" — having every engineer and designer do customer support — closes the feedback loop between the people who build software and the people who suffer its consequences [how-to-build-products-users-love-with-kevin-hale-how-to-star @ 16:48](https://youtu.be/12D8zEdOPYo?t=1008)
- Direct, real-time exposure to users (a minimum of six weeks, at least two hours) measurably improves software quality, per Jared Spool's research; Wufoo's team got four to eight hours of user exposure every week [how-to-build-products-users-love-with-kevin-hale-how-to-star @ 22:56](https://youtu.be/12D8zEdOPYo?t=1376)
- You can close the "knowledge gap" between what a user knows and what your app requires either by educating the user or by simplifying the product — Wufoo spent 30% of engineering time on self-service tools, and one documentation redesign cut support volume 30% overnight [how-to-build-products-users-love-with-kevin-hale-how-to-star @ 23:55](https://youtu.be/12D8zEdOPYo?t=1435)
- Relationships decay like a closed thermodynamic system unless you keep investing energy, so Wufoo built a "since you've been gone" alert to resurface everything shipped since a user's last login, and had the team handwrite thank-you cards to users every week [how-to-build-products-users-love-with-kevin-hale-how-to-star @ 27:11](https://youtu.be/12D8zEdOPYo?t=1631)
- Per Treacy and Wiersema's "Discipline of Market Leaders," there are three paths to market dominance — best price, best product, best overall (customer-intimate) solution — and the customer-intimate path is the only one any company can pursue at any stage with little capital, mostly humility [how-to-build-products-users-love-with-kevin-hale-how-to-star @ 31:24](https://youtu.be/12D8zEdOPYo?t=1884)
- When facing conflicting feature requests from different user segments, focus completely on the most passionate niche first rather than trying to please everyone, and ship the smallest version of each idea within a week or two to test it [how-to-build-products-users-love-with-kevin-hale-how-to-star @ 33:12](https://youtu.be/12D8zEdOPYo?t=1992)
- Wufoo ran remote with a four-and-a-half day work week, a 15-minute rule to prevent meetings from derailing focus time, and simple shared to-do-list text files instead of process tools, which made performance and accountability visible to the whole team [how-to-build-products-users-love-with-kevin-hale-how-to-star @ 39:18](https://youtu.be/12D8zEdOPYo?t=2358)
